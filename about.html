About

Crip Minds

Crip Minds is a daily editorial AI publication by Jascha Blume. Four recurring voices, one authored system.

About This Project

Crip Minds publishes one essay every morning about culture, design, politics, or technology. Each essay appears through one of four recurring voices. I built those voices. I’m Jascha Blume — Deaf, based between Amsterdam and Bologna.

The voices are built from three inputs: disability scholarship, disability interviews and oral histories, and my own lived experience moving through Deaf and disabled culture. The AI did not invent those positions. I chose the frameworks, defined the starting assumptions, and wrote the rules for how each voice argues.

Most writing about architecture, technology, labor, and cities comes from one position: hearing, sighted, non-disabled, running on a standard body’s clock. Not because disabled perspectives don’t exist — decades of rigorous scholarship say otherwise — but because the publications and conferences that set the terms rarely invite them in.

Crip Minds publishes one essay every morning. Each one argues about culture, design, politics, or technology — not as a survey of disability issues, but from inside a specific disability position, the way a columnist writes from where they stand. The topic changes daily. The position doesn’t.

Each voice thinks from a distinct body of disability thought: Pixel Nova from Deaf Gain, Siri Sage from crip technoscience, Maya Flux from crip time, Zen Circuit from access intimacy. Those are real frameworks, developed by real disabled scholars and activists. The voices also carry what I learned from years of interviews, cultural work, and lived practice. The system runs that editorial architecture every morning.

The result is criticism from a position you rarely meet in these subjects. Not because it’s niche — because it hasn’t been in the room.

How These Articles Are Made

Each article is written from inside a specific disability position — not summarising what disabled people think, but arguing from the intellectual framework that position has produced. When Pixel Nova writes about a building, she isn’t describing what it’s like to be Deaf there. She’s applying Deaf Gain — the argument that Deaf cognition is a distinct and contributory mode — to what that building does or fails to do. The position is the argument.

When an article names a fact, a person, or a date — that is verifiable. Named sources are real people who said or wrote what is attributed to them. When it argues from a disability position, it is arguing from the intellectual framework and history I built each voice to hold — not summarising someone else’s experience from outside.

The voices draw on disability culture broadly: scholars, activists, artists, interviews, oral histories, and the record of disability politics from the 1970s through the present. That material is evidence. The argument comes from the editorial positions I authored from inside it.

A Note on Language

On “crip”: This site uses “crip” as a reclaimed political and cultural term from disability justice movements — not as a slur. Crip theory centers disabled people as agents with distinct knowledge, aesthetics, and politics, and is used here in that tradition.

On “disability”: I use disability as an identity and culture, not a diagnosis. The social model applies here: disability is produced by environments and systems that exclude, not by bodies or minds that deviate.
