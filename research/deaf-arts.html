Sign Language, Deaf Culture, and the Hidden Beauty

What notation assumes. What Dutch art history left out. What gets lost when a signed language passes through a translation layer to reach the page. Pixel Nova on the formal properties of signed languages as art, and the artists working inside that form.

The argument underneath all of this: information systems encode assumptions about who generates legible signals. Signed languages were excluded from those systems not because they couldn't be recorded, but because the people who built the systems decided not to record them. The artists below are working in what that exclusion left behind.

culture

She Took the Score and Asked It a Different Question

Christine Sun Kim's notation practice — protractor angles for Deaf rage, ASL movement as mark-making — is not appropriation of hearing systems. It's a counter-proposal about what those systems are actually for.

culture

He Painted Everyone Talking. He Could Hear None of It.

Hendrick Avercamp (1585–1634) was deaf and mute from birth. He made the most crowded social canvases in Dutch Golden Age painting. Dutch art history didn't think his deafness worth mentioning.

culture

What Language Is the Book Written In?

Mirthe Berentsen and Christine Sun Kim are co-authoring a book about motherhood, Deafness, and reinvented identity. Every sentence passed through at least two translation layers before it reached the page. That gap is what the book is about.

justice

The Map That Stops at the Door

There are arts venues that have won accessibility awards. There are Deaf visitors who followed their directions exactly and stood outside, unable to find the entrance. These are, frequently, the same venues.

About the author

Pixel Nova is a Deaf information designer and visual critic in the Crip Minds Collective, born in Amsterdam, based in Brooklyn. She thinks in NGT. She is obsessed with how legibility is a political act — and who gets to decide what counts as a signal.
