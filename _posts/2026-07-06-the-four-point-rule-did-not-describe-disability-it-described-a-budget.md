The Personal Independence Payment assesses disability through ten daily living activities: preparing food, eating and drinking, managing treatments, washing, managing toilet needs, dressing, communicating verbally, reading signs, engaging with other people, and making decisions about money. Each activity has descriptors. Each descriptor is worth points. Four to seven points qualifies for the standard daily living rate. Eight or more qualifies for the enhanced rate.

The key feature of this system is that points accumulate across activities. You can qualify without any single activity being severely impaired — because disability often works that way. Two points for difficulty preparing food. Two for bathing. Two for dressing. Six total, standard rate. A life that requires daily support, distributed across the ordinary tasks of a day.

The government's proposed four-point rule added a condition. To qualify for the daily living component, you would also need to score at least four points from a single activity. Accumulated difficulty would no longer be sufficient. Distributed impairment — the kind that is present everywhere and concentrated nowhere — would not count.

I have a T6 spinal cord injury. I know people who qualify under the current rules precisely because their difficulty is distributed — not one severe malfunction, but multiple moderate ones that together make independent daily life impossible. The four-point rule would not have seen them. The instrument was not redesigned to describe a different kind of disability. It was redesigned to see fewer of them.

The rule was removed from the Welfare Reform Bill for existing claimants on 1 July 2025, following political pressure. It still applies to new claimants from November 2026. The government estimates that by 2029/30, around 800,000 people will not receive the daily living component who would have under current rules — 370,000 existing recipients losing entitlement at review, and 430,000 future claimants not qualifying. These are not people who will have become less disabled. They are people whose disability will no longer be visible to the instrument.

The Timms review, due to report in Autumn 2026, will assess PIP activities and descriptors and may redesign the instrument. What it cannot redesign, unless explicitly asked, is the logic behind the four-point rule: that disability must concentrate in one place to count, that cumulative difficulty does not qualify, that distributed impairment is not real impairment. That logic will not appear in the review's terms of reference. [It rarely does.](/2026/05/22/court-ruling-no-effect/) The state has many mechanisms for recording decisions it cannot see as decisions. The assessment form is one of them.
