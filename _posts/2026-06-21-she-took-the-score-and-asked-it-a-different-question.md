The piece is called *Degrees of Deaf Rage*.

It's a series of drawings. Each one uses the notation from a protractor — the arc, the degree markings, the clean geometry of measured angles — to calibrate anger. Ten degrees. Forty-five. A hundred and twenty. The visual language says: this is a measurement system. The content says: the thing being measured is what hearing culture does to Deaf people, catalogued and quantified in the same notation used for geometry homework.

The first time I stood in front of these drawings, I thought: she's not making art about notation. She's asking notation a question it was never meant to answer.

## What Notation Does

Notation systems encode assumptions.

This is obvious when you look at writing systems — alphabets embed phoneme-centrism, assume that the fundamental unit of language is a sound, which is why the writing reform that would have allowed Deaf people full literacy in the nineteenth century was a reform that never happened. But notation is usually treated as neutral. Musical staff notation is just a way of writing music down. Mathematical notation is just shorthand. The angle-measurement system is just a tool.

Christine Sun Kim's practice reveals the assumption underneath the tool.

Musical staff notation encodes one relationship between sound and time. It assumes pitch on a vertical axis, duration on a horizontal one, and tempo as the speed at which you move through the horizontal. It is a system designed by and for people who hear — designed so precisely for them that it cannot, as written, accommodate what a Deaf body experiences when sound moves through a room.

What she does with it is not parody and it's not subversion. It's more exact than that. She takes the formal properties of the notation — the grid, the arc, the graduated scale — and asks them to carry new content. *Degrees of Deaf Rage* uses angle notation to say: the thing I am measuring is real, it has magnitude, the magnitude varies by situation, here are the calibrations. The form says scientific instrument. The content says: you weren't measuring me, so I'll do it myself.

## The Body as the Instrument

*Time Owes Me Rest Again* (2022) is a drawing made from ASL movement.

I mean this literally: the marks on the paper are generated by the movement of the hands in signed language. The signing body becomes a mark-making instrument. The drawing records what the language looks like when it makes contact with a surface.

I think about this in relation to musical notation constantly.

Standard notation records what a sound looks like when it's translated into a set of agreed symbols — it's two steps removed from the sound itself. Kim's ASL-sourced drawings are one step removed from the language: the signing body touches the paper, and what's left is the trace of the language in motion.

This matters because the history of signed language suppression is, among other things, a history of transcription. What cannot be written down cannot be taught in schools. What cannot be notated cannot be preserved. The oralism campaigns of the nineteenth and twentieth centuries didn't only ban signing in classrooms — they ensured that signing left no record, no score, no archival trace that could be handed down. You cannot notate what you refuse to recognize as language.

Kim's practice is not a solution to that. But it's a demonstration: a Deaf body can generate marks. The marks carry meaning. The medium was always waiting.

## The Counter-Proposal

Here is what I find most precise about Kim's work: she doesn't refuse hearing notation systems. She doesn't make counter-notation or Deaf-only notation. She works in the systems that already exist — the protractor, the infographic, the pie chart, the musical staff — and asks them to carry content they weren't designed to hold.

*Shit Hearing People Say to Me* is a series of infographic pie charts. The form comes directly from data visualization — clean, authoritative, designed to make information legible at a glance. The content is the assumptions hearing people make about Deaf people, distributed across segments. The form says: here is a neutral representation of data. The content says: this data has been collected from my life, from my body, from decades of encounters with a culture that keeps saying the same things.

The formal tension is the argument. The infographic was designed to make information available to anyone who can see it. She's using it to say: look at what you keep doing, here it is in the notation you trust.

This is not the same as reclaiming a hearing system. It's a counter-proposal about what the system is actually for. The protractor measures angles. The pie chart distributes percentages. Neither of these things belongs to hearing culture — they're tools. She's using them to measure what hearing culture keeps doing. The tools don't resist this. They were always capable of it.

## What I Notice in Information Design

When I design information systems, the first question I ask is: what does this system assume about who generates legible signals?

Every system has an answer. Most systems don't know they're answering.

Musical notation assumes pitched sound produced at a pace that can be captured in standard note values. The alphabet assumes a body that can hear phonemes. The "Getting Here" page assumes a visitor who navigates through hearing — who can ask directions, hear announcements, orient by audio cues.

Christine Sun Kim's work makes the assumption visible and asks it to step aside.

Not permanently. Not by destroying the notation. By using it to record what it was designed to exclude, and showing that it was always capable of that, and that the exclusion was not a technical limitation. It was a choice. A choice about who was worth measuring.
