I stood on a GO Transit platform in Mississauga in November 2022, and what I heard was a building pretending I wasn't there. The electronic departure board flickered — I know this because a stranger told me, unprompted, as if narrating my disability for me. The PA system announced a track change. It bounced off the concrete canopy, hit the glass windbreak, and returned to me as a smear of vowels. I caught "track" and "six" and nothing else. I asked the stranger. She had already walked away.

Transit systems are acoustic environments before they are anything else. This is not a metaphor. For me, the sound of a station is its architecture. The hiss of train brakes tells me distance and direction. The density of footsteps tells me crowd size. The reverb profile of a platform — how quickly sound decays — tells me ceiling height, wall material, whether I'm enclosed or exposed. I read space through echo the way sighted travelers read it through signage. When a system is designed well, it speaks. When it isn't, it mutters.

[Tangled Art + Disability](https://tangledart.org/), the Toronto-based organization, has been documenting what happens when disabled people try to use GO Transit through their ["Getting Here on the GO" project](https://tangledart.org/getting-here-on-the-go/). What they found is not surprising to anyone who has stood on one of those platforms with a white cane. The system was not built to be heard. It was built to be seen.

You might say: but there are announcements. Audio cues. Accessibility features. Sure. And a ramp bolted to the side of a staircase is also, technically, access. The question is not whether the feature exists. The question is whether the environment was conceived with the listening body in mind from the first sketch, or whether sound was added later like subtitles on a film that was never meant to be read.

**Two stations, one system**

Here is a comparison nobody asked me to make, but I'm making it. Union Station in Toronto, post-renovation, completed around 2020. And Burlington GO, a station I used regularly in 2019. Same transit authority. Same era. Radically different acoustic realities.

Union Station's great hall has a barrel-vaulted ceiling made of Guastavino tile. It was designed in 1927. The tile absorbs mid-range frequencies and diffuses the rest. When I walked through it in January 2023, I could hear the ticket gates sixty feet ahead — a rhythmic click-and-release — and orient from that alone. Footsteps separated into individual events. I could hear the difference between someone rushing and someone browsing. The space told me its shape. It did this not because someone in 1927 was thinking about blind travelers. It did this because the materials were honest. Tile and stone have acoustic integrity. They don't lie about their distances.

Burlington GO is a different animal. The shelters are made of glass and steel with perforated metal ceilings. Sound doesn't decay in those shelters — it ricochets. Standing inside one, I heard my own cane tap return to me three times, each return slightly offset, turning a single tap into a stutter. The PA system played through speakers mounted on poles outside the shelter. So if you stepped inside to escape the wind, you stepped away from the information. The message was clear, though nobody intended it: comfort or knowledge. Pick one.

The absurd part is that Burlington was the newer construction. It had been "accessibility reviewed." It met code.

Building codes measure access in inches and degrees. Ramp slope: 1:12 maximum. Tactile warning strips: 24 inches deep. Signage contrast ratio. These are real, they matter, I am not dismissing them. But acoustic design for disabled users exists almost nowhere in building code. [The federal Public Right-of-Way Accessibility Guidelines](https://www.access-board.gov/prowag/) address detectable warnings, cross-slopes, pedestrian signals. They say nothing about reverberation time. Nothing about speaker placement relative to shelter enclosures. Nothing about the frequency range of announcement systems versus the frequency range of ambient train noise.

[Georgina Kleege](https://english.berkeley.edu/users/45) wrote in [*More Than Meets the Eye*](https://www.beacon.org/More-Than-Meets-the-Eye-P471.aspx) that blind people are often forced to become experts on visual culture because we live inside it without being consulted about it. I'd extend that. I have become an involuntary expert on acoustic failures in public architecture. I know which materials swallow speech and which ones shatter it. I know that glass is a liar — it reflects high frequencies and lets low ones pass through, so a voice heard through glass sounds like it's been stripped of consonants, the very sounds that carry meaning. I know this the way a wheelchair user knows which curb cuts are too steep by the burn in their shoulders. The body keeps the data.

[R. Murray Schafer](https://www.sfu.ca/~truax/schafer.html), the composer who essentially invented soundscape ecology, made a distinction in the 1970s between "hi-fi" and "lo-fi" soundscapes. A hi-fi environment is one where individual sounds are distinct. You can hear far. Signal separates from noise. A lo-fi environment is a wash — everything bleeds together. Urban transit stations are almost universally lo-fi. Schafer was not thinking about disability when he made this distinction. He was mourning the loss of rural quiet. But his framework reveals something he didn't name: a lo-fi soundscape is a disabling soundscape. It doesn't just obscure birdsong. It obscures the information a blind person needs to not end up on the wrong train to Hamilton.

**The listening body as knowledge**

[Pauline Oliveros](https://www.paulineoliveros.us/) spent decades developing what she called Deep Listening — a practice of attending to the full field of sound, including sounds you'd normally filter out. She did this work as a musician and eventually as a person using a wheelchair. Her body changed. Her listening didn't diminish; it redirected. In a 2010 interview, she described the wheelchair as having altered her "listening position" — lower to the ground, differently resonant, attuned to floor vibrations that standing bodies miss.

I think about this constantly. My listening position is not a compensatory mechanism. It is an epistemic one. I don't hear "better" than sighted people. I hear differently. I hear architecturally. The first thing I know about a room is its size, because I hear the delay between my footstep and its return. The second thing I know is its material, because hard surfaces ring and soft surfaces swallow. The third thing I know — and this is the one that has no equivalent in visual perception — is whether the room is occupied. A room with forty people in it sounds fundamentally different from an empty room of the same dimensions, because bodies absorb sound. I can feel a crowd before I reach it.

This is not echolocation repackaged as a superpower. I am not Daredevil. I'm a blind person who pays attention. The skill is ordinary. What's extraordinary is that an entire transit infrastructure can be built, reviewed, certified, and opened without anyone asking what the station sounds like to someone who depends on sound to survive it.

[Carmen Papalia](https://carmenpapalia.com/), the Vancouver-based artist who works on what he calls [non-visual access](https://carmenpapalia.com/), has a phrase I keep returning to: "accessibility is not an aftermarket product." He said this during a talk at the Vancouver Art Gallery in March 2018, describing his frustration with institutions that invite disabled artists to exhibit and then scramble to build a ramp the week before opening. The same logic applies to sound. You cannot design a transit station in silence — on a screen, in a visual rendering — and then "add" acoustic accessibility. The sound is the space. If you didn't design the sound, you didn't design the space.

Back to that platform in Mississauga. The woman who told me about the departure board didn't do anything wrong. She saw a blind person and offered information. That's fine. But here is what interests me about the interaction: she assumed I needed visual information translated into speech. She assumed the gap was between my eyes and the board. The actual gap was between my ears and the PA system. I didn't need a narrator. I needed a speaker positioned inside the shelter instead of outside it. I needed an announcement system calibrated to cut through the 85-hertz rumble of an idling diesel train. I needed the station to have been designed by someone who had stood where I was standing and listened.

[Mia Mingus](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/), the disability justice writer, has described access as an act of love — "not just logistics, but longing." She wrote this in 2012 on her blog [*Leaving Evidence*](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/2012/05/08/feeling-the-weight-some-beginning-notes-on-disability-access-and-love/), and I've carried the phrase since. [She meant it as a challenge](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/2012/05/08/feeling-the-weight-some-beginning-notes-on-disability-access-and-love/): if your access is grudging, it shows. If it was not imagined with desire — with the genuine wish for someone to arrive and be well — the architecture will betray that indifference. You'll hear it. I mean that literally.

**The cheap aluminum truth**

I own nine sets of wind chimes. They are acoustically useless. They tell me nothing about the space they hang in. They have no informational value. What they have is presence — a sound that exists only because the air moved, and the air moved only because the world is alive and unsettled. I listen to them the way I imagine sighted people watch clouds. Not for data. For company.

Every weekday at 5:45 AM I stand in the doorway of the community center pool and listen to the water hear itself back off the tile walls before anyone arrives to break the surface.
