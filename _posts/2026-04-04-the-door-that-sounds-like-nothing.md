In September 2021, a transit hub in Rotterdam installed automatic sliding doors that were, by every measure, accessible. Flat threshold. Wide clearance. Tactile guide strips leading to the entrance. Accessibility advocates would call this a win. I walked through those doors in November of the same year and stopped three metres past them because I had no idea I was inside.

The doors made no sound. The floor material didn't change. The air moved the same way on both sides. I stood in what I later learned was a vast atrium, but at the moment of crossing I had nothing. No acoustic shift. No temperature change. No signal that the building had begun. A sighted commuter would have seen the ceiling soar. I got silence pretending to be welcome.

Here is where I want to push back on a common argument. Some accessibility advocates make the case that physical access is the fight — the ramp, the entrance, the measurable threshold. They are right that a step blocks a wheelchair absolutely in a way that bad acoustics do not block me absolutely. I concede this. A step is binary. Sound is gradient. But the conclusion often drawn — that access features that are visually obvious and can be documented in photos form the foundation everything else gets built on — misses what happens to bodies that arrive through sound. My access was not built on top of the ramp. It was never in the blueprint at all.

---

[Georgina Kleege](https://english.berkeley.edu/users/45) writes that blind people are experts in visual culture because sighted culture forces us to study it in self-defence. The same inversion applies to [transit](https://urbanomnibus.net/2014/02/getting-you-there-public-transit-as-an-instrument-of-freedom/). I know more about how stations communicate than most architects, because stations were not designed to communicate with me.

A good threshold announces itself. The old Rotterdam Centraal — the main train station, demolished in 2007 — had a stone portal that changed the reverb as you entered. Your footsteps shortened. The air compressed. Your body knew. The new one is glass and open plan. Gorgeous, apparently. To me it is a single unbroken field of white noise from platform to street.

An acoustic designer named [Trevor Cox](https://www.salford.ac.uk/our-staff/trevor-cox) at the University of Salford measured reverberation times — how long sound echoes and bounces around in a space — in modern transit halls in 2014 and found they had converged. Glass, steel, open plan. Every major station built after 2000 sounds nearly identical. He called them spaces without distinct acoustic characteristics. Not hostile. Absent.

The inspection checklist for that Rotterdam hub runs to forty-three pages. Door width: pass. Tactile strips: pass. Contrast marking: pass. Nowhere on the form is there a field for what the space sounds like when you cross into it. Nobody failed. The door opened. I walked through. The building never told me it had started.

Those doors are still open, still silent, still passing every audit they were built to pass.
