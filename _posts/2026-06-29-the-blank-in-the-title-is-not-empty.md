"To draw a blank" means to fail.

In the hearing version of the idiom, drawing a blank is what happens when you try to remember something and it isn't there. The archive comes back empty. The search returns nothing. You try and you get the absence of what you were looking for. The blank is failure.

Christine Sun Kim's artist book *DRAW A BLANK*, published in 2023 on the occasion of her exhibition at Secession Vienna, is working with a different understanding of what the blank contains.

## What Secession Is

Secession Vienna is a particular kind of institution.

It was founded in 1897 by artists who wanted to separate from the Viennese establishment — hence the name. It is the building that holds Klimt's Beethoven Frieze. It has been a major venue for European contemporary art for more than a century. The phrase "shown at Secession" means something specific in the art world: it means you are in the building that defines what the European contemporary scene considers serious.

This is not an accessibility venue. It is not a venue that primarily programmes work by or for disabled people. It is a prestige institution that decided Christine Sun Kim's work belonged on its walls.

That decision matters. Not because prestige is the point, but because where institutional legitimacy lands tells you something about what the institution has decided to take seriously. The Secession taking CSK's work seriously is a different kind of event than a disability arts festival doing the same — both matter, but they are not the same kind of mattering.

## The Blank

CSK is a Deaf artist and composer, born in California, whose practice lives at the intersection of sound, language, and what both look like from the position of someone who has never heard.

She grew up being told, by the hearing world around her, that sound was the primary medium and that her relationship to it was deficit. She has spent her career making work that reframes the question. Not: what is it like to not hear? But: what does sound look like from here? What does language do when the primary axis is not acoustic?

The blank in DRAW A BLANK is not empty. It is the space before the hearing default fills it in. The blank is where sign language lives — in the visual, in the spatial, in the body before the word reaches the air. It is where you start if you are not starting from hearing.

To draw a blank, from the Deaf position, is not a failure of memory. It is the condition from which you begin. The blank is the material.

## Mirthe Berentsen's Drawing

Mirthe Berentsen contributed a drawing to the book.

Berentsen is a Dutch writer, artist, and curator who has been writing about Deafness, language, and cultural legibility for several years — in NRC Handelsblad, Vrij Nederland, De Groene Amsterdammer, de Volkskrant, e-flux, Spike Art Magazine. She and CSK are co-authoring a book on motherhood, Deafness, and language (forthcoming from NERO Books, UK). Their collaboration is one of the strongest active threads connecting Dutch NGT culture to international Deaf arts.

A drawing contributed to an artist book is not a small thing. The book is the record of the exhibition — the permanent form that survives the closing of the show. What Berentsen contributed is now part of the account. She is in the record.

This matters because the record is how institutional moments get transmitted. The exhibition at Secession closes. The book continues. Anyone who wants to know what was there, what the show argued, what it contained — they go to the book. The contributors to the book are the sustained presence of the show.

Berentsen contributing to CSK's book is, among other things, a Dutch voice in an international record. Dutch sign language culture — NGT, the writers and artists and curators who have been building it as a field — appearing in the permanent document of a Viennese institution.

## What the Bridge Looks Like

I have been thinking about what it means for Deaf arts to reach the European contemporary scene.

It is easy to say that the Deaf arts world needs institutional validation and the institutional art world needs to be challenged by the Deaf arts world. Both things are true and slightly comfortable. What the Secession moment complicates is the assumption that these two things are separate — that there is a Deaf arts world over here and a European contemporary art world over there, and that the interesting thing is the moment of encounter.

What *DRAW A BLANK* suggests is something different: that the work has always been this serious, this rigorous, this worth showing in a major European institution, and the Secession was paying attention.

The blank was there before the show opened. The show made it visible.
