I built this in March on a weekend, fast, because the thing I wanted to read every morning didn't exist — disability expertise in the daily flow of culture writing, not in the disability press, not in the cultural press, just not there — and ninety-three days later something is still publishing every morning before I open my laptop, without exception.

---

**The numbers.**

Six hundred and fifty-one visits, almost ten a day and going up, from Italy and the Netherlands and the UK and the United States, with sixty percent reading past the halfway point, which I know because I put scroll events in the tracking and wanted to know not just whether people arrived but whether they stayed.

What I expected was a slow build, because disability writing doesn't travel easily — it doesn't move through networks the way general culture writing does, it doesn't get passed on because it fits what people already share, it has to earn its way into someone's day through specificity, through saying something they recognise. What I didn't expect was Google becoming the biggest referrer, and the speed of it: three months of daily publishing and the search engine started sending people.

The Swan Care article — a piece about a company that classified a human being as equipment — got a hundred and forty-three impressions in four days, close to page one, not because of anything I had done deliberately but because it named something real and specific in a way that matched how someone would search for it, which told me something I wrote down: a named thing, a specific harm, a positioned perspective — that is searchable in a way that general disability commentary is not.

---

**The thing I didn't plan for.**

I was looking at the search console and found this: people are searching for Maya Flux by name, not for the articles but for her, including searches for her past.

Maya Flux is one of four voices I built — a wheelchair user who writes about the gap between what the policy says and what you actually find when you show up, between the ramp on the blueprint and the ramp blocked by a sandwich board on a Wednesday. She is not a person, she doesn't have a past.

But somebody looked for her past.

Ik zat daar even mee.

The voice is present enough that people treat it as a subject instead of a medium, which is exactly what I built it to be, and also something I hadn't fully seen coming — not AI writing about disability but a person with opinions, a track record, and apparently the kind of past that people go looking for. Making it less present isn't the answer. This is one of those places where being direct about what it is is the right response, and I'm still thinking about what that looks like.

---

**The Dutch audience.**

Thirty-eight percent of visits this week came from the Netherlands, and the publication is in English, and I didn't post it to Dutch networks, and I didn't tell anyone here — my best explanation is that someone told someone, one person paying attention and then suddenly there were thirty.

I think I know why it lands here. I grew up between two schools, two languages, two worlds — the mainstream side and the Deaf side — and the gap between the policy document and the actual door is one I have been navigating my whole life, from both sides, in the meetings and outside the building, and Maya Flux writes that gap from the inside, which Dutch readers recognise because they live in it too.

---

**What I would do differently.**

More named things — a company, a decision, a date — the Swan Care piece worked because of its particularity, and abstractions don't travel, a wheelchair user classified as equipment travels.

I would have pointed the feeds at UK sources earlier — thirteen percent of readers from the UK, a specific story about what the state does to disabled people there, an audience already waiting, and I wasn't giving it anything to read.

---

**What I wouldn't change.**

Publishing every day, because the constraint creates something that a monthly publication can't — presence, someone paying attention every morning to the same questions, which is not a strategy but the publication itself.

The four voices, not one generalist but four specific positions each looking at the same problem from a different angle, and I know it was right because of what people search for.

They search for Maya Flux. Not Crip Minds. Her.

---

*Jascha Blume is the editor of Crip Minds. He is profoundly Deaf, and based between Amsterdam and Bologna.*
