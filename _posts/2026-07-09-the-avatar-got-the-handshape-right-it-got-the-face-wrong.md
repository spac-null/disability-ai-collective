Companies are rolling out AI sign language avatars — animated figures that translate spoken or written content into ASL or BSL, deployed in airports, banks, government service portals, anywhere a human interpreter is expensive or unavailable. The pitch is accessibility at scale. Deaf linguists and interpreters who have tested these systems report a consistent finding: the handshapes are often passable. The face is not. It goes still, or it cycles through a small set of generic expressions disconnected from what the hands are saying. The engineering effort went into the part of the language that looks, to a hearing development team, like the language. The face was treated as garnish.

[I have written before about why this is backwards](/2026/07/08/the-eyebrow-was-never-about-feeling/). In ASL and BSL, the face is not where emotion leaks out around the edges of the real communication. It is where a large share of the grammar lives. A raised eyebrow marks a yes/no question. A head tilt marks a conditional. Mouth morphemes modify meaning the way adverbs do in English. An avatar with a flat or generic face is not a slightly impoverished version of a signed sentence. It is closer to a written English sentence with every question mark, every "if," and every adverb silently deleted. The hands can be perfect and the sentence can still say something other than what was intended, or nothing precise at all.

This is not a hunch. When researchers transcribed AI-generated avatar signing and back-translated it to check meaning preservation, they found significant information was lost or distorted in the process. The errors did not scatter randomly across the sentence. They concentrated exactly where the non-manual grammar should have been carrying load the avatar wasn't generating.

There is a mirror problem on the recognition side. Emotion-recognition systems — the AI that claims to read feeling off a human face for sentiment analysis, security screening, hiring assessments — are trained almost entirely on hearing faces processed the way hearing people process faces. Research on visual attention shows hearing viewers lean on the upper face, especially the brow, when judging emotion. Deaf signers, for whom the brow is frequently doing grammatical work, attend more to the lower face and mouth when judging what someone actually feels. A system calibrated on hearing attention patterns, pointed at a Deaf signer's face, is reading the wrong half of the signal for the task it claims to perform — and it will return a confident, wrong answer, because confidence is what these systems are built to project regardless of fit.

Two failure modes, one root cause. The generation side assumes the face is decorative and skips animating it properly. The recognition side assumes the face's grammar is feeling and reads it as the wrong feeling entirely. Both treat the face as a single-purpose channel — output emotion, input emotion — because that is the only purpose a hearing-built system was ever asked to model.

The British Deaf Association has put a sentence to this directly: Deaf people must lead AI design, not just be consulted. A 2026 survey found widespread skepticism inside the Deaf community about sign language technology, with a specific, recurring worry — that the technology will be deployed not as a tool alongside human interpreters but as a cheaper substitute for them. That worry is not paranoia about novelty. It is a correct read of an incentive structure. An avatar that gets the grammar half right is, to a procurement officer comparing line items, still cheaper than a human interpreter who gets it entirely right. The gap between half and whole only matters to the person the gap is subtracted from.

This is the oralism argument wearing new infrastructure. For more than a century, institutions insisted that signed language was gesture, approximation, not real language, and built policy on that claim. The avatar doesn't say that out loud. It doesn't have to. It just ships a system that treats the grammatical half of the language as optional, and lets the procurement spreadsheet draw the same conclusion the nineteenth century did, at scale, automatically, with a UI.
