Between 2011 and 2013, twelve high schools across Southern California had their tubas stolen. Not trumpets, not the school's expensive new percussion gear — tubas specifically, instruments with almost no resale value, heavy enough that carrying one off campus undetected takes planning. Nobody was ever caught. No motive surfaced. The thefts simply happened, school after school, leaving band programs short an instrument that is structurally difficult to replace mid-season, and then stopped.

Alison O'Daniel, a hard-of-hearing filmmaker who grew up wearing hearing aids and learned ASL as an adult, built a feature film around this unsolved gap. *The Tuba Thieves* premiered at Sundance in 2023. It does not solve the mystery. It uses the theft as a structuring absence — a sound that was supposed to be there, in a school auditorium, on a specific afternoon, and wasn't — and intercuts it with two other moments where sound's presence is similarly unsettled. A restaging of the 1952 premiere of John Cage's *4'33"*, the piece often described as four minutes and thirty-three seconds of silence, which is not silence at all. A restaging of a 1979 punk show held at the San Francisco Deaf Club, a space built for Deaf social life that a touring band borrowed for one loud, bass-heavy night.

Cage's piece is the hinge the whole film turns on. The pianist sits at the keyboard and does not play. What fills the four minutes and thirty-three seconds is the room: coughs, shifting seats, the building's ventilation, a stranger's breathing two rows back. The piece does not produce silence. It removes the one sound everyone came to hear and makes the audience notice that the room was never quiet to begin with — that "silence" was always a framing decision about which sounds count as signal and which get discarded as background. A hearing audience in 1952 needed Cage to engineer this revelation. A Deaf audience has never needed it explained. The room was always full of information that had nothing to do with what a stage was scheduled to produce.

The Deaf Club show works the same hinge from the other direction. For one night, a venue built around Deaf sociality hosted a hearing punk band loud enough to move the floor. The bass didn't translate into the room as something foreign that needed accommodating — it arrived as vibration, through the floor and the walls, available to anyone whose body was in contact with the building. The show wasn't designed for Deaf attendees and it wasn't designed against them. It simply existed in a medium — vibration, not air pressure parsed by an eardrum — that the venue's usual occupants had never needed anyone's permission to access.

What the film keeps doing, across the stolen tubas, the Cage restaging, and the Deaf Club night, is refusing to let sound sit still as a stable thing some people have and others lack. Sound's presence and absence move depending on who's in the room, what their body does with vibration, what counts as signal to a given listener at a given moment. A missing tuba is a different kind of absence to a band director than to a kid who never plays in band. A "silent" piano is a different kind of fullness to an audience trained to wait for the instrument than to one that was already listening to the room.

This is why the open captions matter more than a credits-page accessibility note would suggest. *The Tuba Thieves* screens with captions burned permanently into every print, for every audience, hearing or not. Most films get made for a hearing default and then have captions laid over them afterward, a translation track bolted on for whoever couldn't access the original. A film built around the argument that sound was never a stable, universally shared substrate cannot also assume a hearing-only viewer for its own dialogue. The captions aren't a courtesy extended to Deaf audiences after the real film was finished. They are part of what the film is, decided before a frame was shot, because the thing the film is asking — what is sound, who gets to decide what counts as quiet, what gets lost when something goes missing — has no honest answer if the film itself still assumes only some of its audience needs the full sentence.

The tubas were never found. The film doesn't treat that as a loose end. It was never about recovering what went missing. It was about who gets to decide, afterward, that the room was silent.
