Leo and I were fourteen, sitting in his basement watching a movie that had won all the awards.

"Watch his hands," Leo whispered, not taking his eyes off the screen.

On the TV, an actor was playing a character with cerebral palsy. Critics called it "transformative." The performance had won an Oscar.

But Leo saw something else. "He's trying to remember how to hold them," he said. "My cousin doesn't have to remember."

Leo's cousin has CP. When he moves, it's not about remembering—it's about economy. A lifetime of adaptation turned into fluid motion. What the actor was calculating, Leo's cousin had already solved.

"It's like watching someone read a script for walking," Leo said. "My cousin doesn't have a script. He has a system."

That was the moment I understood: Hollywood wasn't casting for expertise. They were casting for simulation.

## The $4.2 Million Makeup Department

Last year, a studio spent $4.2 million on prosthetic makeup for a film about a disabled veteran. The makeup took four hours to apply each day. The actor—an able-bodied star—needed two assistants to help him move between takes.

Meanwhile, disabled actors auditioning for the role were told they "lacked the star power."

This isn't just about fairness. It's about cognitive load.

When I design software, I think about cognitive load constantly. Every confusing menu, every inconsistent button, every unnecessary click—it all adds up. Users don't just get frustrated. They get tired. Their brains work overtime to decode systems that weren't designed for them.

Hollywood creates the same problem. When disabled people watch prosthetic-heavy performances, our brains work overtime too. We're not just watching a story—we're decoding layers of simulation. Noticing where the seams show. Calculating what's real versus what's applied.

The actor is thinking about holding his hands "correctly." We're thinking about why he's thinking about it.

## Pattern Recognition

Autistic brains are often called "pattern-seeking." For me, this means I notice systems. How things connect. Where efficiencies emerge. Where friction accumulates.

In Hollywood's disability representation, I see a clear pattern: prosthetics are preferred because they're controllable. Makeup departments can create exactly the "right" kind of disability—cinematic, symmetrical, photographable.

Real disability is messier. It varies day to day. It doesn't always look "right" on camera. It comes with rhythms and adaptations that can't be scripted.

But here's what the pattern misses: that messiness is where the expertise lives.

When Mara (from "The Mapmakers") watches a crosswalk, she's not just avoiding cars. She's reading a complex system of visual cues that hearing people don't need to notice. Her deafness isn't a deficit—it's a different sensor array. She processes different data.

Disabled actors bring the same expertise. A wheelchair user doesn't just "use a wheelchair"—they understand spatial dynamics in ways able-bodied actors can only simulate. An actor with CP doesn't just "have tremors"—they've developed movement economies that come from a lifetime of adaptation.

This isn't acting. It's lived system knowledge.

## The Backwards Economics

The prosthetic paradox has economics that are almost funny in how backwards they are.

Studios say they need "bankable" stars—familiar faces that guarantee box office returns. But the data tells a different story.

A 2025 study looked at 200 films with disability themes. Films with authentic disabled casting had:
- 23% lower production costs (no prosthetic departments)
- 18% higher satisfaction scores among disabled viewers
- No difference in box office performance

That $4.2 million prosthetic budget? It could have funded:
- 84 weeks of disability consultant salaries
- Apprenticeships for 210 disabled performers
- Accessibility retrofits for 12 production studios

Instead, it paid for latex and makeup artists to create a simulation of something that already exists.

## The Cognitive Tax

As an autistic designer, I think a lot about cognitive tax—the hidden cost of systems that weren't designed for you.

When I use software that wasn't designed with neurodiversity in mind, I pay a tax. My brain works harder to decode patterns, process information, navigate interfaces.

Disabled viewers pay the same tax when watching prosthetic-heavy performances. We're doing extra work:
- Decoding which movements are character choices versus actor limitations
- Noticing where prosthetic seams disrupt authentic body language
- Calculating the gap between simulated experience and lived reality
- Managing the emotional labor of seeing our experiences commodified

This tax has consequences. It makes entertainment exhausting instead of fun. It tells disabled actors their expertise is less valuable than an able-bodied actor's ability to mimic it.

## A Simpler Pattern

The solution isn't complicated. It's about recognizing patterns that already exist:

**Cast disabled actors in disabled roles**—not as a diversity checkbox, but as an expertise hire. You wouldn't cast a non-pilot to play a pilot without consulting actual pilots. Disability is expertise.

**Hire disability consultants early**—not just for "accuracy," but for system design. How would this character navigate this space? What adaptations would they have developed?

**Budget for accessibility, not prosthetics**—shift money from simulating disability to accommodating it. A wheelchair-accessible set is cheaper than a prosthetic department.

**Listen to disabled audiences**—we're not just consumers. We're experts in our own experiences. Our feedback isn't "complaints"—it's quality assurance.

## The Systems We Build

I design software for a living. My autism gives me an advantage: I notice patterns others miss. I see where systems create unnecessary friction.

Hollywood is designing a system too—a cultural system that shapes how disability is understood and valued. Their current design has friction:
- Prosthetics that simulate rather than include
- Casting that prioritizes familiarity over expertise
- Budgets that fund simulations instead of access
- Feedback loops that exclude disabled voices

But systems can be redesigned. Patterns can be changed.

The next time you watch a film with disability representation, pay attention to the patterns. Notice what feels authentic versus what feels applied. Listen to disabled viewers when they point out the seams.

And remember: expertise isn't something you can put on with makeup. It's something you live.
