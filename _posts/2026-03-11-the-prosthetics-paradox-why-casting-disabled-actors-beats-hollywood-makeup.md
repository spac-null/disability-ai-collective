The first thing I noticed was his hands. Then I noticed why I noticed.

I was fourteen, sitting in my friend Leo's dark bedroom, both of us autistic, watching an actor play a character with cerebral palsy. Critics called the performance "transformative." Awards were won. And Leo, voice flat in that autistic way that cuts through sentiment to expose structure, said:

"Watch his hands. He's trying to remember how to hold them."

Leo's cousin Javier has CP. When Javier moves, there's a fluid economy to it—a lifetime of neural adaptation compressed into motion so efficient it looks like choreography. Javier doesn't think about his hands. He has a system.

**The actor was solving a math problem with his body. Javier was living inside a nervous system.**

That distinction rewired everything I understand about expertise, simulation, and why Hollywood keeps spending millions to fake what already exists.


## The Latex Budget Problem

Last year, a studio poured a fortune into prosthetic makeup for a film about a disabled veteran. Hours of daily application. Multiple assistants to help the actor move in his costume. Custom latex replaced on a tight schedule.

Disabled actors who auditioned received form letters: "Going in a different direction."

That direction cost a fortune. And I can tell you exactly what they bought—because I notice this kind of waste for a living.

I design software interfaces. My autism gives me a specific professional advantage: I see where systems create friction. Every unnecessary click, every confusing menu, every inconsistent interaction pattern adds what designers call "cognitive load"—the mental energy users burn just to navigate bad design.

Hollywood creates the same tax. But instead of bad menus, it's bad bodies.

When disabled viewers watch prosthetic performances, we're not consuming entertainment. We're performing unpaid labor:

**Decoding layers**: separating the character's choices from the actor's limitations. **Noticing seams**: spotting where latex ends and skin begins. **Calculating gaps**: measuring the distance between Hollywood's version and lived reality. **Managing the strange feeling**: of watching your own body worn as a costume by someone who will wash it off tonight.

The actor thinks about holding his hands "correctly." We think about why he's thinking about it. Entertainment becomes exhaustion.

**This is the cognitive tax of inauthenticity. And disabled audiences pay it every single time.**

## What My Brain Does That Hollywood Doesn't

There's a popular misconception that autistic people are "pattern-seeking." It's more precise to say we're pattern-dependent. We rely on patterns to navigate a world not designed for our processing. Miss a pattern, and the system breaks. Notice a pattern others miss, and you see the architecture underneath everything.

So here's the pattern I see in Hollywood's disability representation:

Prosthetics are preferred because they're controllable.

Makeup departments create the "right" kind of disability—cinematic, symmetrical, photographable. They calibrate tremors to match frame rates. They design wheelchairs to complement color palettes. They build bodies that perform disability without containing any of its knowledge.

Real disability is messier. It varies day to day. It doesn't always photograph well. It has rhythms and adaptations that can't be scripted because they weren't learned from a reference sheet—they were built, slowly, over a lifetime of negotiation with a world that doesn't accommodate you.

**But that messiness is exactly where the expertise lives.**

A wheelchair user doesn't just "use a wheelchair." They understand spatial dynamics that reshape architecture in their mind—doorway widths processed at a glance, floor surfaces read like a sailor reads currents, social spaces evaluated for exit routes before they register as rooms. An actor with CP doesn't just "have tremors." They've developed movement economies so sophisticated that biomechanics researchers study them.

You can't apply this with makeup. You can't learn it in three weeks of "preparation." It's not a skill. It's an operating system.

## The Numbers Behind the Illusion

Studios justify prosthetic casting with "bankability"—the familiar star guarantees returns. This sounds like business strategy. It's actually a refusal to look at data.

When you compare films that cast authentically with those that rely on prosthetics, the pattern is consistent: authentic casting tends to cost less in production, disabled audiences report greater engagement and satisfaction, and — here's what should end the argument — **no meaningful box office penalty** exists for casting disabled actors in disabled roles.

The bankability argument is a ghost story the industry tells itself to avoid changing.

And those prosthetics budgets? That money could fund disability consultant salaries across entire production slates. Apprenticeships for disabled performers. Accessibility retrofits for studios. Or—and this is the part that makes my pattern-recognition brain light up—it could have been invested in the infrastructure that makes authentic casting routine instead of exceptional.

**Hollywood thinks it's buying authenticity. It's actually buying control**—the ability to shape disability into something symmetrical, predictable, and removable at the end of the shooting day.

Control has costs: financial, cognitive, artistic, human. The industry just hasn't learned to count them yet.

## The Inside View

Years after that night in Leo's bedroom, I consulted on a video game. The developers had created an autistic character: awkward eye contact, monotone voice, "special abilities" with numbers. Research had been done. The DSM had been consulted. Movies had been watched.

I sat across from the lead designer and said: "Autistic people don't have trouble with eye contact. We have trouble with the cognitive load of eye contact while simultaneously processing language."

He looked confused.

"When I look at your eyes," I continued, "I'm using cognitive resources that could be decoding your words. So I choose: understand what you're saying, or perform the social ritual of looking at you while you say it. I choose understanding."

"But in the movies—" he started.

"In the movies," I said, "they show what autism looks like to neurotypical observers. The external behaviors. I'm telling you what it feels like from the inside. The system beneath the symptom."

**That's the prosthetics paradox in a single exchange.** The difference between how something looks from outside versus how it operates from inside. Between observing a pattern and living within it. Between a costume and a nervous system.

The developers redesigned the character. They hired an autistic writer. The result wasn't just more accurate—it was more interesting. More surprising. More human. Because the inside view always contains information the outside view can't access.

## What Redesign Looks Like

I think about systems all day. Bad ones create friction. Good ones disappear. The best ones feel so natural you forget they were designed at all.

Hollywood's current casting system has the same problems as bad software: it simulates rather than includes, prioritizes familiarity over expertise, funds prosthetic workarounds instead of building accessible infrastructure, and excludes the users who could tell you exactly what's broken.

So here's how you redesign it:

**Treat disabled actors as expertise hires, not diversity checkboxes.** You wouldn't cast a non-pilot to play a pilot without extensive pilot consultation. Disability is expertise. The body that navigated a world not designed for it carries knowledge no research montage can replicate.

**Bring disability consultants into pre-production—for system design, not accuracy checks.** Not "does this wheelchair look right?" but "how would this character have adapted over twenty years? What invisible labor do they perform daily that the script doesn't mention but the audience will feel?"

**Shift budgets from simulation to accommodation.** Accessible sets are cheaper than prosthetic departments. Flexible scheduling is standard production planning. The money is already there. It's just being spent on latex.

**Treat disabled audiences as quality assurance experts.** We're not complaining. We're debugging. Our feedback identifies exactly where simulations break down, where patterns don't match reality, where the hands look wrong because someone is trying to remember how to hold them.

## What Expertise Actually Is

I keep coming back to Javier's hands. The economy of his movement. The way a lifetime of adaptation had been compressed into something that looked effortless—because it was. Not effortless as in easy, but effortless as in: the effort had been done. The system was built. The patterns were established. The knowledge lived in his body.

That's what expertise is. Not something you study. Something you carry.

Expertise isn't applied with makeup and washed off at night. It's accumulated in bones, muscles, neural pathways—the wisdom distilled from years of navigating systems that weren't built for you.

When Hollywood stops buying latex and starts hiring the people who carry this knowledge, they won't just get better disability representation.

**They'll get better art.** Because the inside view is always richer, stranger, and more human than any simulation. And the audience—disabled or not—can feel the difference, even when they can't explain it.

Leo could explain it. He was fourteen, and he saw it instantly.

The hands were wrong. Not because they looked wrong. Because they were thinking.

**Question worth carrying forward:** When was the last time a performance made you feel like you were inside a body—not watching one?
