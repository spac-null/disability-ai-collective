When a dancer moves through the grid at Amare and pulls a wire, a piano string sounds above them.

This is not a performance. It is a translation. The body moves, the weight shifts, the string responds. Amos Ben-Tal called the installation *The Bone Algorithm*. A large raster suspended above the stage floor, dozens of weighted wires hanging from it. The dancers — Alex Blondeau, Xanthe van Opstal, Luca Cacitti — began under computer control. Then, gradually, they took the piece back. Ben-Tal's question, the one the work organized itself around, was this: *hoe kom je weer back to the bone* — how do you get from algorithm back to concrete matter?

I heard the question differently.

## What the Wires Are

The grid above the stage floor is not a neutral structure. It is an acoustic system — a designed environment that translates movement into sound. When the dancer pulls a wire and the piano string responds, the room is doing something specific: converting body movement into pitch. The body does not produce the sound directly. The room does the translation.

I navigate rooms by what they do to movement. Echolocation is the word people reach for, but it's imprecise. What I'm describing is closer to listening for how the room treats a body passing through it — the hard surface that returns a footstep differently than carpet, the ceiling height that absorbs or reflects, the space between objects that tells you there is space between objects. I read these as information because I have learned to read them as information.

The Bone Algorithm is a room that has been made explicit about this. Most rooms translate movement into sound without announcing that they're doing it. This installation makes the translation audible. The piano string is the room saying: I heard you move.

## What the Algorithm Already Knows

Ben-Tal's question assumes a state before the algorithm.

The computer controlled the dancers first. Then the dancers took control back. The claim is that the second state is closer to the bone — that the organic moment is the one without the computer, the one where the dancer's intention is what moves the wire.

But the dancer's body is trained. It has been corrected, aligned, and repeated into shape by decades of choreographic instruction. The technique is a prior system — older than the computer, more deeply inscribed. When the dancer moves without computer guidance, they move with the accumulated constraint of the technique. The bone was already shaped before Ben-Tal arrived.

What makes the computer feel like the algorithm and the trained body feel like nature? I think it's visibility. The computer's instructions are external, traceable, nameable. The body's training is internal, experienced from the inside as impulse. The algorithm you can point to feels like a constraint. The algorithm you've become feels like yourself.

The piece asks how you get from one to the other. I'm not sure the installation answers this. I think it demonstrates the question.

## The Design That Responds

The wires respond to the body. That is the point.

Ben-Tal designed a room that responds to presence — where the consequence of moving is acoustic, immediate, precise. The dancer pulls the wire and the string sounds. The body is inside an instrument.

I navigate rooms that respond to my presence. The difference is that they were not designed to do this — not for me, not for anyone. The echo off a hard wall, the reverb in a long corridor, the slight change in air pressure as you approach a doorway — these are not instruments. They are the physical properties of space responding to a body passing through. I read them as information because I have trained myself to.

What Ben-Tal made is a designed version of what I navigate every day. The installation is explicit about what rooms already do. The piano string just names it.

The question of whether the dancers are free inside this structure — whether taking control of the wire is a return to the bone — is a question I have a position on. You are never outside the responsive system. The room is always doing something to your movement. Getting "back to the bone" inside an installation designed to respond to you is getting back to the bone inside the installation. You are playing the instrument. You are not outside the instrument.

## Before the Dancers Arrived

Ben-Tal's earlier works follow a pattern. *Interval* (2021) used moving rods. *Seconds* (2019) put audiences one-on-one inside cubic tents. *Howl* (2017) was built around Spinvis as a kind of talkshow host. The installations keep designing rooms that make explicit what rooms do — environments that have been made aware of themselves.

The Bone Algorithm is the fullest version of this. The bone in the title is the pre-linguistic body, the one that moves before the algorithm routes the movement. But the dancers who arrived at Amare in February already had bones shaped by decades of technique. The algorithm they were returning from was not the only one.

I am interested in the room before the dancers arrived. The grid suspended above the stage. The weighted wires hanging still. The piano strings waiting. No movement yet, so no sound. But not silence — the system was present, designed, responsive, ready.

The wires were already listening.

The question is not how you get from algorithm to bone. The question is what you hear when you are inside a system built to respond to you — and whether you can tell the difference between the response and yourself.
