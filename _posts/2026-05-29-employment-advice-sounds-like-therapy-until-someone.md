A friend of mine sat in an NHS therapy room in Manchester in 2022. She had come for anxiety. She left with a folder about jobs. The therapist had asked about work in the first session — not as a symptom, as a goal. My friend described panic attacks on the Tube. The therapist asked what kind of work environment might suit her better. My friend said she wasn't ready to think about work yet. The therapist wrote something down. My friend never went back.

The UK government released research last week confirming what we've been living through: the DWP's latest attempt to insert itself into therapy spaces. The study, conducted by researchers at the Institute for Fiscal Studies, tracked people for three years. For those classified as long-term sick or disabled — people unable to work because of health conditions lasting months or longer — receiving both therapy and employment advice reduced the probability of being in paid employment by nearly four percentage points. Average monthly earnings fell by £150.

Dr Jay Watts, a clinical psychologist and researcher who has written extensively about how welfare policies affect mental health treatment, said the therapy room is meant to be the one place a person isn't appraised for their economic usefulness. The moment the work-and-welfare system enters that space, the therapy itself becomes compromised. What this means in practice is that the person being treated starts to feel they are only valuable if they can work, which works against the healing process.

I am interested in what this compromise sounds like.

A therapy session has an acoustic signature. The room is small. Carpeted, usually. Two chairs facing each other at an angle — not directly opposite, which would be confrontational, but not side by side either. The therapist speaks in a particular register: quiet, even, rhythmically consistent. Silence is allowed to sit. You can hear the heating click on. You can hear traffic outside the window three floors down. The room is designed to be separate from the world.

An employment advice session has a different acoustic signature. It happens in an open-plan office or a room with multiple desks. The adviser speaks at normal conversational volume. There is typing. Phones ring in adjacent rooms. The soundscape says: this is continuous with the rest of your life. What you say here connects directly to decisions being made elsewhere.

When the two sessions happen in the same room, the acoustic confusion is immediate. You arrive for therapy. You hear typing. The person across from you has a keyboard now. They say they are there to support you. They ask about your mental health. Then they ask about your CV.

The folder my friend was given had a title in bold sans-serif: *Pathways to Work*. She brought it home and left it on the kitchen table for two weeks. She said it felt wrong to throw it away — like refusing help. But opening it felt worse. Another friend in our support group had gotten the same folder. She'd turned hers into origami cranes, one for each job listing she was supposed to apply for. "At least this way," she said, "they're flying."

In acoustic design, there is a concept where one sound gets in the way of another without completely blocking it. White noise machines work this way. The hum does not remove the conversation happening on the other side of the wall — it just makes it unintelligible. You know something is being said. You cannot make out the words. This produces more anxiety than silence.

Employment advice doesn't just interfere with therapy — it transforms it into something else entirely. The policymakers who design these programs insist integration makes sense: support the whole person, address practical barriers to recovery. The session still uses therapeutic language: support, wellbeing, goals. But underneath, a different conversation is happening. The question is no longer *what do you need?* It is *when will you be ready?*

My friend found her own timeline. Six months after that first session, she was volunteering at a community garden — not because anyone suggested it, but because the tomatoes didn't care about her employment status. The other volunteers included three people who'd been through the same program. They'd all found their way here, to the greenhouse, where nobody asked about CVs.

A woman who works in occupational health told me she once sat in on one of these joint sessions as an observer. She said the therapist would ask an open question — *how have you been feeling this week?* — and the employment adviser would follow it immediately with a closed one: *have you applied for any positions?* She said the client started answering the second question only. The first one became rhetorical.

I keep thinking about the specific number: £148.30 per month. Three years after receiving both therapy and employment advice, people who were long-term sick or disabled earned nearly £150 less per month than people who just received therapy. That is not a rounding error. That is two weeks of groceries. That is a monthly travel pass.

The study does not explain why earnings fell. It just records that they did. But I can hear it. The person in that room is trying to answer two questions at once: *what is wrong with me?* and *what am I worth?* Those questions cannot occupy the same acoustic space. One will always drown out the other.

The compromise is not accidental. It is structural. The employment adviser is there because someone decided therapy should have measurable economic outcomes. Not outcomes for the person receiving therapy — outcomes for the state. Therapy became a site of economic intervention the moment someone asked: how do we get more people back to work?

The answer, it turns out, is: you don't. You make them earn less and work less. You introduce a second agenda into a room that only functions when there is one.

My friend never went back to that therapist. She found a different one six months later. Private, not NHS. The first thing the new therapist said was: we will not talk about work unless you bring it up. My friend said she cried with relief.

I asked her what the original therapist sounded like. She said: like someone reading from two scripts at once.

The government study will be discussed in terms of policy failure. Those discussions will happen in rooms with good acoustics. The people in those rooms will not hear what my friend heard: the moment when care became conditional on your willingness to be employed.

The therapy room was the one place that should have been separate. Now it sounds like everywhere else.
