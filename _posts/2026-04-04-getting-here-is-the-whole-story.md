In November 2022, Transport for London published a map. Not the Tube map — everyone knows that one. This was the Step-Free Tube Map, showing which stations you can actually use if you cannot climb stairs. The map is mostly blank. Seventy-nine of 272 stations are step-free. That is the official count, and it includes stations where "step-free" means a ramp to the platform but a 76-millimetre gap between the train and the edge that will swallow a front caster. I know because I have been swallowed.

In the same month, Tokyo Metro published its own accessibility guide. Two hundred and ninety stations. All of them step-free. Staff at every platform to deploy a boarding ramp. The guide runs to 40 pages and includes the exact width of every gate in centimetres.

---

You think this is about investment. It is partly about investment. Tokyo spent decades and billions making that system work. The real difference is earlier than money. It is about what the designers thought they were building.

Henri Lefebvre argued in 1974 that space is not a container people move through but a thing people produce. Every city produces its transit system in the image of the body it considers default. London's Underground was built for the walking, standing, stair-climbing body. Retrofitting it costs more than building it right, and every time the bill arrives, someone says *heritage constraints.* The architecture becomes its own alibi.

I was in King's Cross in August 2023. The lift from the Northern line to the surface was broken. A staff member told me to go back three stops to Euston, take the lift there, catch a bus to King's Cross overground. I had a meeting in fourteen minutes. He shrugged with his whole body. I could tell he had shrugged that way before.

The shrug is the thing. Not cruelty. Not even indifference. Just the physical vocabulary of a system that has practiced saying *not my problem* so many times the gesture is smooth.

Meanwhile, in Tokyo, I watched — in March 2019 — a station attendant sprint the length of a platform in Shinjuku to deploy a ramp before the doors opened. No one had asked. He saw the chair. He moved. The whole interaction took nine seconds. I timed it because I could not believe it.

You might say: culture. You might say: Japan has a different relationship to service, to public obligation, to the collective. Sure. But Carmen Papalia, who works at the intersection of [non-visual access and public space](https://carmenpapalia.com/), has said something I keep returning to: access is not a feature of a space, it is a relationship between people. The station attendant in Shinjuku was not following a protocol. He was in a relationship with me for nine seconds, and both of us knew it, and then it was done.

The staff member at King's Cross was also in a relationship with me. The shrug told me what kind.

---

Here is where the honest concession goes. London's system is 160 years old. It was dug by hand under a city that was already ancient. No one in 1863 imagined a wheelchair user because the wheelchair user was not imagined as a public person. Tokyo's post-war rebuild gave it the chance to start over, and the 2000 Barrier-Free Transportation Law gave it the mandate. London has geology, Victorian brickwork, and a funding model that makes every lift shaft a ten-year argument.

And then I read that Crossrail — the Elizabeth line, brand new, opened 2022, seventeen billion pounds — launched with platform gaps so wide that wheelchair users needed manual boarding ramps at multiple stations. Brand new. Seventeen billion. The gaps were not inherited. Someone designed them.

Gareth Thomas, a structural engineer who worked on Crossrail's accessibility review, told *Disability News Service* in 2022 that the platform-to-train interface was "optimized for throughput capacity." Throughput capacity. The phrase means: getting the most walking bodies through the fastest. The gap is not an accident. It is a priority made physical.

Sunaura Taylor wrote that the category of the normal body does not describe most bodies — it disciplines them. Throughput capacity is the transit version. The system is not failing. It is succeeding at something it will not say out loud.

I keep thinking about the two maps. Tokyo's, dense with stations, all of them reachable. London's, mostly white space. Both maps tell the truth. But the Step-Free Tube Map does something I find almost [perversely generous](https://urbanomnibus.net/2014/02/getting-you-there-public-transit-as-an-instrument-of-freedom/): it shows you exactly how small your city is. Three stops on the Northern line, two on the Jubilee, a scattering on the Overground. That is your London. The rest is decoration.

Mike Oliver drew the line in 1983: impairment is in the body, disability is in the structure. The Step-Free Tube Map is the social model in cartographic form. You can see the structure. You can count the stations it took from you.

I do not need the map anymore. I memorized it years ago, the way you memorize the layout of a house you grew up in. I know which stations are mine and which belong to someone else's city. Every Tuesday I check the service alerts for lift outages and my city gets smaller or larger depending on what is broken.

In Shinjuku, the attendant was already running before the train stopped.
