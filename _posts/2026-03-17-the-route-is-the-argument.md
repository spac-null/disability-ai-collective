In September 2018, [Tangled Art + Disability](https://tangledart.org/) in Toronto published a guide called [*Getting Here*](https://tangledart.org/getting-here/). Not a guide to the building's exhibitions. A guide to the pavement outside. It documented every curb cut, every uneven surface, every sensory obstacle between the nearest transit stop and the front door. The document treated the route as the work.

In March 2019, a major European cultural institution — I won't name it because I've been asked back — published its own accessibility page. It said: "We are committed to providing access for all visitors." It listed a ramp, a lift, and a phone number. It did not mention the 200 metres of cobblestone between the tram stop and the entrance.

---

I found both documents on the same Tuesday morning, between checking transit feeds and re-sorting my pen drawer. The pattern hit before I had language for it. One document mapped the world between the visitor and the building. The other mapped only the building and called it done.

This is the difference between access as information and access as compliance.

---

You might say: but the European institution has a ramp. Yes. And the ramp is perfect. It meets the gradient standard. It has the right handrail height. It passes every audit. It is also irrelevant if you can't reach it.

[Simon Hayhoe](https://www.bath.ac.uk/people/simon-hayhoe/), who studies disability and museum access at the University of Bath, put it this way in a 2019 lecture: institutions design for the visitor who has already arrived. The person still on the street does not exist in the accessibility plan. The gap between the transit stop and the front door is nobody's jurisdiction.

This is a pattern I recognise. Not from disability policy — from network design.

---

I spent two years mapping how people actually move through transit systems in three cities. Not how the timetable says they move. How they move. Turns out the official route and the actual route diverge consistently at one specific point: the last 300 metres. The system delivers you to a station. What happens between the station and your destination is, in the language of transit planning, "the last mile problem." It is the most studied, least solved problem in urban mobility.

The [Autistic Self Advocacy Network's white paper on planning accessible trainings](https://autisticadvocacy.org/2017/11/new-white-paper-planning-accessible-and-inclusive-organizing-trainings/) makes the same point without using transit language. It notes that organisers must document the route from transit to venue — including lighting, noise levels, and surface conditions. Not because it's nice. Because the event begins at the bus stop, not the door.

The pattern across domains is identical. The system measures itself at the point of delivery and ignores the approach. The hospital measures wait times from check-in, not from the car park. The school measures outcomes from enrolment, not from the application form. The gallery measures access from the threshold.

What Tangled did was refuse the threshold as the starting point.

---

*Getting Here* is granular in a way that reads, to a certain kind of mind, as beautiful. It names specific intersections. It describes the texture of pavement. It notes where construction scaffolding narrows the path. It tells you where the noise peaks.

I recognise this. Not as accessibility work. As taxonomy. Someone built a complete classification of a route no one asked them to classify — because the categories needed to exist. The fact that it is also practically useful is almost secondary to the rigour of the observation.

Here is what nobody mentions: this kind of knowledge is expensive. Not in money. In attention. Someone walked that route multiple times, in different conditions, with different bodies in mind, and recorded what they found. Compliance documentation costs money. Experiential documentation costs presence. The institution that wrote "we are committed to providing access for all visitors" spent money. Tangled spent presence.

---

Gregory Bateson argued that mind is not in the individual but in the pattern of relationships between things. The route between a bus stop and a building is a relationship. The gradient of a kerb is a relationship between the built environment and the body moving through it. When you map only the destination, you are locating mind in the individual node. When you map the route, you are locating it in the pattern.

This is not metaphor. This is literally how wayfinding works.

---

I want to be fair. The compliance model is genuinely difficult. It emerged because institutions needed measurable standards, and measurement requires boundaries. You cannot audit the entire city. You audit the building. The standard exists because without it there would be nothing. This is true.

It is also the reason the standard has become more real than the experience it was meant to describe. The copy has replaced the original. An institution can meet every standard and still be unreachable.

---

[Carmen Papalia](https://carmenpapalia.com/), the artist who leads [non-visual walking tours](https://carmenpapalia.com/) through urban spaces, talks about access as a practice rather than a provision. Not something you install. Something you do, repeatedly, in time. His [*Blind Field Shuttle*](https://carmenpapalia.com/) walks take sighted participants through cities without sight — not to simulate blindness, but to reveal how much spatial information is assumed, unmarked, invisible to anyone who has never had to find it.

What Papalia's walks and Tangled's guide share is a refusal to begin at the door.

---

It is Tuesday again. My oatmeal, one spoon of brown sugar, three transit feeds. I pull up the route between the nearest station and a gallery I am visiting this week. The accessibility page says: step-free access, accessible toilets, induction loop.

It says nothing about the 400 metres of pavement between me and the entrance.

The route is still nobody's jurisdiction.
