There is a church in Utrecht — the Janskerk — where a single handclap returns to you eleven seconds later. I know this because I stood in the nave in March 2019 and clapped once and counted. The stone gave it back to me so slowly I had time to forget I'd made the sound. I have never felt more precisely located in space. There are three steps at the west entrance and no ramp.

I have written about this church twice. Both times I wrote about the acoustic. Both times I left out the steps.

---

Here is the thing I have not admitted in any professional context: I love buildings that exclude me. Not because I enjoy exclusion. Because the acoustic qualities that make certain spaces extraordinary — stone, height, hard parallel surfaces, unbroken floor planes — are frequently produced by the same architectural choices that make those spaces inaccessible. The ramp breaks the resonance. The lift shaft absorbs the low frequencies. I know this in my body before I know it as an argument, and I have been complicit in my own silence about it for years.

[Sins Invalid](https://sinsinvalid.org/) has been running their Disability Justice campaign since 2006, and one of their core principles is that bodies are sites of both oppression and liberation simultaneously. Not sequentially. Not one after the other. Both at once, in the same skin, in the same room. What nobody talks about is that this simultaneity extends to pleasure. The space that thrills me acoustically is the space that injures me physically. I have never seen this contradiction named.

---

Two buildings. The first: that church in Utrecht. Romanesque. Built in the twelfth century. The reverberation time hovers around six seconds at middle frequencies, which means a choir singing there produces overtones the singers themselves cannot control. The building finishes the music. I went three times in 2019. Each time someone guided me up the steps. Each time I pretended this was fine.

The second: the [Bridgewater Hall](https://www.bridgewater-hall.co.uk/) in Manchester, opened in 1996. Purpose-built concert venue. Accessible from every entrance. Level access, hearing loops, tactile wayfinding on every floor. The acoustic is precise, engineered, flawless. I attended a performance of [Feldman's *For Philip Guston*](https://www.schott-music.com/shop/for-philip-guston-neue-ausgabe-englisch-9790001137231) there in November 2022 and spent four hours in a seat that was easy to reach, in a hall that was built to include me, listening to music that moved me. The reverberation is controlled to within a fraction of a second. Nothing surprises. Nothing comes back late. Nothing that the designers did not intend.

You might say: so the accessible building works. Yes. It works. It works the way a perfectly calibrated hearing aid works — everything arrives, nothing is missing, and something is gone.

---

I am not arguing against accessibility. I need to say that plainly because the argument I am actually making is worse. I am arguing that I have been silent about a genuine aesthetic loss, and that my silence has made it easier for architects to pretend the loss does not exist. When we cannot name what accessibility costs acoustically, we cannot design for both. We get Bridgewater Hall — competent, inclusive, dead.

[Georgina Kleege](https://english.berkeley.edu/users/45) wrote in [*More Than Meets the Eye*](https://www.routledge.com/More-Than-Meets-the-Eye-Representations-of-Disability/Kleege/p/book/9780415914246) that blind people are often better critics of visual culture than sighted people because we have been forced to think about what seeing means. The same is true of acoustic space. I know more about what reverberation does to a body than most hearing architects because I have no other way into the room. And what I know — the indefensible thing — is that the Janskerk is better. Not better for me. Not better ethically. Better acoustically. And that the reason it is better is partly the reason I cannot get in.

---

In 2017, the acoustic engineer [Jian Kang](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/bartlett/environmental-design/people/jian-kang) at University College London published research showing that adding ramp structures and lift shafts to heritage churches reduced reverberation time by up to 15 percent in certain frequency ranges. When I read this I felt — not vindicated. Seen. Someone had measured the thing I had been hearing. What nobody mentions is that Kang's own recommendation was not to stop making churches accessible. It was to redesign the interventions using materials that reflect rather than absorb. Stone-clad ramps. Glass-walled lifts. Solutions that cost dramatically more than standard installations. Solutions that nobody funds.

An architect I spoke with in Rotterdam in 2023 — she designs cultural spaces, she asked me not to use her name — said something that has not left me. "I know the lift shaft kills the room. I spec it anyway because if I raise the acoustic argument, it becomes a reason not to build the lift. I have watched that happen three times."

She is protecting access by hiding the cost. I have been protecting my aesthetic pleasure by hiding the exclusion. We are both lying about the same room.

---

[Mia Mingus](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/) writes about [access as a practice of love](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/2018/11/03/disability-justice-is-simply-another-term-for-love/), not a checklist. What I hear in that framing is permission to name the full weight of what love requires. Including the loss. Including the sound that disappears when the room finally lets you in.

I went back to the Janskerk in January 2024. There is a temporary ramp now, aluminum, municipal standard. I walked in alone for the first time. I clapped once. The sound came back in nine seconds.
