The Barbican hung a [Lynette Yiadom-Boakye](https://www.thaddaeusropac.com/artists/lynette-yiadom-boakye) painting in an exhibition called [Project a Black Planet](https://www.barbican.org.uk/whats-on/project-black-planet) and buried it in wall text explaining what visions of a united African future it was supposed to illustrate. The Guardian reviewer walked through and heard theory where the painting was trying to sing. I read that review and thought: I know this room. Not the Barbican. The room where someone decides a thing that already communicates perfectly needs an explanation because the original communication happens in the wrong medium.

Yiadom-Boakye paints fictional people. A young woman reading. A man in Pierrot clothes standing alone. These are not portraits. They are inventions. The paintings do not require interpretation to mean something. They mean through color and composition and the specific tilt of a head. But someone — a curator, a committee, an institutional reflex — decided that was not enough. They wrote text. They placed the text next to the painting. Now you read the text first and look at the painting second, and the painting becomes illustration for the text instead of the other way around.

This is not about access. Audio description for paintings is a medium in itself. [Leonard Longmore](https://www.leonard-cheshire.org/about-us/our-history/leonard-cheshire) and [Georgina Kleege](https://english.berkeley.edu/users/45), scholars of disability and accessibility, have both written about how audio description creates its own aesthetic experience — the rhythm of a voice tracing the curve of a brushstroke, the pause before describing shadow. I've stood in galleries where the describer's voice made me laugh at a detail I would have rushed past. The describer has to actually look. In museum spaces, I navigate between what's on the wall and what's in my ears, each telling me different stories. The wall text announces. The audio description discovers.

The complaint in the Guardian review is about too much theory. My complaint is about sequence. The theory arrived first. The artwork became evidence for something it was not trying to prove.

I have been in gallery spaces where the acoustics are designed to let you hear one painting at a time — audio guides that direct sound to specific locations in the room so the description stays with the object. You walk close, the voice begins. You step away, it fades. The voice does not follow you. It lives with the painting. That is respect for the original medium. The voice knows it is secondary.

What the Barbican did — burying visual work under written interpretation — is the inverse of what happens when institutions try to make sound-based work accessible by adding a visual diagram. The diagram becomes the work. The sound becomes secondary. Sometimes institutional context does open new conversations — I've seen wall text that places work in dialogue with history the artist couldn't have known. But that's different from using text to contain what the work already says. Both the Barbican's move and the diagram problem come from not trusting the original medium to communicate. That it requires translation into institutional language before it can be trusted.

Georgina Kleege wrote once about the pleasure of building a painting in her mind from description — how the accumulation of details creates something that belongs to her in a way the visual painting never could. It's not about understanding better. It's about understanding differently. The same is true in reverse. Sighted curators often do not trust visual work to carry its own meaning. They add words. They think the words clarify. What the words actually do is move the site of meaning from the painting to the institution.

The review called it "indigestible intellectual stew." That is half right. The stew is not the problem. The problem is serving the stew first and calling it an appetizer for the painting.

Yiadom-Boakye's fictional man in Pierrot clothes still stands there in the frame, but now he stands inside someone else's argument about visions of a united African future.
