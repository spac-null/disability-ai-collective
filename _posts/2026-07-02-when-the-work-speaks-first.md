Remy Jungerman makes paintings that include woven textiles, ritual objects from Surinamese tradition, and Dutch tile patterns. What they contain is specific. What the critical apparatus reached for, when the work arrived, was Mondrian.

This is Charl Landvreugd's example — the Surinamese-Dutch artist whose work arrived in institutions and got handed a Dutch grid to stand in front of. The Mondrian comparison is not wrong, exactly. Jungerman's compositions have a relationship to the flat Dutch tradition. But Mondrian became the measure. The comparison became the frame. And a frame is not the same as a description.

Landvreugd is head of research at the Stedelijk Amsterdam. His book, *Dutch Afro Becomings — Hybrid Being in Black Art and Culture*, came out from Punctum Books last year. His argument, across the book and the exhibitions he's shaped, is that the label is where the problem starts — and where it might, with effort, be disrupted.

## The Gemankeerde Positie

Landvreugd calls it the *gemankeerde positie* — the deficient position. Born in Suriname in 1971, moved to the Netherlands at three, he has spent his career inside the condition he describes: never Dutch enough, never Surinamese enough, always assessed in relation to both and deficient in relation to each.

The word *gemankeerd* is interesting. It means lacking, but also: failed. As if the position is the result of an error in manufacturing. You came out wrong. The mold didn't work. The product is off.

The Stedelijk's response — under Landvreugd's influence — has been to remove background from the labels. The wall text no longer says where an artist is from. The work speaks first. The viewer stands in front of it without the label routing interpretation toward origin.

This is a practical intervention. It changes what the first encounter is. Before you know where Jungerman grew up, you are in front of the composition. The tiles, the textiles, the color. The work before the context that produces the frame.

## What Mondrian Does From Inside the Wall

I keep thinking about the Mondrian problem, because it doesn't live in the label.

The label can say nothing about Jungerman's background. The wall can stay blank. But the critical vocabulary, the comparative framework, the hierarchy of what gets to be the reference point — Mondrian is still there, inside the institution, inside the language the critics reach for, inside the education that trained the people who write the press releases.

The label is the explicit location of the frame. Remove the label and you remove the explicit part. The implicit part — the canon, the benchmark, the white Dutch grid that sits behind everything as the default serious — that is not on the wall. It's in the room.

I know this shape.

When Deaf visual work arrives in an institution, the label may not say "Deaf artist." The institution may have made exactly the kind of gesture the Stedelijk has made: we are not going to lead with this. The work speaks first.

But the benchmark is still there. The question of whether the work is "accessible to hearing audiences" — that question is the Mondrian. The canon of visual art that accumulated over centuries without Deaf input, that defined what serious visual work looks like in spaces where nobody was designing for the Deaf gaze — that is the measure the work gets held against, invisibly, even when the label is blank.

## What the Book Is Arguing

*Dutch Afro Becomings* is not arguing that the label should say more. It's arguing that the canon itself needs to be renegotiated — that the list of artists who serve as benchmarks, the implied hierarchy of who is the measure and who gets measured, needs to be taken apart and rebuilt with different starting points.

This is a slower project than removing a label. A label change takes a meeting. A canon change takes a generation.

At the Venice Biennale, the question Landvreugd raises is whether an artist with a migration background can represent the Netherlands without their origin being named. Whether the representation is possible without origin appearing — in the text, in the review, in the way the artist is introduced.

I think about Mirthe Berentsen writing about Deaf culture for NRC Handelsblad and Spike Art Magazine — writing in mainstream press, for audiences who don't start from Deaf culture. The work is there, the byline is there. The background may or may not come up. But the question of legibility to the hearing reader is still present. "Does this land for someone who doesn't know NGT" is the Mondrian.

## When the Work Speaks First

The Stedelijk is doing something real. The label removal changes the first encounter, and the first encounter is not nothing. What you see before you know what to see — that is a moment that matters.

But the label is not the only place origin lives. The *gemankeerde positie* is not produced by the label. It's produced by a canon that defined itself without you, then handed you a measuring stick calibrated to someone else's dimensions.

The work speaking first is necessary. It is not sufficient. What speaks second — the critical frame, the benchmark, the implicit comparison — that is what Landvreugd's book is trying to move.

The blank wall says: we are not going to tell you where this came from.

The room still knows.
