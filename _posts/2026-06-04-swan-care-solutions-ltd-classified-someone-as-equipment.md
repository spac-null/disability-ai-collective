Shabin Shaji won his employment tribunal case this week. The case involved allegations that Swan Care Solutions Ltd had recruited him from India on a health and care visa — a UK visa category that allows workers to take care jobs, often at lower pay — then paid him £5 an hour for night shifts in a residential home. The UK national minimum wage at the time was £10.42. When he complained, they stopped rostering him. When he raised a grievance, they dismissed him. The tribunal found in his favour on every count: unlawful deduction of wages, victimisation, and unfair dismissal. This means the tribunal decided the company had illegally taken money from his pay, punished him for complaining, and wrongfully terminated his employment.

The judgment is twenty-three pages. Page eleven contains the line that makes the system visible. Swan Care Solutions argued that Mr Shaji's accommodation — a room in the care home — was part of his salary package, and could therefore be deducted from his wages. The tribunal rejected this. But the claim reveals something deeper about how the visa system works. A person recruited to provide care is also someone who needs somewhere to sleep. Swan Care Solutions treated housing as compensation. The structure of the visa allows this. It classifies a worker's needs as costs.

I recognise this inversion. I know that moment when a new care worker arrives exhausted from a double shift, when the person helping us transfer hasn't eaten since yesterday. The visa system promises opportunities, but what opportunity exists in £5 an hour minus housing costs? The person becomes infrastructure. Their need for food, sleep, a room becomes not a human requirement but a logistical problem to be optimised. The visa defines the worker by function — health and care worker, seasonal agricultural worker — not by personhood. Once you define someone by what they do for you, you create conditions where their other needs become secondary concerns. And when our care workers are ground down by exhaustion and financial stress, we feel it in our bodies — rushed transfers, missed medications, the absence of genuine human connection that makes care relationships work.

This is not metaphor. This is accounting. When you pay someone £5 an hour and deduct housing costs, you are not paying them to work. You are charging them to be present. The wage becomes negative. The worker is not earning money. The worker is accumulating debt by existing in the place where you need them to exist.

Systems that classify people by utility always end up treating survival needs as optional extras. The companies say these visas create opportunities, bring workers to countries with better prospects. But opportunity requires more than permission to work — it requires the possibility of building a life. Mr Shaji needed to sleep. Swan Care Solutions saw a deduction. The visa system permits this because it was designed around the employer's needs, not the worker's. The employer sees the worker as input. Inputs do not have needs. Inputs have costs.

Nicholas McGeehan is a researcher who has documented labour abuses in Qatar's construction sector. He calls the system the "sponsorship trap." The employer controls the visa. The worker cannot leave without losing their legal status. In Qatar it was called kafala — a Middle Eastern sponsorship system that ties workers to their employers. In the UK it is called a sponsored work visa. The mechanism is identical. The person is fixed in place by the document that permits them to be there.

I think of Marcus, who worked nights at my friend's residential home. Even after twelve-hour shifts, he'd sit and really listen to her stories about her cats. That quality of presence — it only happened because he fought to maintain his humanity despite the system. When care works, it's because people like Marcus refuse to become infrastructure. But they shouldn't have to fight so hard just to remain human.

The tribunal awarded Mr Shaji £28,048. Swan Care Solutions is appealing.

This is one case in a pattern. [The UK's WorkWell employment scheme recruited 0.56 percent of its targets](/2026/05/30/nhs-lancashire-and-south-cumbria-recruited/) — and recorded the missing 99.44 percent in a dropdown labelled 'won't comply.' The employer sees input costs. The scheme sees compliance failures. The person is absent from both calculations.
