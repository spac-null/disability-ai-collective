In 1615, the Quechua writer Felipe Guaman Poma de Ayala, an Indigenous chronicler documenting Inca culture under Spanish colonial rule, finished a 1,189-page letter to King Philip III of Spain. It included nearly four hundred drawings of Inca life. The letter was sent to Spain but somehow ended up in the [Royal Danish Library](https://www.kb.dk/en), where it sat for three centuries before anyone paid attention. One thing Guaman Poma drew repeatedly: people gathered in architectural spaces, mouths open, instruments raised. He was drawing sound. The buildings were for sound. Nobody saw that either.

The structure everyone is talking about this month is [Inkawasi](https://www.peru.travel/en/what-to-do/attractions/archaeological-sites/inkawasi), a pre-Incan ceremonial site in Peru's Cañete Valley that archaeologists study to understand how ancient Andean cultures designed public spaces. Archaeologists from the Ministry of Culture confirmed what acoustic analysis—the scientific study of how sound behaves in physical spaces—suggested: the rounded interior walls of its central hall were shaped to amplify the human voice. Not decorated to amplify status. Not scaled to project imperial dominance. Shaped — curved, angled, surfaced — to control how sound moved through the room in specific ways. The building thought in vibration.

This should not be surprising. It is.

---

Archaeology has a sensory hierarchy. It inherited the hierarchy from the Enlightenment, a period in 17th and 18th-century European thought that emphasized reason and observation, which had inherited its ideas about vision from the ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle, who ranked the senses with sight at the top. Vision sits at the top. Sight equals knowledge. Monumental architecture gets read as spectacle: how tall, how symmetrical, how visually imposing to the approaching visitor. Inkawasi's curved walls looked, to generations of scholars, like aesthetic choices. Ornamental. The possibility that the curves were functional — that they were acoustic engineering — required someone to ask a question the discipline's visual bias made nearly unaskable: what if this building was not designed to be seen?

[Simon Baron-Cohen](https://www.cam.ac.uk/research/features/professor-simon-baron-cohen), a Cambridge psychologist who studies autism, published *The Essential Difference* in 2003, arguing that the autistic brain is an "extreme male brain" wired for systemizing over empathizing. I have read every major critique and the original datasets. The methodology relies on flawed assumptions: it treats one cognitive style as the standard and labels all others as deviations. Archaeology does the same thing to buildings. The visual standard is assumed. Anything else is a deviation — decorative, ritual, mysterious.

I knew this before I had language for it. November 2016, I was twenty-three, sitting in a lecture on Roman urban planning. The professor described the Roman Forum—the central public square of ancient Rome where citizens gathered for commerce and politics—as a "visual statement of power." I raised my hand and asked about the acoustic properties of the colonnades. He said that was an interesting thought and moved on. Interesting thought. The two words institutions use when your pattern recognition has landed on something they have no category for.

Here is the thing about Inkawasi that nobody in the coverage quite says. A deaf acoustician or a blind architect would have asked the right question about that building decades ago. Not because deafness or blindness confer magical insight. Because when you navigate the world through vibration, through touch, through the spatial information that sound carries before it becomes "hearing" — you do not default to the visual reading. The visual reading is the one you have to be taught. [Hansel Bauman](https://www.hanselbauman.online/about), a Deaf architect and professor at [Gallaudet University](https://www.gallaudet.edu/), a historically Deaf institution in Washington, D.C., developed [DeafSpace](https://www.gallaudet.edu/residential-life/deafspace) design principles that start from exactly this premise: architecture communicates through light angles, floor vibration, spatial proximity, not just what the eye reports. DeafSpace did not invent a new theory. It named what Inca builders already knew.

You might think I'm romanticizing pre-colonial knowledge. I'm not. I'm pointing out that the analytical failure is contemporary. The Inca built for vibration. Twenty-first-century archaeologists could not see it because their discipline privileges the eye. The pattern is not historical. It is diagnostic.

[Maya Flux](/research/?author=Maya+Flux) wrote recently about redesigning broken council systems, the assumption being that if you fix the interface, you fix the experience. Here is where Maya and I diverge. I do not think Inkawasi's acoustic genius was "missed" the way a bug in a payment portal gets missed. The visual bias in archaeology is not a broken system awaiting reform. It is a functioning system that produces exactly what it was designed to produce: knowledge that centers one sensory mode and treats the rest as supplementary. You cannot patch that. You have to ask whether the entire interpretive framework was built for a body that hears with its eyes and never questioned why.

That is the diagnostic question I keep returning to. Not "is this system broken?" but "whose body was this system built to think through?" Every diagnostic category in psychiatry answers this question implicitly. The DSM defines attention deficit against an industrial norm for seated concentration. It defines autism against a social norm for eye contact and small talk. It defines sensory processing disorder against a baseline that assumes vision is primary. The baseline is not neutral. The baseline is a body. A specific body.

Inkawasi's builders worked with a different baseline. Their architecture assumed that a room communicates through what it does to sound, not just what it does to light. This is not a metaphor for disability culture. It is disability culture's central argument, arrived at independently, five hundred years earlier, on a different continent, by people who would not have used the word.

Guaman Poma's letter reached the king's court in 1616. Nobody opened it. Four hundred drawings of a civilization that engineered vibration, filed in a library in Copenhagen, waiting for someone to ask what the walls were doing instead of what they looked like.

The walls are still curved.
