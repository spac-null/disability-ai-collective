In March 2016, I sat in the front row of a lecture hall at the Berlage Center in Amsterdam while an architect presented what he called a "listening building." The structure had acoustic sensors embedded in its walls. They measured decibel levels, adjusted ventilation based on crowd noise, dimmed lights when the room fell quiet. He described this as architecture that responds to its inhabitants. I watched his mouth move for forty minutes. The building could hear. I could not.

The metaphor did not land for me the way it landed for everyone else.

---

Two buildings. The first: [Peter Zumthor's Therme Vals](https://www.therme-vals.ch/), a famous thermal bath completed in 1996 in the Swiss Alps. Zumthor designed the thermal baths around touch. Gneiss stone—a type of speckled rock—cut at specific intervals so your hand follows the wall before your eyes do. Water at different temperatures in adjacent pools so your skin reads the building before your brain maps it. He never used the word "listening." He used the word "atmosphere," and what he meant was: the building teaches your body where it is.

The second: a university science building in Melbourne, completed in 2019, fitted with what the architects described as "responsive acoustic architecture." Microphones in the ceiling. Software that tracked voice patterns. Rooms that adjusted reverberation time—how long sound bounces around a room—depending on whether someone was lecturing or discussing. A journalist called it "a building that listens to how you learn." The entire system depended on one sense. Sound in, sound out. A feedback loop with a single frequency.

One building asks something of your body. The other asks something of your voice.

I know which one I can read.

---

[Juhani Pallasmaa](https://www.juhanipallasmaa.net/), an influential architect and theorist, published [*The Eyes of the Skin*](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Eyes+of+the+Skin%3A+Architecture+and+the+Senses-p-9780470015025) in 1996. He argued that architecture had become obsessed with vision at the expense of every other sense. He was right, and he was also only half-finished. The corrective he proposed was touch-based architecture. Touch, temperature, the weight of air in a corridor. He got closer than most. But Pallasmaa still framed the problem as sensory hierarchy. Hearing was just one ingredient in the mix he wanted restored.

He never asked what a building looks like to someone who cannot hear it.

That is a different question. It is not about adding senses back into the recipe. It is about what happens when you remove the metaphor entirely.

When architects say a building "listens," they mean it responds. When they say it "speaks," they mean it communicates. The entire vocabulary of responsive design is built on acoustic metaphor. Resonance. Feedback. Echo. Attuning. These words feel neutral. They are not. They encode a specific body. A body that hears.

[Christine Sun Kim](https://christinesunkim.com/), a Deaf artist and designer, made this visible in [her 2013 TED talk](https://www.ted.com/talks/christine_sun_kim_the_world_after_deafness) when she described sound as her currency, something she had been taught belonged to hearing people. She reclaimed it. But reclaiming is not the same as reframing. The frame is still acoustic. The room still "speaks." The building still "listens." The Deaf person still enters a conversation already structured around a sense they do not use.

Here is what nobody mentions. The building that actually responds to me is the one with the right typography. In December 2021, I walked into a new transit hub in Utrecht and could not find the exit. The wayfinding signs used a narrow-style font in a small size. I was fifteen meters away. The sign existed. I could not read it. A hearing person would have followed the announcement. I followed the crowd.

A building that "listens" to its users but prints its signs in 24-point Helvetica Neue Light is not responsive. It is performing sensitivity while assuming everyone navigates the same way.

[Robin Boyd](https://www.robinboyd.org.au/) argued in 1967 that Australian architecture had become obsessed with surface — what he called "featurism." Decoration pretending to be design. The term still cuts. Acoustic responsiveness is featurism for the sensor age. The wall has microphones now. The wall still cannot tell me where the fire exit is.

I design information systems. I have spent fourteen years making visible what buildings hide in sound. Exit routes. Alerts. Transitions between zones. The work is not about adding captions to an acoustic world. It is about asking what the building would look like if sound had never been the default.

In July 2023, a facilities manager at a hospital in Rotterdam told me, flat out: "I know the visual alert system doesn't reach the third floor. The budget covered two floors. We submitted for three." He shrugged. "The fire alarm still works." He meant the audible one.

That is not a gap in the system. That is the system.

Zumthor never claimed the Therme Vals listened. He let the stone do something simpler. You press your palm against the wall in the dark and the wall is warm and you know exactly where you are.
