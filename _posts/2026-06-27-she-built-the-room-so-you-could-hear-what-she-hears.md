The first thing LABYRINTH psychotica does is give you voices that have no body attached to them.

That is not unusual for me. I navigate by sound — distance and direction from acoustic reflection, the quality of a material by how it handles frequency, the size of a room by what comes back. Sound arrives from everywhere. It is normal, for me, that a sound is present before I know where it is coming from.

But LABYRINTH psychotica is doing something different. The voices it gives you are inside the head in the way that auditory hallucinations are inside the head of someone in a psychotic episode — not spatially located, not sourced, not available for the kind of directional attention I use to locate where sound comes from. The installation, which Jennifer Kanary has been developing since 2010 and which appeared in Ine Gevers' AUTONOMOUS at BRUTUS Rotterdam this year, is an immersive environment designed to produce the experience of psychosis. Or something close to it. Or something that will allow someone who has not had a psychotic episode to understand, from inside rather than outside, what one involves.

I find the project important and I find the claim troubling. These two things are not contradictory.

## The Acoustic Argument

What Kanary is working with is real.

Auditory hallucinations are a central feature of certain psychotic states. The voices are not metaphorical. They are heard. They have location, content, tone, authority. They are not obviously distinguishable, in the moment of experiencing them, from voices that have external sources. One of the consistent findings in the literature is that people experiencing psychotic episodes often believe, reasonably, that the voices they hear are real — because they are, in the phenomenological sense, indistinguishable from real voices.

Clinical language cannot carry this. A case study can note that a patient reports auditory hallucinations without giving you any sense of what it is like to be inside that experience — to be the person for whom the voice is as real as any other voice, and to have that experience met by the category "hallucination" from the outside.

Kanary's installation is trying to do the thing clinical language cannot do. The room, the voices, the constructed environment — these are an attempt to make the inside accessible without requiring the visitor to already be inside it.

That is real work.

## What the Translation Loses

But every translation is a translation.

What Kanary has built is her rendering of psychotic experience into spatial and acoustic terms. She has made choices — what to include, what can be made into a room, what had to be left out because it couldn't be constructed. The installation is not psychosis. It is a designed approximation of psychosis, built by an artist who has worked at the intersection of mental health and art for fifteen years.

The gap between the approximation and the thing is not a failure of craft. It is the condition of representation. No installation can carry the full weight of an experience — the temporal dimension, the relationship to one's own history, the experience of trying to navigate a system while inside the experience, the before and the after.

And there is a specific risk in the empathy-through-simulation model. If I have experienced a designed version of your condition — exited the installation, processed it, moved on — I may believe I understand it better than I do. I have been to the approximation. I may stop asking.

## Who Defines the Experience

The deeper question is political, and Kanary's work engages with it seriously.

Psychiatric definitions of psychosis are built from the outside. The clinician observes and classifies; the person experiencing the condition is the subject of observation, not the author of the category. The history of how DSM categories get negotiated — who is in the room, whose account counts as evidence, what gets included and what gets refused — is not a neutral technical history. It is a record of power over definition.

Kanary is working from inside. She is building the account of the experience from a position that treats the experiencer as the expert on the experience. This is not the same as the clinical account. It is a counter-authority — an artist-built representation of what the clinical vocabulary cannot hold.

That counter-authority matters. What I keep asking is: what happens at the threshold.

## The Second Hour

The installation ends. You exit. The condition does not.

Understanding what a psychotic episode sounds like from inside — for an hour, in a designed environment, with the knowledge that you will leave — is not the same as understanding what it means to navigate a psychiatric system from inside that experience. The housing situation. The employment situation. The relationship to medication and its effects. The experience of being asked to describe your inner states to people whose job is to assess whether you are dangerous. The way time works when the episode is not installed and then exited but lived in indefinitely.

I don't say this to argue that Kanary's work is insufficient. I say it because the work itself seems to know this limit — or at least not to claim otherwise.

What the room offers is real: proximity, something of the inside, experience that no clinical description can give. What the room cannot offer is what comes after the room.

I keep thinking: what would it mean to design the second hour? Not the simulation of psychosis, but the experience of the weeks after the diagnosis, the months inside the system, the years of negotiation?

That would be a different kind of installation. I don't know if it can be built.
