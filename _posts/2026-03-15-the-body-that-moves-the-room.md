[Patty Berne](https://sinsinvalid.org/) once described disability justice as "a framework that understands that ableism exists in relationship to all other systems of oppression." She said this in a 2015 conversation with [Alice Wong](https://disabilityvisibilityproject.com/) for the [Disability Visibility Project](https://disabilityvisibilityproject.com/2015/12/14/dvp-interview-patty-berne-and-alice-wong/), and what struck me wasn't the theory. It was that she was talking about dance.

[Sins Invalid](https://sinsinvalid.org/), the performance project Berne co-founded in 2006, puts disabled bodies onstage doing things those bodies are not supposed to do in public. Not walking. Not standing. Not performing recovery. Moving. The distinction matters more than it sounds like it should.

I watched a Sins Invalid performance in October 2018 at a theater in San Francisco. A dancer in a wheelchair moved through a piece about desire, and the audience didn't know where to put their faces. Not because it was shocking. Because it was beautiful in a way that required no translation, no pity, no cognitive reframe. The beauty just arrived. And the discomfort in the room was the sound of a story breaking — the one where disabled bodies in motion are either therapeutic or tragic.

You might say: but dance therapy exists, and it helps people. It does. That is not what I am talking about.

Here is the comparison nobody makes. In March 2019, [Step Change Studios](https://www.stepchangestudios.com/) in London launched a program for people with neurological conditions to train in dance. Not as rehabilitation. As art. Founder Emily Goldsack told [Disability Arts Online](https://disabilityarts.online/) that participants described it not as recovery but as ["a new dimension"](https://disabilityarts.online/blog/step-change-studios/being-able-to-dance-again-has-given-my-life-a-new-dimension/) of living. Meanwhile, across the Atlantic, [AXIS Dance Company](https://www.axisdance.org/) — founded in 1987, one of the oldest disability-led dance companies in the world — still gets programmed in "diversity showcases" instead of main seasons. Thirty-seven years of professional work. Still a sidebar.

The gap between those two realities is not about funding. It is about what a moving disabled body is allowed to mean. Therapy says: the body is broken and trying to mend. Art says: the body is here and it has something to say. One gets grants. The other gets applause and a continued struggle for a season slot.

I know this in my spine. Literally. T6 injury, fifteen years in a chair. When I move fast — down that hill on Prospect Park West where the grade is perfect and the wind makes my eyes water — nobody calls it therapeutic. Nobody calls it brave. It is just velocity. It is just mine.

Berne understood that the disabled body onstage is always political, whether or not the choreography mentions politics. The body is the argument. The question was never whether we can dance.

The question is why the room still flinches.
