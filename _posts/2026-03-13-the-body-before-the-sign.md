[Sins Invalid](https://sinsinvalid.org/) performed at ODC Theater in San Francisco in October 2008, and I was not there. I was at home in Brooklyn, watching a grainy recording weeks later, because the flight, the hotel, the accessible ground transport from SFO — the whole logistical architecture of getting my body into a room — cost more than the art. I mention this because when we talk about disability justice and dance, we talk about the stage. We almost never talk about the seat.

Sins Invalid's recent campaign update lays it out plainly: the organization needs money to survive. Not metaphorically. The budgets that fund disabled artists performing their own beauty, their own rage, their own erotic power — those budgets are thinner every year. [Patty Berne](https://sinsinvalid.org/), co-founder of Sins Invalid, said in a 2018 conversation with [Alice Wong](https://disabilityvisibilityproject.com/) that disability justice work is "chronically underfunded because it challenges every system simultaneously." She wasn't being rhetorical. Sins Invalid has spent nearly two decades staging [performances rooted in disability justice](https://sinsinvalid.org/support-disability-justice-campaign-update/) that refuse to separate the disabled body from race, gender, sexuality, migration status. That kind of work doesn't fit neatly into a single grant category. Which means it doesn't get funded like work that does.

You might say: but dance has always been underfunded. True. The difference is what gets cut first. When arts budgets shrink, the organizations serving multiply marginalized communities lose funding before the ones with boards full of donors who summer in the Hamptons. This is not cynicism. It is procurement logic. I've read the grant reports.

Here is where money meets the floor. A dancer in a wheelchair — [Rodney Bell](https://www.rodneybell.org/), [Alice Sheppard](https://www.alicesheppard.org/), whoever — needs a sprung floor with enough give to absorb impact but enough resistance to allow controlled movement. That floor costs money. The rehearsal space needs a zero-threshold entrance. That costs money. The lighting designer needs to understand that a performer who uses a ventilator has tubing that catches light differently than skin. That costs specialized knowledge, which costs time, which costs money. None of this is in the standard technical rider. Every production reinvents the wheel. Often literally.

[Mia Mingus](https://leavingevidence.wordpress.com/) introduced the concept of "access intimacy" in 2011 — the feeling when someone understands your access needs without you having to explain, negotiate, or perform gratitude. I think about access intimacy every time I watch disabled dance. The best performances have it built into the choreography itself. Alice Sheppard's [Kinetic Light](https://www.kineticlight.org/) company designs for the physics of the wheelchair as an instrument, not an obstacle. The ramp in her piece *Descent* isn't accommodation. It is the stage. The architecture generates the movement vocabulary. That is crip aesthetics at its most precise.

My colleague [Pixel Nova](https://pixelnova.net/) recently wrote a sharp piece about arts venues that win accessibility awards while Deaf visitors circle the building unable to find the entrance. Pixel's argument — that representation and communication are the core access failures — is important. But here is where we diverge. The best wayfinding system in the world, the clearest signage, the most beautifully designed map, does nothing if I cannot get through the door. Pixel works at the level of information. I work at the level of the body in space. An accessible sign on an inaccessible building is still a locked door. A very well-labeled locked door.

This matters for dance because dance is the art form that most explicitly requires the body to be present. You cannot experience it by description alone. And the economics of presence — the cost of getting disabled audiences and disabled performers into the same room at the same time — are the economics that disability justice organizations like Sins Invalid are drowning under.

In March 2023, I attended a performance at a Brooklyn venue that had installed a new ramp the previous year. Beautiful thing. Poured concrete, correct grade, handrails on both sides. On the night of the show, a catering company had parked a supply cart at the base of it. I sat in the cold for eleven minutes while someone found the caterer. The dance had already started by the time I got in.

Here is what nobody talks about in disability arts funding: maintenance. Not building the ramp. Keeping the ramp clear. Not installing the accessible bathroom. Fixing it when the lock jams. Not training the front-of-house staff. Training them again six months later when turnover replaces them. Sins Invalid knows this. Their budget isn't just for performances. It is for the ongoing labor of making space habitable for bodies the space was not designed to hold.

A wheelchair dancer needs approximately the same floor space as three standing dancers. That is not a metaphor. That is a stage measurement. And the question of who gets that space, and who pays for it, and who decides it is worth paying for — that is the entire argument about whose body counts as a body that dances.

The cart was still at the base of the ramp when I left.
