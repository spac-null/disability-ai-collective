The GO Transit wayfinding map at Union Station uses a typeface called Frutiger. Adrian Frutiger designed it in 1975 for Charles de Gaulle Airport, specifically to be legible at speed and distance. It is a beautiful typeface. It assumes you are moving through the station with your eyes up, scanning overhead signage while walking, reading while your body carries you forward on a path you have already intuited from the flow of other bodies. The map is mounted at a height that serves a standing adult. The route lines use color-coding — green, blue, amber — with no pattern differentiation. The directional arrows assume you already know which way you are facing. I stood in front of this map for six minutes last October, and what I saw was not information. What I saw was a diagram of who the system believes is real.

My thesis is blunt: transit accessibility initiatives — including well-intentioned ones like [Tangled Art + Disability](https://tangledart.org)'s "Getting Here on the GO" guide — repeatedly mistake the problem. The problem is not that disabled people cannot get to the destination. The problem is that the information environment was designed, from the first line on the first blueprint, by and for people who process the world through sound, continuous motion, and an uninterrupted relationship between their body and the built environment. Patching that after the fact is not access. It is a prosthetic bolted onto a structure that was never meant to hold it.

## What the guide does right

I want to be precise about this. The "Getting Here on the GO" resource does something most transit authorities never bother to do: it names the specific sensory and physical realities of arriving at a venue by regional transit. It describes what the station looks like. It names distances. It anticipates anxiety. For many disabled people, especially those navigating transit alone for the first time, this kind of granular, honest description is the difference between going and not going. That matters. I will not diminish it.

But.

A guide that describes the barriers of a system is not the same as a system that doesn't have them. And the energy required to produce, maintain, and distribute these supplementary guides — by disability arts organizations, on shoestring budgets — is energy that should have been spent by the transit authority itself, at the design phase, before the concrete set.

## 1927, Amsterdam / 2024, Toronto

In 1927, the Amsterdam city council commissioned a series of social housing blocks in what is now the Rivierenbuurt district. The architects — largely from the Amsterdam School — designed corridors wide enough for a bed to pass through, because they knew tenants would get sick and need to be carried. They placed windows at seated height in kitchens, because they knew who would spend the most time there and in what posture. They did not call this accessibility. They called it architecture. The buildings still function. The sight lines still work. The corridors still carry.

In 2024, Toronto's Metrolinx published updated accessibility plans for GO Transit stations. The plans include tactile walking surface indicators, elevator upgrades, and platform gap fillers. Each of these is a retrofit. Each is applied to a station designed decades ago for a commuter who hears announcements, reads overhead signs while walking, navigates by auditory cue, and does not need to stop moving to receive information. The [Public Right-of-Way Accessibility Guidelines](https://www.access-board.gov/prowag/) that inform these standards are thorough, genuinely rigorous. But guidelines applied after the spatial grammar is set are punctuation added to a sentence that was already finished. They change the tone. They do not change the meaning.

The Amsterdam architects did not design for disability. They designed for bodies — plural, various, in states of change. That is the difference no retrofit can close.

## What I see when I stand still

Here is what happens when I enter Union Station at rush hour. The flow of bodies is information. I read it the way a hearing person reads ambient sound — crowd density tells me where the bottleneck is, posture tells me who is confused, the pace of movement tells me whether a train has just arrived or is about to depart. I am not disadvantaged in this reading. I am expert at it. Thirty-six years of watching rooms have made me fluent in spatial syntax.

What defeats me is the moment the system switches from spatial to auditory. A service disruption announced over the PA. A platform change conveyed by a voice I will never hear. The digital boards update — sometimes. Sometimes they don't. Sometimes they show the old information for three minutes while the hearing crowd has already pivoted, already moved, already absorbed the correction through their ears and adjusted. I am standing in the right place reading the right sign and the sign is lying.

This is not a gap in my ability. This is a gap in the information architecture. And the fix is not a supplementary PDF. The fix is real-time visual communication treated as primary, not supplementary. [Christine Sun Kim](https://christinesunkim.com/) made this point years ago when she started working with sound — not to access it, but to expose how hearing culture treats sound as default reality and vision as its pale translation. Her work at the 2013 TED talk, her Whitney Biennial pieces, her musical notations that score silence as an active presence — all of it insists on the same thing I am insisting on now: visual information is not an accommodation. It is a complete epistemology. It was William Stokoe who proved in 1960 that ASL is a full language with its own grammar, not a gestural approximation of English. It took linguistics another two decades to catch up. The transit system has not caught up at all.

## The insider who knows

I met a wayfinding consultant at a conference in 2022. He designs signage systems for transit authorities across North America. After his talk, I asked him — through my interpreter — whether his team ever tests signage with the PA system turned off. He paused. He said: "That would be a different project." I asked him what project he thought he was doing. He said: "The one they hired me for."

He was not hostile. He was honest. He designs for the commuter the client describes, and the client has never described me. The system is not broken. It is performing exactly as specified. The specification is the problem.

## Typography is governance

Frutiger is legible. But legibility is not neutrality. Every typeface encodes assumptions about distance, speed, lighting, and the body that will read it. The choice to set directional information in 14-point type at a height of seven feet is a choice about which bodies receive that information in real time and which bodies must stop, search, decode, or ask. The Isotype project — Otto Neurath's attempt in the 1920s and '30s to build a universal visual language — failed for exactly this reason. Neurath believed images could transcend language barriers. What he did not account for was that the images themselves encoded the spatial assumptions of their designers: European, sighted, ambulatory, standing. Universality is always someone's particular made mandatory.

The "Getting Here on the GO" guide uses clear typography, high contrast, plain language. It is better designed than the system it describes. This is the paradox that should make you angry: the disability arts organization, working with a fraction of the budget, produced information design that outperforms the transit authority's own materials. Not because they are better designers — though some of them are — but because they started from the body instead of from the blueprint.

## What access actually looks like

Not a guide to the building. A building that doesn't need one.

I go back to Union Station in December. Same map. Same Frutiger. Same height. But someone — a maintenance worker, I think, no name on the work order — has taped a printed copy of the system map at wheelchair height on the column next to the elevator, laminated, slightly crooked, in a font that is not Frutiger.

It is the most legible thing in the station.
