There are fifteen pilot sites. One of them signed up twenty-eight people in six months. The target was five thousand. That is not a rounding error. That is not a slow start. That is 0.56 percent of the target, which means the scheme failed so completely that someone had to calculate the percentage to four decimal places to make it visible at all.

The scheme is called WorkWell. It launched in September 2024 across England. The idea: disabled people and people with health conditions get early support to stay in work or return to it quickly. The Department for Work and Pensions (which handles employment) and the Department of Health and Social Care (which handles health) work together. The scheme is due to expand this autumn to support around 250,000 people.

The review came out this month. IFF Research, an independent research organization, reviewed progress at twelve of the fifteen local NHS organizations running pilots. These local NHS bodies, called integrated care boards, organize health services across their regions. NHS Lancashire and South Cumbria recruited twenty-eight people. NHS Cornwall and the Isles of Scilly recruited fifty-seven out of 2,067. NHS Surrey Heartlands recruited 171 out of 7,200. The two best-performing sites — NHS Northwest London and NHS North Central London — hit thirty percent of their targets. Thirty percent is the success story.

I have sat in procurement meetings where someone presents a timeline for accessible features and the timeline has a column labelled 'stretch target.' That column is never funded. It sits there in the spreadsheet like a wish someone made once and then forgot. The thing about stretch targets is that they are designed to fail without anyone being responsible for the failure. No one decided not to do it. It just did not happen. The number twenty-eight is what a stretch target looks like when it becomes a recruitment target.

---

WorkWell is logical on paper. It links health services and employment support. It provides early action. The theory is sound: catch someone early so employment gaps don't appear unreliable to employers. The earlier the action, the cheaper the support, the better the result. Prevention is cheaper than cure. That is the pitch.

But the scheme depends on someone showing up. Showing up requires knowing the scheme exists. It requires believing it will help. It requires having the energy to apply while managing the condition that made you eligible in the first place. The review does not say why twenty-eight people signed up instead of five thousand. It does not say whether the other 4,972 people knew the scheme existed. It does not say whether they tried to access it and gave up, whether they were confused by the forms, or whether they could not reach anyone. It just reports the number.

In 2019, I tried to access a council-funded employment scheme for disabled people. The office was on the third floor of a building with a lift that had been out of service for six weeks. The receptionist told me they were waiting on a part. She offered to have someone bring the paperwork down to the ground floor. I filled out the forms on a bench in the lobby. The first question asked whether I had 'drive to work.' I left the form on the bench and went home.

Schemes that link health and employment are designed by people who have never had to choose between attending a medical appointment and keeping a shift. The choice is not theoretical. It is: do I see the consultant who might adjust my medication, or do I keep the job that pays my rent? The scheme that tries to bridge that gap without acknowledging that the gap is built into how work functions — that work is designed for people who do not get sick, or who can afford to get sick without losing income — is not a bridge. It is a plank suspended over a canyon with no railings.

WorkWell assumes that the problem is access to services. But the problem is not access. The problem is that the services themselves are designed for a person who does not exist. A person who can attend appointments during business hours. A person who can take unpaid time off without losing their home. A person whose condition is stable enough that 'early action' means something other than 'we caught you before you got so sick we could not ignore you anymore.'

---

The review does not name the people who did not sign up. It does not follow the person who clicked on the referral link and then closed the tab because the entry rules were hard to parse. It does not track the person who called the helpline and was told to try again in three weeks because the system was down. It does not count the person who signed up and then dropped out because the appointment was scheduled during their only shift that week.

What it does count is money. The government is expanding the scheme this autumn. That means more integrated care boards, more targets, more reviews. The expansion is not tied to whether the pilots hit their targets. The expansion is happening because the scheme exists, and schemes that exist get expanded, and eventually someone will write a report that says the scheme reached 250,000 people, and that number will appear in a policy document, and the policy document will be cited in a debate, and the debate will reference the success of the programme, and no one will ask how many of those 250,000 people stayed in work, or returned to work, or wanted to work in the first place.

I know someone who works in an integrated care board. Not one of the fifteen running WorkWell pilots, but a neighbouring one. She told me last month that her board is preparing for the autumn expansion. They are hiring coordinators. They are drafting referral pathways. They are building a database to track results. She said the database has a field for 'barriers to engagement.' The dropdown menu includes 'lack of drive,' 'chaotic lifestyle,' and 'won't comply.' She said this flatly, the way you say something you have said so many times it has stopped sounding absurd.

The dropdown menu is the scheme. Not the marketing materials. Not the policy document. The dropdown menu is where the theory meets the person, and the person gets sorted into a category that makes sense to the system. 'Lack of drive' is the category for someone who did not sign up because the scheme was hard to access. 'Chaotic lifestyle' is the category for someone whose condition makes it hard to keep appointments. 'Won't comply' is the category for someone who tried the scheme and it did not work.

Twenty-eight people signed up in Lancashire and South Cumbria. I do not know who they are. I do not know whether the scheme helped them. I know that 4,972 people did not sign up, and somewhere in a spreadsheet there is a column explaining why, and the explanations are categories designed to make the failure the responsibility of the person who did not show up, not the system that failed to reach them.
