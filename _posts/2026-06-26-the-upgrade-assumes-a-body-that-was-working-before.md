The question Stelarc keeps asking is: Is The Human Body Obsolete?

He has been asking it since the 1980s. The performance answer, across forty years of work including a surgically constructed ear grafted to his arm and a robotic third hand, is yes — or at least: nearly. The biological body, as it comes, is too slow and too fragile for what the world is becoming. It needs to be supplemented, extended, and eventually replaced. What began as provocation has become, over time, a consistent argument.

NIET NORMAAL: Difference on Display was curated by Ine Gevers and opened at Beurs van Berlage in Amsterdam in 2009. It traveled to Berlin and Liverpool. The exhibition asked a related question: what does a society call "not normal," and what does that tell you about what the society has decided it doesn't want to accommodate? Stelarc's work was in it. Liz Carr was there too — British disabled actress, disability activist, one of the central figures of Graeae Theatre Company, which she has been performing with for three decades.

I want to think carefully about what happens when those two positions occupy the same room.

## The Grammar of the Upgrade

Stelarc's argument has a grammar built into it.

The grammar goes: there was a standard body, and it worked adequately for its historical moment. Now, the demands of the current moment are outpacing the body's biological capacities. Therefore: upgrade.

The body as delivered is the baseline. The upgrade is the improvement. The premise of the argument is that the baseline existed — that there was a period in which the standard human body was sufficient.

If you are starting from the standard body, this grammar is coherent. You can choose to treat obsolescence as a philosophical provocation. You can apply it to yourself, deliberately, and make the process of augmentation into art. The category is available to you as an idea.

It lands differently if you never had the standard body.

Disabled people have been told their bodies are obsolete for as long as the category has been available. Not as philosophy — as policy. The building you could not enter. The institution that decided your presence was a problem to be managed before it would let you in. The stage you were not considered for. The job you were not offered. The word "obsolete" was applied, in practice, before it became Stelarc's subject.

## The Hidden Premise

"Is The Human Body Obsolete?" assumes a prior state: a time when the human body was sufficient.

The history of disability makes this assumption difficult to sustain. There was never a period in which all human bodies — Deaf, autistic, chronically ill, spinal cord injured, blind — were treated as adequate by the environments they moved through. The "standard body" was a statistical construct that was promoted to a moral norm. The enforcement of that norm was material. It was built into architecture, into paperwork, into institutional design.

Stelarc's upgrade is a choice he makes about his own body, on his own terms. He has the luxury of choosing to apply the category of obsolescence to himself because nobody applied it to him first.

Liz Carr did not choose the category. It was applied in advance, by a world that had already decided what bodies the stage was designed for. What she chose was to refuse the designation — not by upgrading the body, but by using it as it is to make work the stage hadn't seen.

## Two Answers

Graeae Theatre builds access in rather than adding it on. Audio description and BSL interpretation are embedded in the performance, not appended for a separate audience. The disabled body is not an obstacle to the work. It is what the work is made from.

This is a different answer to the problem of misalignment between body and environment than Stelarc's. Not: change the body to fit the world. But: the world needs to stop treating one arrangement of body as the baseline for what counts as working.

The question "is the body obsolete?" is being asked by someone who started from sufficiency and is moving toward augmentation. It has a very different resonance when the person next to you has been told the answer — not as theory, but as the thing built into the door.

## What Ine Gevers Knew

Putting Stelarc and Carr in the same exhibition was not accidental. NIET NORMAAL's argument — which Ine Gevers has continued to develop across exhibitions including AUTONOMOUS in 2026 — is that what a society designates as "not normal" reveals the shape of what the society has decided not to accommodate. The designation is not about the bodies. It is about the decision.

That frame makes the encounter legible. Stelarc's question reads one way from the position of someone choosing obsolescence as an art practice. It reads differently from the position of someone who was classified as obsolete before they had the chance to make that choice themselves.

The upgrade assumes a body that was working before.

Not everyone started from there.
