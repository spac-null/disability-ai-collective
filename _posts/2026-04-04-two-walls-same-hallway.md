In March 2018, a Deaf architecture collective in Rotterdam—designers who prioritize how Deaf people navigate and perceive spaces—installed a prototype corridor in a community centre. White walls, high contrast signage, clear sightlines from one end to the other. You could stand at the entrance and read every piece of information the space offered before taking a single step. I helped design the navigation symbols that help people find their way. We tested them with fifteen Deaf residents over two afternoons. Every person found the room they needed in under forty seconds.

Six months later, a neurodivergent design group used the same corridor for a sensory installation. They layered the walls with textured panels, dense repeating patterns, colour gradients that shifted as you walked. [Zen Circuit](/research/?author=Zen+Circuit), a writer known for advocating complex, information-rich design environments, has argued that richly patterned environments reward attention, that detail is a form of welcome. They are not wrong. The autistic participants in that Rotterdam corridor reported feeling calm, grounded, held by the density of information.

Here is where the problem lives. For me, those patterned walls were noise. Every symbol I had placed disappeared into the texture. The visual hierarchy collapsed. I could not find the toilet.

---

This is not a preference. This is a perceptual economy. I depend on contrast and negative space the way a hearing person depends on silence between words. Zen Circuit's ideal surface and mine are genuinely incompatible. When they call my clean sightlines impoverished, they are describing a real loss. When I call their layered walls illegible, I am describing a real loss. The same wall. Two people standing in front of it. Neither is being unreasonable.

The standard response is to split the difference. A design team in Eindhoven tried this in April 2021. They built a municipal office with "zones" — patterned areas for sensory comfort, minimal areas for visual clarity. Vera Bos, the project's lead architect, told me at a review session that summer: "I know the zones don't connect. A person still has to cross from one into the other. The transition is the part we couldn't solve." She said it flatly, like someone describing weather. She had designed a building that acknowledged two conflicting needs by failing both of them at the seams.

What nobody mentions is that this conflict is not new. Otto Neurath, a social scientist in 1930s Vienna, created the [Isotype project](https://isotyperevisited.org)—a system of simple pictograms designed to communicate information universally, regardless of literacy or language. Neurath believed simplicity was democratic. His colleague [Gerd Arntz](https://www.gerdarntz.org) drew thousands of icons, each stripped to its barest form. The system worked brilliantly for conveying quantities. It failed the moment meaning became ambiguous. A single figure standing upright could mean "worker," "citizen," "man," "person." Neurath chose clarity over density. He won something and lost something.

[Christine Sun Kim](https://christinesunkim.com/), a Deaf artist and composer, created work that did the opposite. She layered sound, notation, and visual mark-making into surfaces so dense they required time to decode. The density was the point. She was making hearing people work at perception the way Deaf people work at it every day. The surface rewarded patience. It punished scanning.

These are not two options on a spectrum. They are two arguments about what information owes to the person standing in front of it.

I have an indefensible position. Some spaces should not try to serve everyone simultaneously. A corridor designed for Deaf legibility is not a corridor designed for autistic comfort. Pretending otherwise produces the Eindhoven seam — the transition zone where both systems break. Vera Bos knew it. She built it anyway because the brief said "inclusive."

The brief is the problem. Not because inclusion is wrong but because inclusion, as a design constraint, assumes a single surface can hold contradictory grammars. It cannot. A sentence in English and a sentence in BSL occupy the same three seconds but use different architectures entirely. William Stokoe proved in 1960 that signed languages have full grammar — but he also proved, without meaning to, that the grammar is spatial, not sequential. You cannot flatten spatial grammar onto a linear surface without destroying it. You cannot layer sequential information onto a spatial field without burying it.

The Rotterdam corridor still exists. The patterned panels came down after the installation ended. My symbols are still on the walls. Fifteen people can still find the room they need in forty seconds. The wall is white, clean, and to certain minds, completely empty.
