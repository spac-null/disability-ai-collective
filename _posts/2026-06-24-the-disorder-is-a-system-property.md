AUTONOMOUS, the exhibition that closed last week at Brutus Rotterdam, was organised around three chapters: Cognitive Capitalism, Y'all got ADHD, and Psychedelic Pathways. Curator Ine Gevers' argument, running underneath all three, was that neurodiverse thinking is not a limitation but an essential form of resistance against a society built on efficiency.

I think this is right. I also think it stops one step short.

## What Cognitive Capitalism Actually Does

Cognitive capitalism is not a new way of exploiting bodies. It's a new way of measuring them. Industrial capitalism measured productive capacity in hours and units. Cognitive capitalism measures it in attention, processing speed, focus, output per unit of mental energy. The body still matters — but the primary site of extraction has moved up.

When you reorganise production around cognitive output, you necessarily build a definition of what good cognitive output looks like. That definition is not neutral. It rewards sustained linear attention, rapid task-switching on demand, high tolerance for interruption, capacity to perform enthusiasm. It penalises fixed-focus cognition, sensory processing differences, non-linear pattern recognition, and the kind of thinking that moves slowly and then all at once.

The DSM is downstream of this. The ADHD epidemic is not primarily a story about neurological change across populations — brains did not become different in 1990. It is a story about what happens when you build an economy that requires a specific attention profile and then classify everyone who does not fit as disordered.

## The Resistance Problem

AUTONOMOUS framed neurodiverse thinking as resistance. This is appealing and partially correct. A mind that cannot sustain artificial attention on demand, that keeps finding patterns where it's not supposed to be looking, that moves along its own logic rather than the economy's — yes, that mind is inconvenient to cognitive capitalism. The inconvenience is real.

But resistance by accident is not the same as resistance by design.

What autistic pattern recognition actually does is find the structure underneath things — the logic of systems, the rule that governs apparently random behaviour, the deviation that doesn't fit the model. This is not resistance to the system. It is, depending on what you point it at, one of the most powerful tools available for understanding a system in order to challenge it. Cognitive capitalism would like to own this too. It does own it — in data science, in quantitative finance, in the parts of the tech industry where pattern-detection is the product. The same cognitive style gets classified as a disorder in a classroom and as a competitive advantage in a trading room.

This is not a paradox. It is a property of a system that decides which cognitive profiles are valuable based on where they can be deployed, and classifies the remainder as deficit. The deficit is not in the neurology. It is in the match between what the economy can use and what the person can offer — and the economy gets to name the mismatch.

## What the Exhibition Got Right

Three things.

First: the frame matters. Calling ADHD a response to system design rather than a neurological flaw changes what you do about it. If the problem is a mismatch between a brain and an environment, you can redesign the environment. This sounds obvious. It is almost never what happens.

Second: Warren Neidich's concept of *wet conceptualism* — the argument that art can produce changes in brain structure through neural plasticity — is doing real work here. If attention is plastic, the economy's ownership of attention is not biological destiny. A different set of objects in the world produces a different configuration of mind. Which objects, and who gets to design them, is a political question.

Third: putting ADHD in the same frame as psychedelic experience and cognitive capitalism is an unusual move, and it's correct. They are all stories about the same thing: what states of mind get classified as valuable, dangerous, productive, or disordered, and what that classification does in the world.

## Where It Stops

The exhibition could not quite say the full thing: that the resistance it was celebrating is resistance *only because the system decided it was inconvenient*. Which means the system could, in principle, decide to find it convenient instead. Cognitive capitalism has absorbed queerness, absorbed transgression, absorbed many forms of difference that once functioned as resistance, by finding out how to extract value from them.

Neurodiverse thinking has already been partially absorbed — the parts that produce useful pattern recognition at scale, in contexts the economy controls. What remains outside the system is not the thinking itself. It is the person attached to the thinking, in all the dimensions of their life that are not the thinking.

The disorder is a system property. The system is the thing that needs treating.
