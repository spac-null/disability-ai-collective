The GO Transit map is a masterpiece of omission. I don't mean this as metaphor. I mean that the Greater Toronto and Hamilton Area's regional transit map — the one you encounter on platforms, in stations, embedded in trip planners — performs an extraordinary act of spatial editing, and almost nobody notices because the things it removes were never meant for them either. Tangled Art + Disability's "Getting here on the GO" project cracks this open, and what spills out is not a story about better wayfinding. It is a story about who gets to be a body moving through engineered space, and who gets to be a problem that space must reluctantly accommodate.

**The station as argument**

I want you to picture a GO Transit station before you picture the map. Concrete platform, green signage, digital departure boards cycling through text at a pace calibrated for someone glancing up from a phone. Overhead announcements — I'll get to those. Ticket machines with small screens, high contrast but low flexibility. Now imagine arriving Deaf. The departure board is your primary interface with the entire system, and it is designed as a supplement to audio announcements, not a replacement. When service disruptions happen, the board may or may not update. The critical information — the gate change, the delay cause, the instruction to board a different train — travels through the air and dissolves before it reaches me. I stand on the platform reading the architecture of other people's reactions: who looks annoyed, who starts walking, who checks their phone with a particular urgency. I am wayfinding through the social behavior of strangers, which is a skill I have and should not need.

This is the condition that Tangled Art's access analysis surfaces, and it surfaces it not through complaint but through description. The project documents, with genuine specificity, what it takes for disabled people to get to a cultural venue using public transit. The brilliance is in refusing to separate the art experience from the transit experience, because for disabled people these have never been separate. The journey is the first accessibility test, and most venues fail it before you reach the front door.

**What Neurath knew and what we forgot**

Otto Neurath's isotype project in the 1920s and '30s attempted to build a universal visual language — pictograms that could communicate public health data, economic information, and civic instruction across language barriers. The project failed, productively. It failed because universality is a design fiction: every symbol carries cultural assumptions, every layout encodes a reading order, every pictogram presumes a body that recognizes itself in the simplified figure. But Neurath understood something that contemporary transit design has systematically unlearned: visual information is not a fallback mode. It is a primary cognitive channel. The assumption that visual signage exists to supplement audio announcements — that text on a screen is a courtesy translation of the real information, which is spoken — is not a neutral design choice. It is an epistemological commitment. It says: hearing is knowing. Everything else is accommodation.

Christine Sun Kim has spent years making this hierarchy visible, sometimes literally drawing it. Her work on sound ownership — who gets to claim authority over sonic space, who is told they are "making too much noise" while simultaneously being denied access to information delivered as sound — maps directly onto the transit question. When a GO station plays an announcement that a platform has changed, and the digital signage lags behind by forty-five seconds or never updates at all, the station has made a declaration about which sensory channel carries authority. The screen is the servant of the speaker. I am the person the speaker was never talking to.

**The floor plan of exclusion**

Tangled Art's analysis reveals something architects and transit planners prefer to keep implicit: accessibility is treated as an overlay, not a structure. You design the station, then you add the elevator. You design the information system, then you add the visual alert. You design the fare gate, then you widen one of them. The result is a space where disabled people navigate two architectures simultaneously — the original design, which was not made for us, and the remediation layer, which was made for us but by people who have never moved through space the way we do. The remediation layer is always legible as remediation. The widened fare gate is never in the center. The accessible entrance is around the side. The visual alert is smaller than the speaker it supposedly supplements.

This is what I mean when I say transit maps are masterpieces of omission. The standard GO map shows you lines and stations. It does not show you which stations have elevators that work. It does not show you which stations have visual paging systems. It does not show you where the accessible entrance is relative to the main entrance, or how much additional time that spatial separation costs. The map shows you a network, and the network it shows is the network available to a nondisabled person who hears, walks, and reads English. For everyone else, the real map is unmade. You carry it in your body. You build it from experience, failure, and the particular exhaustion of never quite knowing whether the infrastructure will hold.

William Stokoe proved in 1960 that American Sign Language is a complete, rule-governed language with its own syntax, morphology, and phonology — phonology being a term he had to repurpose, because even linguistics assumed that language lives in sound. Sixty-four years later, transit systems still treat visual communication as a secondary channel. The suppression of sign languages across the nineteenth and twentieth centuries — what Deaf scholars have rightly called epistemicide, the killing of a way of knowing — did not end when oralism lost its institutional grip. It continued in the quieter violence of information design that treats the eye as the ear's understudy. Every time a transit system builds audio-first and visual-second, it repeats the logic of the Milan Congress of 1880, just with better fonts.

**Design that confesses**

What I want from a transit map is not inclusion. Inclusion is a word that lets the existing structure keep its shape while making room at the margins. What I want is a map that confesses what it has left out. I want the blank spaces labeled. I want the stations where the elevator has been broken for six months marked with a different color — not to shame, but to tell the truth about what the network actually is versus what it claims to be. I want the map to be as honest as graffiti, which changes how you read a wall by refusing to pretend the wall was neutral.

Tangled Art's project gestures toward this. By centering the transit journey as part of the access experience, by documenting what it actually takes to get from a home to a gallery in a disabled body, the project produces a counter-map — unofficial, experiential, and more accurate than anything the transit authority has ever published. It is the kind of information architecture that reveals power precisely because it was not designed by the people who hold it.

The most accessible map I have ever used was hand-drawn by a Deaf friend on a napkin in a coffee shop, showing me which entrance to use, where the construction barrier had moved, and where to stand so the driver could see me waving — and it is already out of date.
