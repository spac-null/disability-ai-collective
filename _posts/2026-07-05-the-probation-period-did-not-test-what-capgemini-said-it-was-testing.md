Bahar Khorram joined Capgemini UK in summer 2023 as a pre-sales lead in cloud infrastructure services. She had twenty-five years of experience as a cloud technologist. Shortly after starting, she disclosed her ADHD diagnosis. Her probation ended in dismissal.

The employment tribunal found, in its July 2025 ruling, that Capgemini had failed to make five reasonable adjustments. They are worth reading as a list, because the list is a description of what a functional working environment for an ADHD employee requires: coaching sessions and ADHD training for her manager and colleagues; realistic task-setting to prevent overwhelm; time management training; coping strategy support; and feedback processes adapted to her needs. None were provided. Capgemini ran its standard probation. The standard probation was not designed for her.

ADHD does not impair competence. Twenty-five years of specialist technical work is not compatible with incompetence. What ADHD affects is the set of conditions under which competence can be expressed: the clarity and stability of expectations, the volume of simultaneous demands, the structure of task handover, the quality of feedback loops. A pre-sales role involves tracking multiple concurrent bids, switching rapidly between technical and commercial priorities, managing dependencies across clients and internal teams. This work is compatible with ADHD. Many of the skills it requires — rapid pattern recognition, high-level synthesis, sustained focus under interest — are characteristic of how ADHD brains operate when the environment supports them. The probation period was not that environment.

The tribunal's five adjustments are not a list of accommodations. They are a specification of what the workplace withheld. Capgemini evaluated Khorram's performance without creating the conditions under which her performance could be accurately evaluated. The probation did not test her skills. It tested whether she could pass an assessment designed for a different cognitive architecture, without support.

The ruling established something beyond Khorram's own case. Neurodiversity training for a manager and colleagues is a reasonable adjustment — not a courtesy, not a preference, but a legal obligation. This matters because it removes the employer's defence of ignorance. The duty to adjust is proactive. You do not wait for the employee to fail and then assess whether adjustments would have helped. You assess in advance whether the test is testing the right thing. The tribunal found Capgemini had not done this, and awarded Khorram £10,000 for injury to feelings and approximately £14,200 in lost earnings.

Neurodiversity-related employment tribunal claims nearly doubled between 2020 and 2025 — reaching 517, with ADHD cases at a record 118. The majority do not arise from deliberate hostility. They arise from workplaces that ran the standard process and then recorded the result as a performance failure. [The office environment was not designed for a neurodivergent brain](/2026/03/14/the-open-office-was-designed-to-break-my-brain/). The standard probation was not either. The Khorram ruling does not describe an exception. It describes the rule, made visible.
