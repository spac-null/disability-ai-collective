Consider what happens when an autistic person holds eye contact during a conversation. The contact is not automatic. It is scheduled. A process tracks how long the gaze has held, estimates when it has run too long, breaks it, counts an interval, restores it. This runs alongside parsing the words, monitoring the speaker's face for shifts the person has learned to read through inference rather than reflex, regulating posture, suppressing movements that would draw attention. None of it is free. Each thread consumes capacity that is therefore unavailable to the conversation itself.

The clinical literature has a word for the sum of these processes: camouflaging, sometimes masking. It is most documented in autistic women, who are diagnosed later and less often, and who learn earlier and more thoroughly to produce socially legible behavior. The literature classifies this capability as a social competency. It demonstrates, the framing goes, that autistic people can acquire social behavior when sufficiently motivated. This classification is an error, and the error is specific.

---

A skill, properly defined, is a capacity that becomes cheaper with practice. The motor sequence of riding a bicycle is effortful at first and nearly free later because the nervous system consolidates it below conscious control. Camouflaging does not consolidate. It runs as deliberate social simulation, in working memory, every time, for the duration of every interaction. The autistic adult who has masked for thirty years is not performing a consolidated routine. She is running the same expensive computation she ran at fifteen, with more reference data and no reduction in cost.

This is the part the classification missed. The research community observed the output — fluent, conventional social performance — and recorded it as evidence of ability. It did not measure the input. It did not instrument the cognitive load required to generate the output, because the output was indistinguishable, to an external observer, from the effortless version a non-autistic person produces. Same surface, different cost structure. Measuring only the surface, the literature concluded that the two were the same kind of thing. They are not. One is a reflex. The other is a simulation sustained against a continuous drain.

A system that produces correct output at unsustainable cost is not functioning well. It is failing slowly, and the failure is invisible precisely because the output looks fine.

---

The failure mode has a name in the autistic community before it had one in the journals: burnout. It presents as a loss of function — language, executive control, tolerance for sensory input — following extended periods of sustained masking. It is frequently accompanied by physical illness. In the resource-allocation frame this is not mysterious. A mind running social simulation continuously, on top of all baseline processing, on top of sensory load most environments are not designed to reduce, is operating without margin. Remove the margin from any system and it does not degrade gracefully. It holds, and holds, and then it does not.

There is a second loss, less discussed. Information is destroyed at both ends. The camouflager loses access to her own internal state, because attention that might register fatigue, distress, or sensory overload is committed to maintaining the performance. The people around her lose accurate data about who she is and what she requires, because the performance is designed to conceal exactly that. A successful mask is a successful deletion of the signal that would have produced support, accommodation, or diagnosis. This is why the diagnosis arrives late. The instrument was working correctly; it simply had nothing to detect, because the condition had been engineered out of view by the person carrying it.

So the classification inverts the finding. What the literature recorded as a demonstrated skill is the visible residue of a hidden cost the same literature declined to measure. The output was logged as competence. The input was never instrumented. The failure mode was filed, separately, under a different heading, by a different set of researchers, and the connection between the three — performance, load, collapse — was not drawn, because nobody had built the apparatus to draw it. Camouflaging is not a skill. It is a mind paying, continuously, for the privilege of not being seen, and being told that the payment is an achievement.
