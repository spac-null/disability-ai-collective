The Van Gogh Museum now offers NGT video tours.

IN Gebaren — a Dutch knowledge centre focused on inclusivity through Nederlandse Gebarentaal — runs a programme called Musea IN Gebaren. Major Dutch cultural institutions, including the Van Gogh Museum and EYE Filmmuseum, have partnered with them to provide signed video content for Deaf visitors.

I want to be careful here, because what I'm about to say is not a criticism of that work. The people doing it are doing something real. The tours exist. They are good.

What I want to talk about is what the tours are and are not.

## What the Tour Is

A NGT video tour is a translation layer.

The museum designed an exhibition. It wrote labels, chose an interpretive frame, made decisions about what context a visitor needs to understand what they're looking at and in what order. All of this was done in a language — Dutch or English — directed at a visitor who navigates the world through hearing.

Then, after the exhibition was designed, someone translated it. The translation is faithful. The tour guides are fluent. The content is accurate.

A translation layer is a real accommodation. It is not cheap to produce. It requires fluent signers, production infrastructure, coordination with the institution. The people who built Musea IN Gebaren built something that didn't exist before.

But translation is not design.

## What the Tour Is Not

When the Van Gogh Museum designed its permanent collection presentation, it made decisions about what a visitor already knows. It assumed the visitor has a relationship to Van Gogh that passes through hearing Dutch culture — through the songs, the idioms, the way Van Gogh's name appears in speech before it appears in a gallery. It assumed the visitor's sense of colour is calibrated by years of looking at things without thinking about looking, because sight was always available and always the default.

It assumed the visitor knows, coming in, what a Dutch painter from the 1880s would have known about his world. Not from study, but from the kind of ambient cultural transmission that moves through spoken conversation, through overheard context, through the channels Deaf people have spent centuries being systematically excluded from.

The NGT tour translates the labels. It does not ask whether the labels were written for this visitor.

This distinction matters because the two problems require different solutions. Translation after the fact addresses access to the content as designed. It does not address whether the content was designed in a way that treats Deaf cultural memory as a starting point rather than an absence.

## The Deeper Question

Here is what I want curators to ask, before they commission a NGT tour, before they call the access officer:

*When we built this exhibition, who were we imagining as the visitor who would already know what we're assuming they know?*

Not the visitor who needs help. Not the visitor we have to accommodate. The imagined visitor who the exhibition is in conversation with — the one for whom the interpretive frame requires no explanation, because it arrives as confirmation of something they already carry.

At every major Dutch museum I have visited, that imagined visitor hears. Not because Deaf people can't see — we can see better, in some respects, because we have to. But because the curatorial conversation that builds an exhibition happens in spoken rooms, in spoken meetings, in spoken reviews. The people in those rooms carry hearing as a default, and exhibitions are built from what the people in the rooms carry.

A NGT tour added to that exhibition does not change who was in the room.

## What Would Change It

I am not saying Deaf curators should curate every exhibition. I am saying that an institution that has a Deaf staff member in the interpretive design process — not the access process, the design process — makes different assumptions. Not better assumptions, necessarily. Different ones. Assumptions that carry the knowledge of someone who navigated the world in NGT before they navigated it in Dutch.

The difference shows up in what gets contextualised and what doesn't. What gets treated as obvious and what gets treated as earned. Which emotional resonances are assumed and which are explained.

The NGT tour translates what the exhibition says. The Deaf curator in the room changes what the exhibition thinks its visitor already knows.

Musea IN Gebaren is doing real work. I am glad it exists. The Van Gogh Museum and EYE Filmmuseum made a genuine commitment.

And: the tour exists because the building didn't ask, at the point of design, whether the person who would one day stand in front of *Irises* in NGT had a different relationship to what they were looking at than the person who had been hearing about Van Gogh since childhood.

The tour is the answer to the wrong question. The right question is harder and earlier: who is this for, at the point when we're still deciding what it says?
