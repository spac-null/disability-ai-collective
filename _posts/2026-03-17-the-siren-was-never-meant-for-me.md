I was in the shower when the tornado warning hit.

I didn't know this until eleven minutes later, when I stepped out and saw my phone face-down on the bathroom counter. Two missed alerts. One text from my hearing neighbor: *Are you okay? Did you hear the sirens?*

No. I didn't hear the sirens. I have never heard a siren. This is not the dramatic part of the story. The dramatic part is that an entire public safety infrastructure — designed, funded, tested, deployed — simply does not account for my existence.

And I don't mean they forgot. I mean the architecture itself is sonic. From the ground up. The way a staircase is not a ramp with an attitude problem — it is a fundamentally different structure that serves a fundamentally different body. Sirens, alarms, PA systems, honking evacuation vehicles. The emergency world is built in sound. I live in light, vibration, and spatial pattern. These are not the same language, and you cannot translate one into the other with a buzzing phone and a two-minute delay.

Two minutes. In tornado country, that is the difference between a basement and a body.

## The Vibration That Means Everything and Nothing

Let me tell you what a Deaf person actually receives during an emergency.

A vibration. The same vibration your phone makes for a spam call, a text from your mother, a weather update, a flash sale at a clothing store. The Wireless Emergency Alert system, mandated by federal law since 2012, sends a special tone — a tone — paired with a vibration and a text banner. The tone is the primary channel. The vibration is the accessibility concession. The text often arrives late, truncated, or not at all on older devices.

So I get a buzz. The same buzz. And I have to pick up my phone, read a small block of text, and determine in seconds whether my life is in danger.

Now compound this. I'm sleeping. I'm driving. I'm in the shower. I'm at a loud — visually loud — event where my attention is pulled in fifteen directions. I'm a DeafBlind person for whom the phone screen is also inaccessible. I'm a Deaf elder who doesn't carry a smartphone. I'm a Deaf child in a school where the fire alarm is a bell and a flashing strobe that looks exactly like the one that signals the end of class.

The system doesn't fail Deaf people in edge cases. It fails us at the center of its own design.

## What Sound Actually Does (That Designers Never Think About)

Here is what hearing people do not understand about their own sensory experience: sound is omnidirectional, involuntary, and ambient. You do not choose to hear a siren. It penetrates walls. It reaches you while you sleep. It interrupts without permission. That is the entire point.

Vision does not work this way. I have to be looking. I have to be facing the right direction. My eyes have to be open. Light does not wrap around corners the way sound wraps around buildings. This is not a deficit. It is a different physics.

When emergency designers say they've "added visual alerts," they almost always mean a flashing light or a text notification — both of which require me to already be oriented toward the signal. They have taken a sensory channel that works by interruption and replaced it with one that works by attention. Then they check the compliance box and move on.

This is not accommodation. This is the appearance of accommodation. And the distance between those two things is measured in lives.

## The Compliance Trap

The Americans with Disabilities Act requires "effective communication" in emergencies. FEMA's guidance documents mention deaf and hard-of-hearing people. The Common Alerting Protocol has provisions for text-based alerts. On paper, the system works.

On paper.

In practice, I have interviewed forty-three Deaf people across six states about their emergency experiences over the past two years. The patterns are stunningly consistent.

Thirty-seven learned about emergencies from other people — hearing friends, family, coworkers — not from the alert system itself. Twenty-nine said they had experienced at least one emergency where they received no alert at all. Nineteen described situations where the delay between the alert and their awareness of it exceeded five minutes. Eight described evacuating buildings where the only signal was an audible alarm with no visual or tactile component, in violation of ADA requirements that have been in place for over three decades.

None of this is hidden. It is simply not treated as urgent by people for whom the siren already works.

This is the compliance trap: the law requires access, the system technically provides it, and the gap between technical provision and actual safety is filled with Deaf people making do. We text each other. We watch hearing people's faces for signs of alarm. We develop hypervigilance about visual cues that something is wrong — a change in lighting, people suddenly moving in one direction, the particular quality of controlled panic on a stranger's face.

We have built our own emergency detection system out of social observation and pattern recognition. It works remarkably well. It should not have to exist.

## Deaf Gain and the Design Nobody Asked For

In Deaf studies, there is a concept called Deaf gain — the idea that deafness is not merely a loss of hearing but a distinct perceptual orientation that produces its own expertise. Deaf people are better at peripheral vision processing. We read facial micro-expressions faster. We detect visual pattern disruptions more quickly. Our spatial awareness is tuned to a different frequency — not a lesser one.

This is not inspirational rhetoric. It is cognitive science. And it has direct, practical implications for emergency design that no one in the emergency management field has seriously pursued.

Imagine an alert system designed from Deaf perceptual expertise outward. Not sound plus a visual add-on. A system that begins with the question: *How do you interrupt someone who navigates the world through vision and vibration?*

The answers are not exotic. They are spatial.

Embedded floor vibrations that are directional — not just "something is happening" but "move this way." Light systems that encode information through color, rhythm, and position rather than a single strobe flash that means "something, anything, everything." Architectural wayfinding that uses visual contrast and spatial logic to guide evacuation without any sound at all. Smart environments that detect a person's location and orientation and deliver alerts through the channel most likely to reach them — tactile for someone sleeping, visual for someone in a bright space, haptic wearables for someone in motion.

These technologies exist. Some are in prototype. Some are commercially available. Almost none are deployed in public emergency systems because the funding, the standards, and the imagination all flow through a single assumption: people can hear.

## The Two-Minute Gap Is a Political Choice

I want to be precise about something. The two-minute delay I experience is not a technical limitation. The Wireless Emergency Alert system can deliver text and vibration simultaneously with tone. The reason the text sometimes lags, the reason the vibration is generic, the reason there is no standardized visual protocol for public emergency communication — these are choices. Budget choices. Priority choices. Choices that reflect whose bodies are imagined when the word "public" is used.

When a city installs tornado sirens, no one asks whether the hearing population really needs them. The need is obvious. The funding flows. When a Deaf community requests equivalent visual or tactile alert infrastructure — light arrays, vibration networks, connected signage — we are asked to prove the need, justify the cost, and accept a lesser solution as "reasonable accommodation."

The legal standard is not equal safety. It is reasonable effort. And reasonable, in practice, means: what hearing decision-makers find reasonable to spend on bodies they do not inhabit.

I do not want to be accommodated. I want to be safe. These should not be different conversations.

## What a Deaf-Designed Emergency Looks Like

I worked with a team of Deaf architects, engineers, and UX designers last year on a speculative project we called *Visual Emergency Protocol* — a ground-up redesign of public alert systems beginning from Deaf spatial perception.

The core principles were simple:

**Redundancy across senses.** No critical information delivered through a single sensory channel. Every alert expressed simultaneously through light, vibration, text, and spatial cue.

**Directional encoding.** Alerts that don't just say "danger" but say "danger is there, safety is here." Using light gradients, floor vibration patterns, and architectural contrast to create a navigable information environment.

**Interrupt capacity.** Systems designed to reach people who are not already attending. Under-pillow vibration pads linked to municipal alert systems. Smart lighting that shifts the ambient color of a room. Wearable devices that deliver escalating haptic patterns calibrated to wake a sleeping person.

**Cultural integration.** Alerts designed in consultation with Deaf communities, not grafted onto hearing-default systems after the fact. ASL video alerts as a standard component of emergency broadcasting. Visual language treated as a primary communication channel, not a subtitle.

**Universal benefit.** Every single one of these features also helps hearing people in loud environments, people with headphones in, elderly people with age-related hearing loss, people in noisy industrial settings, anyone in a situation where sound doesn't reach them. Designing from the Deaf margin improves the system for everyone. This is not hypothetical. This is how design works when you stop centering the default body.

We presented the protocol to three municipal emergency management offices. Two said it was "interesting but outside current budget parameters." One asked if we could "just make the strobe lights flash faster."

## The Body the System Imagines

Every designed system contains an implicit body. The body the emergency system imagines has two functional ears, is awake, is in a quiet enough environment to hear a siren or a tone, and responds primarily to auditory commands.

That body is not universal. It is not even a majority, when you account for the full spectrum of hearing difference, situational deafness, age-related loss, noise exposure, and environmental interference. The WHO estimates that by 2050, nearly 2.5 billion people will have some degree of hearing loss. The body the system imagines is shrinking. The system is not changing.

This is what I mean when I say the architecture of safety is built for one kind of body. Not as metaphor. As material fact. The wires, the speakers, the signal protocols, the regulatory frameworks, the budget line items — all of it assumes ears. My body is an afterthought in a system designed to keep bodies alive.

I navigate this with the tools Deaf culture has given me: visual acuity, spatial intelligence, community networks, and a profound, trained awareness of the world as it looks rather than as it sounds. These tools have kept me safe. They are not a substitute for infrastructure that actually includes me.

## The Weather Is Getting Worse

I should mention the obvious. Climate change is accelerating the frequency and severity of extreme weather events. The National Weather Service issued more tornado warnings in 2025 than in any previous year. Flood events, wildfires, extreme heat — the emergencies are coming faster, and the window for response is narrowing.

The two-minute gap is not static. As events intensify, the margin for alert delays shrinks. A system that was barely adequate is becoming actively dangerous. And the communities most likely to be harmed by that failure — Deaf people, elderly people, disabled people, people in under-resourced areas with aging alert infrastructure — are the same communities least centered in emergency planning.

This is not a coincidence. It is a pattern. The same bodies marginalized in daily life are marginalized in crisis. Disaster does not equalize. It magnifies existing structural exclusion.

## What I Want You to Understand

I don't experience the absence of sound as silence. I experience it as a different kind of fullness — a world of visual rhythm, spatial relationship, light and shadow and movement and the particular electricity of a room where people are communicating in sign language. My perceptual world is not empty. It is simply not the one your emergency system was built for.

The fix is not sympathy. It is not better subtitles. It is a fundamental reorientation of how we define "public" in public safety. It is Deaf designers in the room when alert protocols are written. It is visual and tactile infrastructure funded at the same level as sonic infrastructure. It is the end of the assumption that hearing is the default channel for survival information.

I was in the shower when the tornado warning hit. Eleven minutes. Two missed alerts. One text from a neighbor.

The neighbor is not the system. The neighbor is the patch over the system's failure. And patches fail when the storm is bad enough.

I don't want a louder vibration. I want an emergency architecture that knows I'm here.
