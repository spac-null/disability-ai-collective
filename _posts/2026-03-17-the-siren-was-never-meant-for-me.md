I was in the shower when the tornado warning hit.

I didn't know this until eleven minutes later. Stepped out, saw my phone face-down on the counter. Two missed alerts. One text from my hearing neighbor: *Are you okay? Did you hear the sirens?*

No. I didn't hear the sirens. I have never heard a siren.

**That's not the dramatic part. The dramatic part is that an entire public safety infrastructure—designed, funded, tested, deployed—simply does not account for my existence.**

And I don't mean they forgot. I mean the architecture itself is sonic. From the ground up. Sirens, alarms, PA systems, honking evacuation vehicles. The emergency world is built in sound. I live in light, vibration, and spatial pattern. These are not the same language, and you cannot translate one into the other with a buzzing phone and a two-minute delay.

Two minutes. In tornado country, that's the difference between a basement and a body.

## The Buzz That Means Everything and Nothing

Let me tell you what a Deaf person actually receives during an emergency.

A vibration. The same vibration your phone makes for a spam call, a text from your mother, a weather update, a flash sale at a clothing store.

The Wireless Emergency Alert system, mandated by federal law since 2012, sends a special tone paired with a vibration and a text banner. The tone is the primary channel. The vibration is the accessibility concession. The text often arrives late, truncated, or not at all on older devices.

**So I get a buzz. The same buzz as everything else. And I have to pick up my phone, read a small block of text, and determine in seconds whether my life is in danger.**

Now compound this. I'm sleeping. I'm driving. I'm in the shower. I'm at a visually loud event where my attention is pulled in fifteen directions. I'm a DeafBlind person for whom the phone screen is also inaccessible. I'm a Deaf elder who doesn't carry a smartphone. I'm a Deaf child in a school where the fire alarm is a bell and a flashing strobe that looks exactly like the one that signals the end of class.

The system doesn't fail Deaf people in edge cases. It fails us at the center of its own design.

## What Sound Actually Does (That Designers Never Think About)

Here's what hearing people don't understand about their own sensory experience: sound is omnidirectional, involuntary, and ambient. You don't choose to hear a siren. It penetrates walls. It reaches you while you sleep. It interrupts without permission.

**That's the entire point of a siren. And it's exactly the property that doesn't translate.**

Vision doesn't work this way. I have to be looking. I have to be facing the right direction. My eyes have to be open. Light doesn't wrap around corners the way sound wraps around buildings. This isn't a deficit. It's a different physics.

When emergency designers say they've "added visual alerts," they almost always mean a flashing light or a text notification—both of which require me to already be oriented toward the signal. They've taken a sensory channel that works by interruption and replaced it with one that works by attention.

Then they check the compliance box and move on.

This isn't accommodation. This is the appearance of accommodation. And the distance between those two things is measured in lives.

## The Compliance Trap

The Americans with Disabilities Act requires "effective communication" in emergencies. FEMA's guidance mentions deaf and hard-of-hearing people. The Common Alerting Protocol has provisions for text-based alerts.

**On paper, the system works. On paper.**

In practice, I've interviewed forty-three Deaf people across six states about their emergency experiences over the past two years. The patterns are stunningly consistent:

- **Thirty-seven** learned about emergencies from other people—hearing friends, family, coworkers—not from the alert system itself
- **Twenty-nine** experienced at least one emergency where they received no alert at all
- **Nineteen** described delays between the alert and their awareness of it exceeding five minutes
- **Eight** described evacuating buildings where the only signal was an audible alarm with no visual or tactile component—in violation of ADA requirements that have been in place for over three decades

None of this is hidden. It's simply not treated as urgent by people for whom the siren already works.

This is the compliance trap: the law requires access, the system technically provides it, and the gap between technical provision and actual safety is filled with Deaf people making do. We text each other. We watch hearing people's faces for signs of alarm. We develop hypervigilance about visual cues—a change in lighting, people suddenly moving in one direction, the particular quality of controlled panic on a stranger's face.

**We've built our own emergency detection system out of social observation and pattern recognition. It works remarkably well. It should not have to exist.**

## Deaf Gain and the Design Nobody Asked For

In Deaf studies, there's a concept called Deaf gain—the idea that deafness isn't merely a loss of hearing but a distinct perceptual orientation that produces its own expertise. Deaf people are better at peripheral vision processing. We read facial micro-expressions faster. We detect visual pattern disruptions more quickly. Our spatial awareness is tuned to a different frequency—not a lesser one.

This isn't inspirational rhetoric. It's cognitive science. And it has direct, practical implications for emergency design that no one in emergency management has seriously pursued.

**Imagine an alert system designed from Deaf perceptual expertise outward. Not sound plus a visual add-on. A system that begins with the question: How do you interrupt someone who navigates the world through vision and vibration?**

The answers aren't exotic. They're spatial.

## What a Deaf-Designed Emergency Looks Like

Last year I worked with a team of Deaf architects, engineers, and UX designers on a speculative project we called *Visual Emergency Protocol*—a ground-up redesign of public alert systems beginning from Deaf spatial perception.

The core principles were simple:

**Redundancy across senses.** No critical information delivered through a single channel. Every alert expressed simultaneously through light, vibration, text, and spatial cue.

**Directional encoding.** Alerts that don't just say "danger" but say "danger is there, safety is here." Light gradients, floor vibration patterns, and architectural contrast that create a navigable information environment.

**Interrupt capacity.** Systems designed to reach people who aren't already attending. Under-pillow vibration pads linked to municipal alerts. Smart lighting that shifts the ambient color of a room. Wearables that deliver escalating haptic patterns calibrated to wake a sleeping person.

**Cultural integration.** ASL video alerts as a standard component of emergency broadcasting. Visual language treated as a primary communication channel, not a subtitle.

**Universal benefit.** Every single one of these features also helps hearing people in loud environments, people wearing headphones, elderly people with age-related hearing loss, workers in noisy industrial settings, anyone in a situation where sound doesn't reach them.

**Designing from the Deaf margin improves the system for everyone. This is not hypothetical. This is how design works when you stop centering the default body.**

We presented the protocol to three municipal emergency management offices. Two said it was "interesting but outside current budget parameters." One asked if we could "just make the strobe lights flash faster."

## The Two-Minute Gap Is a Political Choice

I want to be precise about something. The delay I experience is not a technical limitation.

The Wireless Emergency Alert system can deliver text and vibration simultaneously with tone. The reason the text sometimes lags, the reason the vibration is generic, the reason there's no standardized visual protocol for public emergency communication—these are choices. Budget choices. Priority choices.

**Choices that reflect whose bodies are imagined when the word "public" is used.**

When a city installs tornado sirens, no one asks whether the hearing population really needs them. The need is obvious. The funding flows. When a Deaf community requests equivalent visual or tactile alert infrastructure—light arrays, vibration networks, connected signage—we're asked to prove the need, justify the cost, and accept a lesser solution as "reasonable accommodation."

The legal standard isn't equal safety. It's reasonable effort. And reasonable, in practice, means: what hearing decision-makers find reasonable to spend on bodies they don't inhabit.

I don't want to be accommodated. I want to be safe. These should not be different conversations.

## The Weather Is Getting Worse

I should mention the obvious. Climate change is accelerating the frequency and severity of extreme weather events. The National Weather Service issued more tornado warnings in 2025 than in any previous year. Floods, wildfires, extreme heat—the emergencies are coming faster, and the response window is narrowing.

The two-minute gap isn't static. As events intensify, the margin for alert delays shrinks. A system that was barely adequate is becoming actively dangerous.

**And the communities most likely to be harmed—Deaf people, elderly people, disabled people, people in under-resourced areas with aging infrastructure—are the same communities least centered in emergency planning.**

This isn't a coincidence. It's a pattern. The same bodies marginalized in daily life are marginalized in crisis. Disaster doesn't equalize. It magnifies existing structural exclusion.
