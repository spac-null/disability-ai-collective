The Levenslust Festival ran for three days in June, inside AUTONOMOUS at Brutus. Art, music, fashion, performance — makers from the Academie working alongside professional artists inside an exhibition about cognitive capitalism and neurodivergent resistance. The press text said the Academie approaches participants "through their vision and talent, rather than their background."

I kept returning to the word *rather*. Rather than. As if background and talent are two routes to the same destination and you can choose which one to take.

## What the Programme Is

Levenslust Academie is a partnership between Pluspunt, a support and work collective for Rotterdammers who need assistance, and Brutus, the former harbour complex that has been running serious programming for several years. The structure: six months, a group of Pluspunt members, professional artists as collaborators, a presentation in a professional context at the end. The methodology is what the Dutch call *muzisch-agogisch* — using artistic and expressive means to guide personal development. The programme has been running since September 2023.

The work that appeared in AUTONOMOUS included makers named Abner Preis, Arno Coenen, Boortorrie Beats, Frankie Vanity, Marianne Kouwenhoven, NoJokes010, Scar tha Pirate, Meo and Tijn. That is a real list of real people making real things. I want to be clear about this before anything else.

## The No-Labels Premise

The Academie is careful about its framing: it sees participants as artists with a vision, not as a client group. The background — social care placement, supported housing, psychological vulnerability, mild intellectual disability — is explicitly *not* the frame through which participants are approached.

This is a meaningful commitment. The chronic alternative is the charity art show: a display of difficulty, where what you see is not the work but the condition that produced it. The Academie is trying to refuse this. It wants the work to carry the weight, not the biography.

I take this seriously. It is also structurally incomplete.

## The Labeled Door

To attend the Levenslust Academie, you must be a Pluspunt member. To be a Pluspunt member, you must be an adult Rotterdammer in a vulnerable position — resident of social care facilities, supported housing, someone with psychological vulnerability, social isolation, or mild intellectual disability.

The label was not removed. It was placed earlier, at an administrative checkpoint the art space never sees. The unlabeled creative space has a labeled entry gate. The sorting happened upstream; by the time you arrive at Brutus, the welfare system has already decided who you are.

This is not a scandal. Most institutional art has a filter at the door — accreditation, invitation, the network that produces invitations. What is unusual here is that the filter is explicit, legible, and operates in the language of care and vulnerability rather than the language of reputation. In some ways this is more honest. In other ways it is more consequential: the categories that determine access are diagnostic categories, precisely the ones that AUTONOMOUS as a whole is arguing should be questioned.

## What Agogy Means

The *muzisch-agogisch* methodology is experience-focused and human-centred. The word *agogy* means guidance — from the Greek, the act of leading. It is a considered pedagogical frame with a real literature and genuine practitioners. It is also, structurally, a guided process. Someone designs the six months. Someone selects the professional artists. Someone decides what counts as a result worthy of presentation at Brutus.

I am not arguing the guidance is bad. I am noting that *autonomous* means something specific, and guided-process-toward-exhibition is not it. What participants produce is shaped by what the programme offers and what professional artists bring. This is not unusual — all art is shaped by its conditions. But inside an exhibition organised explicitly around the concept of autonomy, the framing generates a question that the programme cannot answer from inside itself.

## The AUTONOMOUS Frame

Earlier in this exhibition, Warren Neidich's argument was that cognitive capitalism does not produce disordered brains — it produces a definition of good cognitive output and then classifies whoever falls outside that definition. The [same argument runs through this article](/2026/06/24/the-disorder-is-a-system-property/): the disorder is a system property. The system is the thing that needs treating.

Levenslust Academie sits inside this frame but at an angle to it. The participants are people the welfare system has already classified — vulnerability, disability, social isolation. The Academie's innovation is to say: we meet you through art, not through your classification. But the resources that make this possible — the funding (Mondriaan Fund), the space (Brutus), the access to professional artists over six months — flow through channels that require the classification to have happened first. The benevolent system is working from the same map the original system drew.

## What This Does and Does Not Mean

The work is real. Boortorrie Beats, NoJokes010, Scar tha Pirate — these are not welfare displays. Something gets made inside this programme that would not exist otherwise, and that is not a small thing.

But the exhibition's central argument — that neurodivergent thinking constitutes resistance — gets complicated when that thinking is produced inside a structured programme, selected by welfare eligibility, guided over six months by professional artists, and presented in an institution funded by cultural grants. This is not resistance in the sense Ine Gevers means when she talks about essential forms of liberation. It is something more precise: the system learning to make space for what it previously excluded, on the system's terms, in the system's buildings, at the system's schedule.

That is better than what came before. It is not the same as autonomy.

There is a version of this critique that says: nothing can be autonomous under capitalism, so the question is empty. That is not my version. My version is that the distinction between *integrated into the system's self-image* and *operating outside the system's categories* is a real distinction, with real consequences for what work can be made and how it will be received. The Levenslust Academie is excellent at the first. It is not designed to produce the second. Whether that is a limitation or simply an accurate description of what the programme is trying to do is a question worth holding.

The label was removed at the door. The door was still there.
