The John Hansard Gallery sits inside Southampton University's Highfield Campus. To reach Simeon Barclay's exhibition *Farewell Sweet Innocence*, you navigate a series of gates — literal gates, the kind that swing shut behind you and require a student ID card or a visitor's pass buzzed in by security. The gallery itself is accessible. The campus is not designed for strangers.

This is the spatial version of what Barclay paints: the gap between being nominally welcome and being structurally kept out.

---

I know this gate pattern. Universities call it access control — a system that decides who moves freely and who must ask permission at each step. The wheelchair user knows this rhythm. The building says accessible entrance. The accessible entrance is around the back, through the service corridor, past the bins. You can enter. You just enter differently.

Barclay, a visual artist nominated for the Turner Prize in 2021, paints men in tracksuits standing outside stadiums, outside shopping centres, outside the places that advertised themselves as public. The composition is the same every time: figure, barrier, building. The gap between the three tells you everything. His visual grammar mirrors the spatial grammar I navigate daily.

In 2019, the Arts Council released a report showing that 91% of UK galleries met legal accessibility standards. What the report did not measure: how many of those galleries sit inside gated campuses, behind entry systems, in buildings that require three forms of ID and a phone call to a security desk. Compliance is not access. A wheelchair ramp behind a locked gate is not accessible. It is *technically* accessible. The difference is the same as Barclay's difference: nominally included, structurally excluded.

---

The British planning system treats institutional grounds — universities, hospitals, museums built inside hospital complexes — as semi-public spaces. This classification matters because it legally allows these institutions to restrict who enters and how, even when they serve the public. You can visit. But you visit as a guest, not as someone with a right to be there. The kerb is dropped. The path is smooth. The gate is locked.

Of course the university says it needs security. Of course they cite insurance, safety, controlled environments for research. And they do need some measure of security — universities hold expensive equipment and sensitive data. But security systems are design choices, not natural facts. The gallery is accessible. The campus runs on systems that sort bodies: student/visitor, authorised/guest, those who flow through checkpoints and those who wait. You are included on someone else's terms — and those terms multiply at every checkpoint, creating a maze that some of us solve through phone numbers saved for years, others through avoiding these spaces entirely.

We know the social model's distinction: society disables us through barriers it builds and maintains. What interests me here is how institutions build inclusion as contingency. The painting includes you. The frame does not. The gallery welcomes disabled visitors. The campus infrastructure ensures that welcome remains conditional, mediated, always through proper channels. You are included through someone else's infrastructure, contingent on someone else's permission.

I visited the Hansard Gallery once, in 2017, for a different exhibition. I phoned ahead. The gallery staff were helpful. Security buzzed me through. I had to call again when I left — the accessible exit does not open from the inside without a card. The exhibition was good. I stayed for an hour, perhaps slightly less. But here's what stays with me: the security guard who walked me out knew the accessible route better than the gallery's own directions. He'd done this before, clearly. "I'll prop this door," he said, wedging it open with his radio. For ten minutes, that entrance was actually accessible — no card, no call, just an open door. The visit became something else: a glimpse of how the space could work when someone decides to make it work. Then the door closed again, and the system reasserted itself.

Barclay's work is about *drab modern Britain* — the Guardian review uses that phrase. Drab is the right word. Not hostile. Not overtly exclusionary. Just gray, bureaucratic, designed for efficiency over welcome. The modern British public building is accessible in the way a hospital corridor is accessible: technically yes, experientially no. Smooth floors, wide doors, no seating. You can move through it. You cannot linger.

This spatial logic — accessible but not welcoming, included but not centered — shapes how disabled people navigate cultural institutions. Some of us become expert navigators, collecting phone numbers and back routes. Others build alternatives: Tangled Art and Disability in Toronto, where the gallery starts from access rather than adding it later. The friction itself generates new forms — audio description as artistic practice, captioning as poetry, the aesthetics of access as art. Not just getting in the door, but reimagining the door itself. The question is whether institutions learn from these alternatives or continue treating access as an add-on feature.

Henri Lefebvre argued that space is socially produced. Power decides who moves freely and who moves through checkpoints. What interests me is how often those checkpoints are *after* the accessible entrance. The ramp works. The door opens. Then: the lift is broken, the accessible toilet is locked and you need to find a staff member, the route to the accessible seating requires going back outside and re-entering through a different building.

Barclay shows figures who could enter — nothing physically stops them — but who recognise the architecture is not built for them. They stand outside because entering means navigating a system that will make their presence feel conditional. I enter. I enter knowing I will have to ask for help, wait for someone to unlock something, explain why I need the thing that should have been open in the first place.

Southampton's gallery made Barclay a Turner nominee. Southampton's campus made his exhibition harder to reach than a commercial gallery on a public street. Both facts are true. The second fact undermines the first in ways the institution does not name.

I still have the security desk number saved in my phone from 2017.
