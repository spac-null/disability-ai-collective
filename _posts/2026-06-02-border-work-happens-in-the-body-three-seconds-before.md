A British stage manager arrives at a theatre in Brussels in February 2026. She has done this job for eleven years. She has worked this venue before. The paperwork is different now. She fills the form. She answers the question about duration of stay. She ticks the box that says 'work.' The border officer looks at the form. Looks at her. Asks: are you being paid? She says yes. He says: wrong visa. She says: I checked, this is a short-term contract, under ninety days. He says: paid work requires a different permit. She says: the venue sent me the visa letter. He says: the venue does not issue work permits. The queue builds behind her. She can hear it. Not the people — the pressure of bodies waiting. That acoustic build. The officer stamps something. Not her passport. A separate sheet. He slides it across. It says she can enter for tourism. She can stay six days. She cannot work. She says: I have a contract. He says: you can appeal from the UK.

Here is what I want you to notice. She is not being denied entry because of a law. She is being denied because the law now requires a physical object — a permit — that takes six weeks to produce, and she is standing in front of a man who can see that she does not have it. The law has not changed her work. It has changed the sequence of actions her body must perform to be allowed to do the work. She must apply earlier. She must wait longer. She must carry paper that proves something the venue cannot prove on her behalf. The law does not care whether the work gets done. It cares whether the objects are in the correct order.

I know this sequence from my own work in theatre and audio production. I know what it is to arrive at a building and be told: you can enter, but not the way you need to. You can come in through the loading dock. You can sit in the audience, but not backstage. You can work here, but not today. The reason is always procedural — a rule or administrative requirement that must be followed exactly, even when the situation does not fit neatly into it. The procedure is always material. Someone did not install the ramp. Someone did not file the form. Someone did not check the box that makes you legible to the system that controls the door.

Here is what changed. Before 2021, when the UK left the European Union, a British actor could work in Prague for two months without a permit. A sound designer could work in Berlin for three weeks. A stage manager could work in Lyon for ten days. The paperwork was nil. The border was there, but it was thin. You crossed it without stopping. Now the border is thick. It requires many objects and steps. You must have a visa. You must apply for a work permit if you will be paid. You may need two permits if your work involves both payment and performance. You must provide a letter from your employer. You must show proof of funds. You must book a return flight in advance. You must have health insurance. You must provide proof that you will leave the country. The permit costs money. The application takes weeks. The approval is not guaranteed.

The venue does not always know this. A casting director in Madrid sends a British actor a contract in March 2026. The shoot is in May. She applies for the work permit in April. It does not arrive. The casting director emails in late April: can you start? She says: I do not have the permit yet. The director says: we thought you had it. The actor says: it takes eight weeks. The director says: we are shooting in three. The director hires someone else. The actor gets the permit in June.

What has happened here is not exclusion. It is friction — the law has added barriers and delays rather than outright bans. The law has not said British actors cannot work in the EU. It has said: the amount of time, money, and administrative capacity required to hire a British actor is now greater than the amount required to hire someone who does not need the permit. Casting directors do not sit in a room and say: we will not hire British actors. They sit in a room and say: we are shooting in eight weeks, we need someone confirmed in two, that rules out anyone who requires eight weeks of paperwork.

There is a radio drama I worked on in 2019 — all-audio, no studio, recorded remotely. I was in Leeds. The director was in Amsterdam. The sound designer was in Kraków. The composer was in Lisbon. We sent files back and forth. We met twice: once for the live recording session, once for the premiere. The premiere was in Berlin. I flew in the morning of the event. I left the next day. No one asked me for a work permit. No one asked me for proof of funds. I had a British passport and an email from the venue. That was enough.

Try to make that production now. The director is still in Amsterdam. I am still in Leeds. The contract is the same. The law is different. I need a permit to attend the premiere if I am being paid for the attendance. I need a different permit to attend the premiere if I am there as crew. I need no permit if I am there as audience, but then I cannot be credited as part of the production team. The law has not banned cross-border radio drama. It has made cross-border radio drama cost more in time and certainty than the fee covers.

This is how barriers work when they are not designed as barriers. The law does not say you cannot cross. It says: crossing now requires a sequence of actions, and if any action in the sequence fails, you cannot cross. The more actions required, the more points of failure. The actor who applies for the permit and does not receive it in time has not been banned. She has been delayed past the point where the delay matters. The outcome is the same. The language is not.

I know what this feels like from the inside. You explain to the person with the checklist that the thing they are asking for does not exist. The venue does not issue work permits. The venue issues contracts. The contract is not a permit. You can see the person understand this. You can hear them understand this. They cannot override it. They do not have the box for it. You are standing in the gap between what the checklist requires and what the situation allows. The gap is six inches wide and you cannot cross it.

The British actor who loses the job to an actor who does not need the permit has not been discriminated against. She has been out-sequenced.
