The vibration comes up through the wall before I'm awake enough to name it. Seven o'clock. I lie in a bed in a building I don't live in — a friend's flat on Calle Tribulete 7, a street in Madrid — and feel the drilling arrive in my teeth, my jaw, the long muscles along my spine that were already braced from yesterday. Outside, the tenants have covered the building in banners and papier-mâché after an investment fund bought the block. A journalist called it the most beautiful act of resistance she had seen. From inside my body it is the sound of a fund emptying a building.

In the same fund's playbook, the drilling starts at seven. Not the ramp. Not the notice. The drilling.

Run those two facts next to each other and something clicks. The banners are the story. The drilling is the eviction.

Landlords in Spain learned a long time ago that you don't need to break the law to empty a building. You make it unlivable and let the tenants remove themselves. Aggressive construction, "accidental" flooding, the jackhammer that runs from seven to seven — these are precision tools. In 2013, tenant unions in Barcelona were already documenting the tactic under a name: *acoso inmobiliario* (property harassment). The council knew. The developers knew. Everyone knew.

Here is what the beautiful-resistance frame misses. Endurance is not distributed evenly. The tenant who can absorb six months of jackhammer and turn it into a mural has a nervous system that lets her. The tenant with a chronic pain condition cannot sleep through the vibration through the wall. She does not get to make art. She gets to leave.

I think about a woman I interviewed in 2019 in a Brooklyn building where the new owner had cut the lift "for renovations" as a way to force out existing tenants. She used a wheelchair. When I met her she was sitting at the base of a dead lift, in the lobby, because the climb to her floor was no longer a climb she could make. She moved out in the fourteenth month and the lift came back the week after. No court would call that eviction. It was just maintenance.

The construction site is the eviction notice for the body that cannot tolerate a construction site.

And so "radical creativity" quietly sorts the tenants. The ones who stay long enough to paint the banners are, by definition, the ones the harassment failed to remove. The frame photographs the survivors and calls their survival a victory. The tenants who left in month three because their kid is autistic and could not stop screaming through the noise are not in the photo. They are the point of the drilling.

Here is where my own argument turns on me. I have written for a decade that the answer is not endurance but planning — the maintenance budget, the enforced law, the fund that gets fined until it stops. I still believe it. But the tenants of Tribulete 7 did everything I recommend. They organised, they unionised, they found a lawyer. The lawyer did nothing. The banners kept the survivors in their homes — and the quiet tenant on the third floor, the one who never came to a meeting, left anyway, in the same weeks the papier-mâché went up. Both things are true at once. The idea of simply letting disabled people exist in their own homes turned out to need papier-mâché, and the papier-mâché saved the ones whose bodies could wait for it to work. I don't know what to call a victory that has that shape.

So the drill runs. Someone paints over the sound. And I keep turning it over: what would a resistance look like that counted her — the tenant already gone by month three, the one no banner reached in time — as the measure of whether we won. I have not found it. I only know the frame I have doesn't have a place to put her.
