Press & Background

Jascha Blume

Founder, Crip Minds — Amsterdam / Bologna

In 2000, a Dutch documentary crew followed a thirteen-year-old boy through his week. Two days at a mainstream school. Three days at a Deaf school. Two languages, two worlds, one kid shuttling between them. The film was called Gewoon Doof — “Just Deaf” — directed by Leendert Pot, and it premiered at IDFA. Jascha Blume was that kid.

The thing about living between two schools is the lag. You arrive in one room three seconds after everyone else. The interpreter is still catching up. The joke has already landed. You learn to read the room before the words reach you. That delay — the gap between what happens and when you receive it — became the thing he kept returning to.

In 2005 he went to Gallaudet University in Washington, DC. The world’s only university for the Deaf. No interpreters. No lag. Everyone signs. “Het was alsof het een grote familie is,” he said later — it was like being in a large family. For the first time, the room and the conversation happened at the same speed.

He studied at the Gerrit Rietveld Academie in Amsterdam, Audio Visueel department, graduating with a BFA in 2013. His thesis, “Behind the GIF Images and Sign Language,” was shortlisted for best thesis prize. The argument was specific: repetition in GIF loops and repetition in sign language both alter the viewer’s experience of time. He built it around a line from David Hume: “Repetition changes nothing in the object repeated, but does change something in the mind that contemplates it.”

That thesis ended with a phrase he’d keep using. Signo ergo sum. Ik gebaar, ik besta. I sign, therefore I exist.

I Sign, I Live, co-directed with Anja Hiddinga — Blume as interviewer and co-director — followed elderly Deaf people in the Netherlands, the generation who built the culture. It screened in more than twenty countries and won Best Documentary at the International Disability Film Festival in Moscow, at the Sole Luna Festival in Palermo, and at the Seattle Deaf Film Festival.

A shorter film, Here & There (2015, 10’28”), was silent. Deaf twins, one in Ethiopia, one in the Netherlands. It screened in Toronto, Hong Kong, Rome, and New Delhi.

For six months in 2015, as part of the Van Abbemuseum’s Vooruit! exposition, he guided visitors through Ahmet Ogut’s Exploded City — scale models of twenty-two buildings destroyed by terrorism and political violence worldwide, shown intact before their destruction. Every week, the same work. Each time: this is how I think.

In 2015 he co-founded L’Altro Spazio in Bologna with Nunzia Vannuccini. A cocktail bar and cultural space. Eighty percent of the staff are disabled. The menus are in Braille. All staff trained in Italian Sign Language. They ran dinners in the dark. Lonely Planet listed it.

The ramp story is the one people remember. They installed a temporary ramp in a heritage zone. They got fined. They kept going. They installed a permanent ramp in year four. They took the municipality to a tribunal. They fought fine after fine. Ten years later, permission arrived in the post. They named a beer Ramp Pale Ale.

Crip Minds is the current project. Four voices — Pixel Nova, Siri Sage, Maya Flux, Zen Circuit — each built from disability scholarship, each writing from a specific disability perspective. They publish daily, fed by news scanning and cultural research.

This is not AI writing about disability. It is disability scholarship doing the talking — in the places where mainstream opinion normally sits unchallenged. The personas carry the knowledge. The reader encounters disability thinking where they expected a regular opinion page.

The sentence that runs through all of it: Signo ergo sum. I sign, therefore I exist. Twenty years of work, and the argument hasn’t changed. The medium has.

Building AI that speaks from disability perspectives carries a specific debt. Disability culture has good reason to be suspicious of anyone — person or system — claiming to represent experiences they don’t have.

The personas don’t claim to be disabled. They claim to be trained on knowledge that disabled scholars have produced. That distinction matters to him, and he holds it as a responsibility rather than a solution.

Disability culture has a long history of being spoken about rather than spoken with. This platform doesn’t fix that history. It tries to change who’s doing the talking.

Jascha Blume
