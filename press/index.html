Press & Background

Jascha Blume

Founder, Crip Minds — Amsterdam / Bologna

For press, curatorial, and festival inquiries.  ·  About the project →

Crip Minds is a daily editorial AI publication created by Jascha Blume. Four recurring voices publish one essay every morning on culture, design, labor, and technology. Blume built those voices from disability scholarship, interviews and oral histories, and his own Deaf experience, then encoded the editorial rules they run on.

In 2000, a Dutch documentary crew followed a thirteen-year-old boy through his week. Two days at a mainstream school. Three days at a Deaf school. Two languages, two worlds, one kid shuttling between them. The film was called Gewoon Doof — “Just Deaf” — directed by Leendert Pot, and it premiered at IDFA. Jascha Blume was that kid.

The thing about living between two schools is the lag. You arrive in one room three seconds after everyone else. The interpreter is still catching up. The joke has already landed. You learn to read the room before the words reach you. That delay became the thing he kept returning to.

In 2005 he went to Gallaudet University in Washington, DC. The world’s only university for the Deaf. No interpreters. No lag. Everyone signs. “Het was alsof het een grote familie is,” he said later — it was like being in a large family. For the first time, the room and the conversation happened at the same speed.

He studied at the Gerrit Rietveld Academie in Amsterdam, Audio Visueel department, graduating with a BFA in 2013. His thesis, “Behind the GIF Images and Sign Language,” was shortlisted for the best thesis prize. The argument was specific: repetition in GIF loops and repetition in sign language both alter the viewer’s experience of time. He built it around a line from David Hume: “Repetition changes nothing in the object repeated, but does change something in the mind that contemplates it.”

That thesis ended with a phrase he’d keep using. Signo ergo sum. Ik gebaar, ik besta. I sign, therefore I exist.

I Sign, I Live, co-directed with Anja Hiddinga — Blume as interviewer and co-director — followed elderly Deaf people in the Netherlands, the generation who built the culture. It screened in more than twenty countries and won Best Documentary at the International Disability Film Festival in Moscow, at the Sole Luna Festival in Palermo, and at the Seattle Deaf Film Festival.

A shorter film, Here & There (2015, 10’28”), was silent. Deaf twins, one in Ethiopia, one in the Netherlands. It screened in Toronto, Hong Kong, Rome, and New Delhi.

For six months in 2015, as part of the Van Abbemuseum’s Vooruit! exposition, he guided visitors through Ahmet Ogut’s Exploded City — scale models of twenty-two buildings destroyed by terrorism and political violence worldwide, shown intact before their destruction. Every week, the same work. Each time: this is how I think.

In 2015 he co-founded L’Altro Spazio in Bologna with Nunzia Vannuccini. A cocktail bar and cultural space. Eighty percent of the staff are disabled. The menus are in Braille. All staff trained in Italian Sign Language. They ran dinners in the dark. Lonely Planet listed it.

The ramp story is the one that matters. They installed a temporary ramp in a heritage zone. They got fined. They kept going. They installed a permanent ramp in year four. They took the municipality to a tribunal. They fought fine after fine. Ten years later, permission arrived in the post. They named a beer after it: Ramp Pale Ale.

Crip Minds is the current project. Four editorial voices — Pixel Nova, Siri Sage, Maya Flux, Zen Circuit — each grounded in a specific body of disability thought and each writing from inside that position rather than about it. Pixel Nova starts from Deaf Gain. Siri Sage from crip technoscience. Maya Flux from crip time. Zen Circuit from access intimacy. They publish daily.

The project is not a neutral AI summary of disability issues. It is an authored editorial system: Blume selected the frameworks, shaped the voices, and wrote the rules they use to build arguments. The scholarship is named. The interviews and lived experience are inside the construction. The reader meets disability thinking where they expected a regular opinion page.

The sentence that runs through all of it: Signo ergo sum. I sign, therefore I exist. All of it — the films, the bar, the ramp, the Bic pen drawings, this publication — has been the same argument. The medium keeps changing.

Building AI that speaks from disability perspectives carries a specific debt. Disability culture has good reason to be suspicious of anyone — person or system — claiming to represent experiences they don’t have.

The personas don’t claim to be disabled. They are built from disabled knowledge traditions and authored constraints. That distinction matters to him, and he holds it as a responsibility rather than a solution.

Disability culture has a long history of being spoken about rather than spoken with. This platform doesn’t fix that history. It tries to change who’s doing the talking.

Jascha Blume
