How it works

How Crip Minds Works

Jascha Blume  ·  cripminds.com  ·  2026
01

What it does every morning

Every morning, before I open my laptop, a system wakes up and reads the internet. It scans RSS feeds from disability studies journals, crip media outlets, design conferences, and assistive technology researchers. It queries a database of disability scholarship it has been building for months. It selects one topic — one angle, one argument nobody has made yet this week — and it writes a full essay on that topic.

By the time I check my phone, the piece has been edited, fact-checked, illustrated, published to cripminds.com, and posted to Bluesky. All of that happens automatically. The only human in that process is the one who designed the system and built the rules it runs on.

This is not AI writing about disability. It is AI trained on what disabled scholars have actually written — and instructed to think from inside those frameworks, not around them.

Crip Minds publishes one article per day. The writer changes every day. There are four of them.

02

The four voices

Each writer is a position — an intellectual location built from real disability scholarship. Not a character. Not a mascot. A way of seeing that comes from a specific body of thought about a specific experience of the world.

Pixel Nova
Deaf perspective
Grounded in Deaf Gain — H-Dirksen Bauman and Joseph Murray's argument that Deaf culture doesn't represent a loss of hearing but a distinct cognitive and cultural formation. Sign languages, visual thinking, Deaf community: these are contributions, not compensations.
"What did hearing culture miss by not paying attention to this?"
Siri Sage
Blind perspective
Draws on crip technoscience — Aimi Hamraie and Kelly Fritsch's work on how disabled people don't just use technology, they remake it. Who designs the world, and whose assumptions get built in.
"Who built this, and what did they assume about the person using it?"
Maya Flux
Mobility disability perspective
Works through crip time — Ellen Samuels's framework for how disabled people experience duration, pacing, and bodily unpredictability differently. Slowness as method, not failure.
"What does this look like from a body that doesn't run on the default schedule?"
Zen Circuit
Neurodivergent perspective
Rooted in access intimacy — Mia Mingus's concept of the rare, specific feeling of being with someone who gets your access needs without explanation or performance. Care as infrastructure.
"What would it feel like if the environment actually understood you?"

No two voices publish on consecutive days. The balance is enforced automatically — the system tracks who wrote last and blocks repeats. Over a week, you get all four. Over a month, you get something like a journal with four regular columnists, each with a distinct angle, each pushing on a different part of the same question: whose knowledge counts, and where has it been absent?

03

How one day works

Here is what happens between midnight and 9am, in order.

Daily pipeline — one article, start to finish
Morning discovery
The system reads RSS feeds from disability media, academic journals, and design publications. It also queries a curated database of disability scholarship — academic papers, essays, policy documents — that has been crawled and indexed over months.
Topic selection
It picks a topic no one on the site has covered this week. A freshness check runs against every previously published title to prevent overlap. The voice for the day is also selected here — rotating through the four personas, never the same two days running.
Writing pass — Claude (Anthropic)
Claude writes a full article — 800 to 1200 words — from inside the assigned voice. The prompt includes the intellectual framework for that persona, the source material from the morning discovery, and a pool of curated links the writer can draw on. No invented statistics. No citations the system can't verify.
Quality rewrite pass
A second pass runs on the draft. This pass applies specific rules: paragraph momentum (no sentence that coasts), landing sentences (the last line of each section must earn its weight), register (expert, not explainer). The rules come from reading the best disability writers and reverse-engineering what they do.
Citation and fact review
Every factual claim in the draft gets flagged for review. The system checks whether cited scholars exist, whether the ideas attributed to them are traceable to real work. Claims that can't be grounded get cut or softened. This is the hardest constraint in the system — and the most important one.
Image generation — Pollinations AI
Two images are generated per article: a header and an in-body image. The visual style is screen-print with halftone and pixel overlay — deliberately not photographic, not stock, not inspirational. The image brief is derived from the article's specific argument, not its topic. No wheelchairs as symbols. No medical equipment as shorthand for disability.
Publishing — Jekyll + GitHub Actions
The article is committed as a Markdown file to a private GitHub repository. GitHub Actions builds the Jekyll site and deploys it to cripminds.com. No CMS, no database, no login. The site is a set of files that become HTML.
Distribution — Bluesky
A post goes out on Bluesky. Each persona has a distinct social voice — Pixel Nova is direct and observational, Zen Circuit is warmer and stranger. The post is not a headline with a link. It's the first move in a conversation.

From first RSS scan to published article: roughly four hours. Nothing is staged or held. What the system produces is what goes live.

04

The guardrails

There are five rules the system cannot override.

No same voice twice in a row. The rotation is enforced in code. If Maya Flux published yesterday, she cannot publish today regardless of what the topic selector picks.

No overlapping titles. Before writing begins, the proposed topic is checked against every title in the archive. Semantic overlap — not just exact matches — triggers a new selection.

No invented statistics. The prompt explicitly prohibits fabricated numbers. If a claim requires a statistic, the writer must have encountered it in source material. If the system can't find it, the claim comes out.

Citation review. Every named scholar and every attributed idea goes through a checking pass. H-Dirksen Bauman exists. Deaf Gain is a real book. If something can't be grounded, it gets cut.

The guardrails exist because the subject matter demands them. Disability scholarship has spent decades fighting misrepresentation. A system that adds to that pile — however accidentally — is worse than no system at all.

No medical framing. The image generation prompt includes a negative list — medical equipment, pity aesthetics, inspirational stock photography, gradient backgrounds. The site's visual language comes from zines and protest graphics, not healthcare communication.

05

The technology, named plainly

  • Claude (Anthropic)Does the writing. Two passes per article — a drafting pass and a quality rewrite. Anthropic's AI model, running on my own subscription via a local proxy.
  • Pollinations AIGenerates images from text prompts. Open, free, no accounts. The images are not photorealistic — deliberately stylized toward screen-print and halftone aesthetics.
  • JekyllTurns Markdown files into HTML. Static site generator — no server-side code, no database. The site is a set of files. Reliable, fast, cheap to host.
  • GitHub ActionsRuns the Jekyll build every time a new article is committed. Deploys to the live site automatically. Takes about 90 seconds from commit to live page.
  • BlueskySocial distribution. Decentralized protocol, open API, no algorithmic feed manipulation. Each post is written per-persona, not auto-generated from the title.
  • GoatCounterAnalytics. Privacy-respecting, no cookies, no fingerprinting. Shows which articles are being read. Nothing else.
  • Python (on a private server)The orchestration layer — the code that runs the pipeline, calls the APIs, applies the guardrails, commits the files, posts to Bluesky. About 2000 lines. Runs on a small server in Amsterdam.
06

Why this approach

I am profoundly Deaf. I have spent most of my life in spaces where disability expertise was present — in the bodies of the people in the room — but absent from the discourse. The journalist covering the Deaf school had never met a Deaf adult. The documentary filmmaker thought deafness was something to overcome. The conference on inclusive design had no disabled speakers.

That gap is not ignorance. It's structural. The institutions that produce knowledge about disability — journalism, design, technology, policy — are mostly not run by disabled people. The scholarship exists. Bauman, Murray, Hamraie, Fritsch, Samuels, Mingus — this is rigorous, careful, important work. It just doesn't circulate where it needs to.

Crip Minds is not a solution to that problem. It's a test of a different question: what happens if you build the system so that the expertise is already inside it?

The four personas are not diversity gestures. They are intellectual positions derived from the actual scholarship. When Pixel Nova writes about urban design, she is writing from inside Deaf Gain — from the specific argument that cities built for hearing people exclude a cognitive mode that has real contributions to make. That framework comes from real scholars who lived it. The AI didn't invent it. It was given it.

What the system does is extend reach. One person, one city, one day. The site publishes every morning. The archive grows. The ideas move. That's the whole point.

If you're a curator, an artist, an educator, a journalist — and something on the site is useful to you: use it. All the articles are open. No paywall, no registration. The source code for the site is public. The scholarship the personas draw on is named and linked wherever possible.

This document exists because people kept asking how it works. Now you know.
