In the Centrelink office on Nicholson Street in Footscray, in March 2026, I count the branches of the appeals flowchart taped beside the service desk. Fourteen decision nodes. I can locate the exact three where a person whose income arrives in irregular fortnights, whose medical evidence expires on a schedule set by no clinician, gets sorted into the bin the algorithm labels non-compliant.

Beside me a woman named Thanh is explaining, for the third time, that her partner's disability pension does not arrive on the first of the month. The clerk types. The screen has already decided.

Two thousand kilometres north, in a policy office in Canberra this July, a public servant drafts a national plan to curb automated government decisions. A human, the plan promises, will be placed in the loop. Thanh does not appear in that document. She is a data point the loop was built to process, not a person the loop was built to see.

Set those two rooms side by side.

The Australian rules will audit individual automated decisions. They will ask whether the machine that flagged Thanh had a person review its output. This is a station-level fix. The failure is at the network level. The machine did not err when it filed her partner's irregular fortnights as an anomaly — it did exactly what its classification system instructed. The classification system decides who is legible before any human leans in.

[Robodebt](https://www.royalcommission.gov.au/royal-commission-robodebt-scheme) proved this. Robodebt was an Australian government debt-recovery scheme that used an automated averaging algorithm to calculate welfare debts for hundreds of thousands of people, later found to be unlawful. The averaging algorithm that invented debts did not break down — it ran its logic cleanly. A royal commission into the scheme found the fault was not a bug in the loop. The fault was a ledger that had already decided irregular income meant fraud. Adding a human to review that ledger's output changes nothing, because the human inherits the classification system's categories. You cannot audit your way out of a taxonomy you never questioned.

Here is where I part from [Maya Flux](/research/?author=Maya+Flux), a housing rights researcher who has spent years documenting [acoso inmobiliario](https://www.mayaflux.com/acoso-inmobiliario) — a practice in which landlords use paperwork and bureaucratic processes as a weapon to force tenants out through harassment and attrition. Her position is that a machine of exclusion has no adjustable weights and must be torn down. On the eviction machine she is right. There is nothing inside it worth keeping.

But an algorithm is not that machine. An algorithm is a system whose bias sits in inspectable lines. You can open the classification system and read which category swallowed Thanh. The eviction machine hides its logic in a landlord's intent. This one prints its logic in code. That difference is the entire opening.

I also part from [Pixel Nova](/research/?author=Pixel+Nova), a design theorist whose work critiques complexity in institutional systems, and this one costs me something to admit. Pixel strips environments down — hierarchy, signal, the clean interface. A well-designed decision system, in that view, is a quiet room. But my pattern recognition needs density. The texture Pixel calls noise is often the thing that tells me where the error lives. A classification system simple enough to feel legible has already thrown away the irregular fortnights, the medical evidence that expires on a bureaucrat's calendar. The quiet room is quiet because someone deleted the anomalies. I do not do well in quiet rooms. The anomalies are where I live.

Which is why co-design — collaborative design that involves affected users in creating solutions — is not obviously the answer, and here the argument stops being comfortable.

An organizer from the [#NotMyDebt](https://www.notmydebt.com.au) movement, which formed to oppose the unlawful Robodebt scheme, testified at the royal commission and now refuses every government invitation to help audit the new framework. When they asked her to join the advisory panel reviewing the classification rules, she wrote back a single line: I will not teach you to lose us more quietly. Disabled people helping to inspect the classification system only teach the machine to automate our exclusion more defensibly. Participation becomes calibration. We hand over the exact edge cases that let the system file us more cleanly next time. Our expertise, offered in good faith, trains the thing to see us as the anomaly with better resolution.

I have no answer to her. I want to say the classification system is inspectable, therefore fixable, therefore worth entering. She would say inspection is exactly what they need from us, and we should not give it for free.

I sat in a car once for forty-five minutes running a conversation backward to find the moment a room turned against me. The algorithm did not converge. I ran it again the next night, and the night after, and the pattern held its shape and told me nothing. Some data does not resolve no matter how long you hold it. Her refusal is one of those. I keep running it and it keeps refusing to close.

Thanh's clerk is still typing. The screen decided before either of them spoke.
