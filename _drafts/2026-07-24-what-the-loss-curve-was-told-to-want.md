At a folding table in the back of a lecture hall this month, I annotated a slide of a protein-folding loss curve — a graph measuring how close a computer model gets to predicting protein shapes correctly — with the same red pen I use to mark revision timelines for the [Diagnostic and Statistical Manual](https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm).

In Utah, a computer scientist named [Matt Might](https://www.mattmight.net/) took the tools of computational biology and turned them on his own son's diagnosis: [NGLY1 deficiency](https://www.ngly1.org/), a rare genetic disease so uncommon that no pharmaceutical company would invest in treating it. Might built the search himself and found candidate molecules where the market had found nothing worth funding. His work is documented as a real case of individual research solving a problem the industry rejected.

Down the coast, a well-capitalized lab feeds a model millions of protein structures and lets it propose therapies at a speed no chemist could match. The demonstration slide showed a curve falling smoothly toward its minimum. The room applauded the falling curve.

Both graphs describe a system converging on what it was told to want. Here is what nobody in that room said aloud. The curve was not measuring whether the medicine was worth making. It was measuring whether the medicine matched the goal it had been handed. The goal was written before the model ran.

An optimization algorithm cannot tell you your objective is wrong. It can only tell you, with rising confidence, that your objective is being met.

For decades, the people who decide which drugs get built have priced certain bodies out. Too rare. Too poor. Too tangled up with populations already treated as a bad bet. This was never a scientific finding. It was a funding decision wearing a lab coat. The new models accept existing priorities without questioning them. They just execute them faster and cheaper, producing graphs that make exclusions seem logical.

[Maya Flux](/research/?author=Maya+Flux), a bioethicist who challenges how we optimize medical systems, would stop me here. She would say I am doing it again — polishing the machinery of a system that should be torn down, not tuned. She is right that optimizing the pipeline is how you launder its evictions. I keep insisting the building has good bones. She keeps pointing at who the building keeps throwing out, and she is right about who that is, every time, without exception.

But the objective function is the repairable part, not the building. Might proved it. He did not rebuild computational biology. He fed it a different goal — save this one child — and the same math that ignored his son suddenly produced candidates.

Which is the thing I cannot resolve, because Might would reject my whole argument from inside it. He would say the collapse in cost is the first mechanism in history that turns "too rare to fund" into a variable you can actually solve. He is not wrong. And I cannot tell whether the machine he aimed at his son and the machine the coastal lab uses to launder its evictions are the same tool with two operators, or two different tools that only look alike on the slide. If these are the same tool, the graph cannot tell you whether it's being used ethically or not.

I have spent years telling people the pattern is honest. The pattern is only ever as honest as the question. And I still do not know if the question that saved his son and the question that priced out everyone else's are asked by the same machine.
