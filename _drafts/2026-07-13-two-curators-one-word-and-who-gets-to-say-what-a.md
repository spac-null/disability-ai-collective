In July 2026, a co-curator in Manchester announced that his new show of 140 LS Lowry paintings would finally bust the myth that Lowry was "naive and uncultured." A man who painted matchstick figures pouring out of mills, dead against the theory of his own time, will now be defended as sophisticated. The gallery will explain what he was really doing.

In 2004, my friend Marleen stood in front of a Rothko in a museum I will not name and tried to tell a docent, on a notepad, what she saw in the red. The docent kept writing back what Rothko meant. Marleen was not asking.

I have thought about those two rooms for twenty-two years and only this week did they lock together.

Lowry sold newsprint scenes to a country that decided he was a folk primitive — untrained, cute, safe. He trained for years under Adolphe Valette. He knew exactly what he was refusing. The label "naive" was never a description of Lowry. It was a description of what his audience could afford to believe about a man from the industrial north.

Now a second set of experts arrives to correct the first set. Notice what does not change: Lowry still is not the one talking. His seeing is still a question that someone with the right credential answers on his behalf.

This is the move I cannot stand, and I recognise it in my own field instantly. The 1880 Milan Congress banned sign languages from Deaf schools on the authority of hearing educators who had decided that gesture was primitive, that speech was culture. For a century, hearing experts told Deaf people what Deaf perception meant. William Stokoe proved in 1960 that ASL was a full language with its own grammar. And the correction, like the Lowry correction, came from a hearing linguist explaining Deaf sight to a world that had never asked the signers.

Here is where my own argument turns on me. I once wrote that every sign recognition model in production was trained by hearing researchers who never really involved Deaf communities — models good at spotting signing, bad at understanding it. I still believe that. But Stokoe was hearing, and Stokoe was right, and Deaf people I respect will tell you his outsider's eye saw the structure we were too close to name. The correction from outside is not always a theft.

Which leaves me with the July show and no clean verdict. Maybe those 140 paintings finally let Lowry's own decisions do the talking. Maybe the wall text answers a question he was never asking.

Marleen never got her red back. She stopped writing on the notepad, looked at me, and shrugged. She had already seen it. The docent just kept telling her what it meant.
