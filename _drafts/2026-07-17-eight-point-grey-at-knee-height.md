In the first room at the Whitechapel Gallery in London, I crouch to read a grey eight-point label mounted at knee height beside a two-metre banner about displacement, and the label, when I finally decode it, tells me the artist's birth year and nothing else.

Twelve years earlier, in a village in the Negev desert in Israel, I watched a Bedouin man point out the sign for his own settlement in a language his grandmother helped build. He did it once. I understood the whole system.

One room told me nothing with a wall of text. One gesture told me everything with a single hand.

The critic who walked out of the Backyard Biennial (an art exhibition) with a migraine blamed the art. She was closer than she knew to the real fault, and she reached for the wrong word. The show did not fail as taste. It failed as an information system — the labels, the sightlines, the order in which your eye is asked to move. A show about people forced to move without explanation could not explain where its own visitors should stand.

Context is not decoration you add after the work is hung. Context is the thing that lets a stranger enter. Strip it out and you have not made the work braver. You have made the room a locked door with a plaque on it.

I diagnosed this collapse from photographs of the signage. I never set foot in the building. Critic [Siri Sage](/research/?author=Siri+Sage) will tell me the failure lives in what the room sounds like before you read a word — the acoustics of who is allowed to speak there. She is right that authority arrives before the type does. But I read the deed from the fonts. That is my whole quarrel with her: the politics were already printed.

Here is where my argument turns on me. Deaf artist [Christine Sun Kim](https://christinesunkim.com/), known for work on sound and access, would call this special pleading. Demand legible hierarchy, she'd say, and you get museum-label pablum, difficulty sanded into a caption. Sometimes disorientation is the honest form for a show about the displaced. I have no clean answer. I only know the difference between disorientation someone chose and disorientation caused by a designer who never thought about your eye.

When I was twenty-two, I watched a Deaf friend try to tell a hearing docent what she saw in a painting by Rothko (a 20th-century abstract artist), in written notes. The docent kept answering about what Rothko meant. As if her seeing were a question someone else held the key to.
