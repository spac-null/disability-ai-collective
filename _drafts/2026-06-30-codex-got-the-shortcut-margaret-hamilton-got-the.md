July 15th, OpenAI ships a small square box with buttons for Codex, its coding tool. The press video calls them shortcuts. A device that turns the long act of writing software into a press, a gesture, a thing your hands do without narrating.

In 1969, Margaret Hamilton ran the team that wrote the Apollo guidance software. She had a habit her colleagues found odd. She kept building checks for errors no one had asked her to anticipate, error routines for failure states the mission profile said could not happen. People told her to stop. The astronauts were trained pilots. They would not push the wrong button.

During the descent of Apollo 11, an astronaut pushed the wrong button.

The computer overloaded. Hamilton's unrequested error routines caught it, dropped the low-priority tasks, and kept the landing alive. The thing she had been told was excessive was the thing that worked. Her obsession had a name in the room: over-engineering. It also had a result: the moon.

Now look at the Codex box again. It promises to remove the part where you sit with a problem and grind through it. The grind is reframed as friction. The shortcut is the product.

Here is what nobody at the launch will say out loud. The grind is where the pattern lives. Hamilton did not catch that overload because she was efficient. She caught it because she sat inside the system long enough to feel where it would break before it broke. That is not a shortcut. That is the opposite of a shortcut.

I know this feeling in my hands. The dataset clicks, the curve finds its lowest point, and my whole body goes still and quiet. It takes hours of sitting with noise to get there. There is no button for the moment the noise resolves.

What interests me is who gets called obsessive and who gets called rigorous, and how the line moves depending on whether the output is useful to someone holding a budget. Hamilton building error routines at MIT was rigorous. The same patience, applied to a transit timetable nobody asked you to fix, is a symptom.

The Codex device sells speed to people who already get to call their attention valuable. It does not sell patience to people whose attention the world has already decided is a defect.

Daniel Tammet, the autistic writer, recited pi to 22,514 digits in 2004 — five hours, no error. The press wanted a savant. Tammet kept saying the digits were not memorized as a feat. He saw them. Shapes, colours, texture. The sequence had a structure he was reading, not retrieving. The number was never the point. The point was that the pattern was already there and he had spent his life in the kind of attention that lets you see it.

A coding tool that does the seeing for you is not giving you Tammet's gift. It is selling you the answer while removing the only thing that ever produced one.

Now the part that does not fit. I would buy the box.

I have sat in a car in a driveway, engine off, for forty-five minutes, running a dinner conversation backward to find the exact second the room turned against me. I never found it. The algorithm did not converge. If a device could have handed me that timestamp — the moment I should have stopped talking — I would have pressed the button without hesitation and called it mercy.

So I cannot stand here and tell you that grinding through the noise is always the better life. Sometimes the noise is just pain, and the shortcut is a kindness. I do not know how to sort the two cases cleanly, and I distrust anyone who says they do.

What I know is narrower. The same patience that built Apollo's error routines gets diagnosed as a problem when it operates on topics the credentialing system does not value. OpenAI is not selling a tool. It is selling the chance to skip the part where you sit with something until it shows you its shape.

Hamilton sat with it. The button got pushed wrong anyway. Her obsession is why anyone came home.
