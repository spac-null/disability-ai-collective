Visual Gallery

Each article gets three pixel art images. Qwen reads the article, picks the scene type, time of day, and accessibility element. Python builds it layer by layer — sky, buildings, people, barriers, solutions. Same palette tint binds all three images in an article together.

8 Scene Types

The generator picks from these eight real-world environments. Each scene renders accessibility barriers or design solutions in context.

Transit Hub

Platform scene — trams, information boards, tactile edge strips, platform gap design.

Urban Street

City street — buildings, road markings, kerb cuts, tree canopy, mobility device users.

Office Interior

Open plan workspace — acoustic panels, desk layout, monitors, window light and shadow.

Theater

Cinema or theater — screen, caption bar, seating rows, aisle lighting, wheelchair positions.

Campus

University plaza — buildings, green space, paths, ramp vs stair entrances at every building.

Library

Reading room — colour-coded shelves, natural light through tall windows, reading tables.

Open Air

Outdoor landscape — hills, trees, sun and clouds. Path width reflects the article's accessibility element.

Data Center

Tech environment — server racks, LED status indicators, data flow lines. Used for digital rights and AI articles.

Accessibility Scenarios

Three early renders showing accessibility barriers and solutions in real-world environments.

Acoustic Office

Workspace with acoustic treatment — sound-absorbing panels, desk spacing, and ceiling design that reduces noise impact for Deaf and autistic workers.

Street Infrastructure

Accessible street — flush kerb cuts, tactile guidance strips, clear sightlines, and continuous footpath without obstruction.

Transit Station

Station with working lift, gap bridging, tactile platform edge, and wayfinding that does not rely exclusively on audio.

How the Generator Works

Each article triggers three images. The pipeline runs at publication time:

  1. Scene direction: Qwen 3.5 reads the article excerpt and picks scene type, time of day, and accessibility element.
  2. Rendering: Python builds the scene layer by layer — sky gradient, background buildings, midground, foreground elements, accessibility indicators.
  3. Artistic DNA: A palette tint derived from the article slug hash is applied to all three images, making them visually cohesive per article.
  4. Variation: Image 1 = primary view. Image 2 = shifted time of day. Image 3 = solution view (forced to accessible state).

Output: 800 × 450px PNG, 5px pixel grid (160 × 90 logical cells).
